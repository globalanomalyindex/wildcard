I work in a corner of metrology most people never think about: gauge-block wringing. When you need a dimensional reference accurate to tens of nanometres, you take precision-ground steel or ceramic blocks and slide them together in a specific way — and they adhere, molecularly, through van der Waals forces and near-perfect fluid exclusion. Stack them and the length is additive to nanometre precision. My obsession is surface condition: a block can have perfect nominal dimensions and still fail to wring flat if there's contamination, a micro-scratch, or an oxidation film you can't see. The geometry is correct on paper; the *material state* of the surface determines whether that geometry is expressed.

Three things from my world look structurally similar to what you're describing.

---

**The contamination-trapped-in-the-wring failure.** In my work, the worst failure isn't a block I know is bad — it's a block that *looks* good, wrings into a reference stack, and carries a hidden offset that propagates through every measurement made downstream with that reference. The defect is set at the moment of intake, when the block came back from field service and wasn't properly recalibrated before being returned to the reference set. Everything built on it inherits the error invisibly.

Your "available" machines that haven't been inspected are structurally this exact thing. The logical state looks certified; every promise made on that basis inherits the hidden offset. The error is set at intake — when the machine came back and sat uninspected — and propagates downstream through every customer commitment. You might find it useful to ask whether the system could represent "returned, uninspected" as a distinct third state, not available, not down — because right now the contaminated blocks are in the same tray as the clean ones, and the only way to know which is which is someone's memory.

---

**You cannot trust the catalogue rating; only current surface-state assessment tells you readiness.** A block is rated 50mm in the spec. If it's been in service, only inspection of its current surface tells me if it's still 50mm. The nominal record and the present condition are two different things, and treating the first as a proxy for the second is where quiet errors live.

Your inconsistent check-in photos are the place where this assessment either happens or doesn't. When a machine comes back muddy or low on fuel, the photo at return is the one moment where nominal spec (the asset record) gets checked against actual present condition. When that's inconsistently done, the assessment doesn't happen, and the asset record becomes the only basis for decisions it was never designed to carry. In my field, the fix isn't a better catalogue — it's a standardized surface-inspection protocol run at every intake, before the block re-enters the certified pool. The exact parallel might be a structured checklist at return — not a photo of the whole machine, but specific condition points, in sequence, so the inspection is reproducible and the result is disputable from evidence rather than from competing recollections.

---

**Certified blocks and uncertified blocks never share a tray.** This is close to a cardinal rule in gauge-block practice. Even if you know in your head which is which, under time pressure — when a job is waiting — the probability of mixing them is too high. The segregation has to be imposed by the system: different physical trays, different shelves, different labels. The sorting decision cannot live in anyone's memory.

Your Saturday 7-to-9-AM surge is the exact condition where "I'll remember which haven't been checked" fails. High volume, one loader, one forklift driver, pressure to move units — that's when a mixed pool with no physical segregation becomes a problem. In my world, the fix is that an uncalibrated block cannot physically enter the certified section: the physical environment enforces the rule, not the person. You might ask whether returns — arriving at the yard — could be routed to a physically distinct zone before inspection clears them, so the separation doesn't depend on anyone tracking it mentally during the surge. The logical-state fix and the physical-routing fix tend to reinforce each other: when the system state matches the physical location, the two checks confirm each other rather than one depending on the other.
