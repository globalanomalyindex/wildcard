I work in repoussé and chasing - raising relief out of sheet silver by driving punches against the metal from back and front. My obsession is the constraints side of the craft: silver is ductile, but its ductility is finite, and the whole practice is governed by knowing where that limit sits. Every blow I strike displaces dislocations in the metal's crystalline lattice. At first the metal moves readily. But each blow raises dislocation density - the metal stiffens, gets brittle. Push past the threshold without annealing, and the sheet splits at its most stressed point. The crack doesn't announce itself: the metal looks fine right up until it tears.

Looking at your problem, I notice three things from inside that constraint.

---

**The economy of blows.** In my craft, a blow that doesn't deform the metal toward the design isn't neutral - it *only* adds hardening. It depletes working capacity without contributing to the relief. Blows cost something whether or not they do useful work. Your 30-weekly alerts with a 10% actionable rate aren't just inefficient; they are actively accumulating hardening in the people who carry the hammer. The structural point isn't "90% of pages are wasted effort" - it's that the 90% non-actionable pages are loading capacity that the 10% critical pages need. The craft has an aesthetic around this: unnecessary blows are a form of damage, not merely a form of waste. You might find it useful to frame the noise-reduction problem that way internally - not as a quality-of-life issue but as a capacity-preservation problem for the hits that actually matter.

---

**Anneal before you need to, not after the metal tells you.** The failure mode I fear most is cracking at the point of highest deformation - and the treacherous part is that the metal gives no visible warning until it's too late. The intervention has to be proactive, calibrated to accumulated-blow accounting, not to observed surface distress. Your system has already cracked in one place (a resignation letter citing on-call load), and you have pre-crack indicators in the others (phone muting). In my craft that is a late-stage signal: the metal has been in an unsafe window for some time. The transfer worth thinking about: what is your "anneal interval" - the threshold at which you reduce load on a detector before departure, not after - and are you tracking accumulated hardening (non-actionable pages per engineer per period) as a leading metric rather than waiting for the visible surface event?

---

**Protect the deep-relief zones, not the whole sheet uniformly.** Here is the constraint move that I think is most directly useful to you. I cannot raise my threshold uniformly because not all areas of the design carry the same deformation risk - the tightest curves and deepest undercuts are where cracks originate. I can't inspect the lattice directly to see where stress is accumulating. So the resolution is not to find a way to see inside the metal before acting; it's to classify sections by structural role - which areas are load-bearing for the design's complexity, and which are background - and then protect the former while giving the latter more tolerance. You have the same situation: you cannot raise thresholds globally because you don't know which alerts guard the regulated payment paths. That uncertainty doesn't need to be resolved everywhere at once. The metalwork move is to work the classification problem in one direction: start by positively identifying the alerts that *are* load-bearing for compliance (the deep-relief zones), annotate those explicitly, and then everything unclassified has a clear default - it can be tested for threshold loosening with low risk to the protected set. The problem isn't "we can't raise thresholds without knowing everything"; it's that you need a smaller, targeted audit: which specific alerts sit on the five-minute payment-failure paths? That list is probably short.
