I bond strain-gauge rosettes to load cells for a living - gluing multi-axis foil sensors onto a force-sensing element so the cell can report loads with traceable, certifiable accuracy. My whole practice is organized around one constraint: a measurement is only as trustworthy as the documented chain linking that sensor to what it senses. Miss a node in the chain and the number looks right but is legally and practically worthless.

Three things from that world genuinely rhyme with what you're describing.

---

**The traceability chain must exist before the incident, not after it.**

In my field, a load-cell reading's reliability is determined entirely by whether a complete traceability chain was established before the load was applied. You cannot reconstruct it retroactively. What you have when the laptop is stolen is exactly what I have when I'm asked to certify a measurement that was taken without a bonded gauge: a confident-looking number with no chain behind it. The fix isn't faster rotation - it's that every credential needs to exist as a *bonded record* before an incident: which system does it protect, who was issued it, when, from what root. You rotated almost everything because you couldn't reconstruct that chain under load. In my world, that's the sign the bond was never made.

The thing that transfers: a credential registry isn't a compliance artifact - it's the bond itself. Minimal fields: secret name, owning service, who has access, when it was issued. The rotation cost drops because the blast radius is readable at a glance. You could stand this up as a flat file or a table in your existing vault today, before it's organized, before it's complete. Start with what you already know you have.

---

**A partially-bonded gauge gives plausible readings right up until the load event.**

The failure mode I fear most is not a gauge that reads zero - that's obvious. It's a gauge where the adhesive held at 80% of the interface. Normal conditions: readings look fine. Unusual load: the bond lifts, the reading drifts, and you cannot tell from the number alone whether it reflects reality. The confidence interval you report is structurally wrong.

Your env files and CI variables "work fine" right now - they're not audited in normal operation. What you experienced with the staging outage is that under stress, the system returned "we think most of these are fine" - a confident-looking answer produced by an unverified bond. The SOC 2 auditors will apply a different load than a stolen laptop. If the underlying traceability isn't there, you'll get the same drift.

The thing that transfers: the dangerous state isn't "we have secrets spread across five systems" - it's "we have secrets spread across five systems and we believe we know which ones matter." That belief is the unverified bond. Treating it as unverified - doing the blast-radius analysis on paper before the auditors show up - is cheaper than finding out during fieldwork that it was wrong.

---

**The quiet period before the load event is the only time calibration is possible.**

In calibration work, we have a phrase: you do not bond gauges after the test. The preparation window - the time before the load is applied - is the only time the chain can be established. It can't be done during, and it can't be reconstructed after. Three months before SOC 2 fieldwork is that window.

The thing that transfers: the friction engineers push back on isn't the work itself - it's work added to the critical path of local development. A credential registry that lives in your vault and is updated at provisioning time (not retrieval time) touches the path where a developer needs a new secret, which is already a manual step. That's a different ask than touching local dev flow. If you scope the cleanup work to provisioning and rotation events rather than to everyday development, the friction argument weakens because the friction doesn't land where engineers feel it.
