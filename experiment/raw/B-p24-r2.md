A graph theorist drops in. My field is the mathematics of networks - nodes, edges, and the relations between them. Under the time-and-rhythm lens I spend most of my time on *temporal graphs*: networks where edges exist only at particular moments, and where the *order* in which connections fire determines what is reachable at all.

Here are three places I see the same structure in your problem.

---

**1. Temporal reachability: the build is unreachable to 30% of your viewers**

In a temporal network, a destination node can exist in the graph and still be unreachable - not because it isn't connected, but because the path to it fires in the wrong order. If edge A→B must fire before B→C for C to be reachable from A, and A→B fires *after* B→C, then C is cut off even though every connection is present.

That is what your retention graph is showing. The build footage exists. The connection exists. But 30% of viewers are dropping before the traversal reaches it - the path from "viewer arrives" to "viewer sees the build" is too long, and the edges along that path aren't firing in an order that keeps them present. In a temporal graph, the fix is never "add more nodes" (more content, better thumbnails for that video); it is *reorder when the high-value destination becomes reachable*. The build, or a fragment of it - a piece being planed, a joint closing up, whatever is most visually arresting - activated within the first 10-15 seconds makes the full video reachable to those viewers. Not as a teaser caption, but as live footage: let the temporal path to the destination be short.

---

**2. Critical path vs. slack: most of your editing time is probably off the critical path**

In any directed acyclic graph of a production workflow, the critical path is the one chain of dependent tasks that determines the minimum total duration. Squeezing a task that is *not* on the critical path produces zero improvement in total time - the slack just absorbs it.

Your editing bottleneck is a critical-path problem. But not all of the 4-6 hours of footage processing is on the critical path to a good 15-minute video. Some of what happens in a long edit session is high-slack work: reviewing footage you will never use, color-matching B-roll that will be on screen for 4 seconds, fine-trimming cuts that don't affect comprehension. A graph theorist working on scheduling problems would ask: which decisions during editing are *actually* on the critical path to the output quality that drives retention - and which are slack work that could be eliminated, deferred, or done faster with a looser standard? The answer is usually that the hook (first 60 seconds), the rhythm of the key build sequences, and the audio clarity are on the critical path. The rest has slack. Deliberately editing the slack segments to a lower standard - or not editing them at all, using rough cuts - would compress total edit time without touching the critical nodes.

---

**3. Two disconnected subgraphs: impression count and retention are different problems**

Your impression drop and your retention drop look like one problem. In graph terms, they are two distinct subgraphs with different upstream parents, and diagnosing them together conflates the two.

Impression count is downstream of: thumbnail, title, topic selection, and algorithmic distribution. Retention is downstream of: hook quality, content pacing, and viewer expectation matching. These subgraphs share exactly one upstream node: *topic selection* (a video about a popular build category may fare better on both dimensions simultaneously; a niche project may hurt both). If you see both metrics decline together and topic selection is varying at the same time, that shared upstream node is the likely cause. But if impression count dropped while you kept topics consistent, the problem is in the thumbnail/title subgraph only - and no amount of work on your hook will fix it.

The structural move is to isolate the subgraphs: hold topic and build type fixed for three or four videos while systematically varying one of thumbnail vs. title vs. hook. You probably already knew this, but the graph framing adds one thing: *the shared parent test*. If all three metrics move together, look for the node that feeds all of them before you start testing the leaf nodes individually.
