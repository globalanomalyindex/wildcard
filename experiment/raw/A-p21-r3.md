I restore deteriorated archival recordings for a living - lacquer discs with groove damage, oxide-shed tape, wire recordings with flutter. My daily problem is the same as yours, viewed through a different medium: the content is all there, encoded in the signal, but it is buried and inaccessible. The "damage" on your 410 episodes isn't oxide shed; it's the absence of a text layer. And just as I never get to re-record a degraded master, you can't re-record four years of interviews. Here are four things from my toolkit that I think genuinely rhyme with your situation.

---

**Triage before restoration.** In archival work, you never apply full treatment to every recording equally. You assess the whole collection for priority first, then work highest-value to lowest, applying only what each asset actually requires. Running full transcription on all 410 episodes in three weeks, solo, in the evenings, is the equivalent of trying to restore an entire archive before the exhibition opens - it collapses under its own scope. But the publisher doesn't need all 410 transcribed; they need quotes from a probably small cohort. The guests emailing you are asking about a different, probably small cohort. Those two lists might overlap significantly. The structural move is: get both lists in hand first, union them, and run your automated transcription on that cohort only. The scope may shrink from "impossible" to "evenings over two weeks." Your call entirely on how to weight the priority, but in my field, triage before treatment is what keeps a deadline alive.

**The sidecar: a separate index layer that never touches the source.** My restorations and my finding aids are strictly separate artifacts - I never annotate by modifying the original file. Every recording gets a sidecar document (condition notes, provenance, timestamp markers) that lives alongside it without altering it. Automated transcription tools work the same way: Whisper, for instance, outputs a timestamped JSON or SRT file next to the source audio - a sidecar. Critically, this means the folder chaos on your two drives doesn't need to be reorganized before you can start. You don't need a clean naming convention to begin generating transcripts; you just run the tool against whatever the file is currently called and the sidecar accrues alongside it. The 250 MB upload cap is also irrelevant here, because local Whisper runs entirely on your machine. You might find it useful to start generating sidecars now, before the folder structure is sorted, rather than waiting until everything is clean.

**Confidence thresholding - don't overcorrect.** The failure I fear most in restoration is overcorrection: a recording that sounds pristine but has had real phoneme content removed along with the noise. The cleaned version *looks* complete but words that were there are now gone, and the listener can't tell. A publisher licensing verbatim quotes from a faulty transcript is the exact structural analog - the output looks like retrieval but the wording is wrong. The transfer from my field: automated transcription tools output per-word confidence scores. Working with a confidence threshold - flagging any word below some cutoff for human verification before delivery - lets you hand the publisher a high-confidence subset of quotes on time, with uncertain regions marked rather than silently wrong. In my world, a restoration log that marks uncertain regions is more valuable than a seamless-sounding result that has hidden the damage. You might find a similar distinction useful: "verified to threshold" and "needs human review" as two delivery tiers, rather than one undifferentiated transcript.

**The finding aid as forward infrastructure.** When I restore a recording, I build the finding aid at the same time - not as extra work, but because every hour spent indexing now makes every future retrieval free. The finding aid converts a sequential-access-only archive into a random-access one, permanently. The three-week deadline is forcing exactly this investment in your case: whatever transcripts you produce to satisfy the publisher become permanent infrastructure. The next guest inquiry costs nothing. The next licensing request costs nothing. The structural parallel holds precisely: a one-time indexing effort that changes the nature of the archive forever, not just for this deadline. I'd note this means the work has two clients simultaneously - the publisher's immediate need and your own ongoing operation - and scoping it to serve both (even slightly) is worth the small extra thought now.
