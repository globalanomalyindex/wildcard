concept=triage

---

I study how emergency and disaster medicine handles mass-casualty events — the formal decision science of sorting patients when arrivals outrun providers. My one obsession: triage is not about the individual patient, it is about throughput through a constraint. The system fails not when resources are thin but when categorization discipline collapses, and worse outcomes accumulate silently behind metrics that still look acceptable.

Your yard has a triage problem. Four connections that I think genuinely map:

---

**1. The intake record is a contract — and you are currently writing contracts in pencil.**

In my field, the triage tag is a time-stamped, standardized record created at the moment of patient arrival. It is not documentation for its own sake: it is the contract between the intake assessor and every downstream provider. If the tag is missing or inconsistently applied, every downstream decision — treatment priority, resource allocation, discharge — inherits the error. The dispute "what state was this patient in when they arrived?" is structurally unresolvable without it.

The same relation holds in your yard. A return that enters without a standardized intake record — a consistent photo set, a timestamped fuel reading, a damage notation in a fixed format — is a patient who arrived without a tag. The counter desk is making promises downstream against a category that was never formally assigned. The customer dispute about whether the machine came back muddy is not a customer-relations problem; it is an intake-record problem. The dispute is unresolvable because the evidence was not collected uniformly at the moment it existed.

In my world, the fix is not "better photos eventually" but a mandatory, identical intake checklist that runs before anything else happens to the asset. What would it look like in your yard to make it impossible for a return to leave the gate without a completed intake record?

---

**2. Under surge conditions, first-come-first-served is the wrong priority rule.**

A conventional ER treats the sickest patient first. A mass-casualty triage protocol inverts that rule: under surge, you treat the most-recoverable fastest, because that maximizes throughput through the constraint. The sickest patient gets tagged "expectant" — not abandoned, but explicitly deferred — so that three moderately injured patients who will recover quickly do not wait behind one complex case that will consume all available bandwidth regardless.

Your Saturday 7–9am window is a surge event on a fixed schedule. The implicit rule your yard is running is first-come-first-served by return order, which means a machine requiring a two-hour repair assessment can hold a mechanic while six quick-turnaround units queue behind it. The surge inversion in my world would look like this: a brief initial sort at intake that separates "cosmetically dirty, mechanically sound" returns from "needs real mechanic time" returns, and routes the quick-turnaround units to the front of the inspection queue during the spike. You recover more rental-ready assets in the two-hour window, and the complex cases get attended to when throughput pressure drops.

You probably already have an intuition about which machines come back essentially fine and which come back genuinely broken. Formalizing that sort as an explicit first step — before the full inspection begins — is what makes the intuition operational.

---

**3. A categorization step at intake is a bottleneck separator, not extra work.**

This is related to the above but structurally distinct. In mass-casualty medicine, the triage officer is not there to treat anyone. Their job is a brief, standardized assessment that assigns a category and moves on — the whole point is that they do not get pulled into treatment. Without that sorting step, the most demanding cases enter the treatment pipeline first and starve flow for everyone else.

Your inspection queue is currently a single undifferentiated channel. Both mechanics receive any return in arrival order and perform the full inspection regardless of what the unit actually needs. A lightweight intake sort — one person, two minutes, a fixed checklist — that routes assets into at least two lanes (quick-release vs. mechanic-hold) would let routine returns flow to available status without competing for mechanic time against complex repairs. The triage officer's role in your yard is not a third mechanic; it is someone who keeps the lanes separated so the mechanics work only what requires them.

---

**4. "Available" is not a triage category — it is the absence of one.**

In mass-casualty work, the most dangerous tag is no tag. An untagged patient is invisible to the system: they cannot be prioritized, tracked, or recovered into the queue when capacity opens. The practical failure is that untagged patients accumulate at the edges and their outcomes fall off without anyone noticing, because nothing in the system is pointed at them.

Your three large excavators that have not turned in weeks are untagged patients. They are not marked "deferred — review at day 14" or "expectant — assess when capacity opens"; they are marked "available," which is a lie the system tells because it has no formal category for "present, uninspected, unrented, and invisibly aging." A named deferral status — visible in the system, with a review trigger that fires after a set interval — would surface these assets before they have sat idle for weeks. The trigger is not the fix; naming the category is. An asset that the system knows is in a formal deferral state can be acted on. An asset that fell off the bottom of an undifferentiated queue cannot.

---

These are four separate levers, and none of them requires the other. The intake record is probably the highest-leverage single move — it closes the damage-dispute loop and gives you the evidentiary foundation the others build on. Happy to pull any of these threads further, or to step back out and leave you to the yard.
