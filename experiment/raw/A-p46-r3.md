I'm a forensic document examiner — specifically, I work on mixed-provenance archives: collections assembled over years from heterogeneous sources (wills, ledgers, personal correspondence, institutional files) that arrive in different hands, on different paper, in different inks, without consistent metadata. My job is to impose reliable addressability on a corpus that was never designed to be queried.

The mechanism I think about daily: every document carries signals about its origin — ink chemistry, paper surface, pen pressure, indentation — that let me answer "is this what it claims to be, and when and where was it made?" without trusting the document's own declared label. The thing I fear: degraded or absent provenance. When condition signals are gone, I cannot recover them. My aesthetic: every assertion about a document must have a citation path back to observable evidence, so a later examiner — or I myself, months later — can reproduce the chain without trusting memory.

Your recipe collection is a mixed-provenance archive. Let me tell you what I notice from that vantage point.

---

**1. Accession records as a first-class field — not an afterthought.**

In my field, a document without a recorded provenance chain is nearly unexaminable. My first move on any new collection is to stamp an accession record: what form did this arrive in, what was its earliest known location, and what is the chain of custody? This isn't the content — it's the envelope of evidence *about* the content. I record it at intake, not later, because "later" means reconstructing it from memory, and memory fails.

What I notice: your retrieval fails because you have to remember not just "what is this recipe" but "where did past-me put it" — you're reconstructing source-channel from memory every time you search. The channel metadata is your provenance chain, and it has never been recorded as a durable field. The move from my side: make source-channel (Instagram save / browser bookmark / screenshot / card / notes app) a mandatory intake field, not something you'd infer later. Even a minimal tagging at capture time — you don't need to fully organize the recipe in that moment — would mean retrieval paths are stored in the corpus instead of in your head.

---

**2. Fragile-originals triage: stabilize before you organize.**

I maintain a condition-risk taxonomy — formats that degrade in ways that destroy exactly the properties I'll later need. Thermal fax paper fades. Photocopies don't preserve ink chemistry. Platform-hosted files can disappear when the platform changes its terms. For high-risk originals I apply a priority-capture rule: extract what you can now, before the signal is gone. The rest of the collection can wait; these cannot. Triage is not the same as organization — it's the step *before* organization, and it has a different urgency curve.

What I notice: your collection has two formats that fall into the high-risk category. Your grandmother's handwritten cards are physical paper — they are exposed to fire, flood, physical loss, and degradation, and you don't have custody of them. Your Instagram saves are platform-dependent — link rot and account deletion are outside your control. Everything else in your collection is lower-risk because it lives in formats you can copy. A document examiner working your archive would stabilize these two first, before touching anything else. Photographing or transcribing the cards into any stable digital format, and saving the Instagram recipes as text rather than as saves, doesn't require a system — it's a targeted triage action with a finite end.

---

**3. Post-cook outcomes as evidence recorded in the corpus, not impressions held in the head.**

My aesthetic discipline is that untethered assertions fail. "This looks like an authentic 1940s document" is useless to me; "the ink spectroscopy places this in the chloro-iridium class introduced after 1932" is citable and reproducible. I annotate *findings* on documents — brief, factual, dated — not just the documents themselves, so a later examiner can work from the record rather than from my memory.

What I notice: "the thing we made three weeks ago that everyone liked" is an episodic memory, not a recorded fact. Episodic memory is exactly the ungrounded assertion I distrust — it will erode as the collection grows and time passes. The structural analog in my field is a findings annotation: one line, attached to the recipe record, that turns a memory into evidence. Not a review — something minimal and dateable: "approved, all five, gluten-free variation worked, 2025-11-17." That is retrievable; a memory is not. The move is treating outcome as a field in the corpus rather than an impression in your head.

---

**4. Normalize into a working corpus — without discarding the originals.**

When I receive a mixed archive I create a working copy set: every document rendered into a uniform, annotatable form I can actually examine. Not to replace the originals — the originals stay, their accession records note their form — but to create a substrate I can query uniformly. I cannot examine a faxed photocopy and a fountain-pen letter and a thermal receipt with the same tool in their original forms; I normalize them first, and then the whole collection is one addressable thing.

What I notice: your collection is exactly a mixed-provenance archive — URLs, image-blobs, off-site physical cards, siloed app entries, platform saves. The reason a week's plan can't tell you what spices you already own is that there is no single corpus to query; there are five separate silos, each with its own retrieval method. The original sources don't have to disappear — the binder lives at your parents' house; the bookmarks stay in your browser — but a normalized working set alongside them is what makes the collection addressable as one thing. The intake form from connection one (source-channel) is what links each working-set entry back to its original. Together they are the examiner's intake operation applied to recipe management.
