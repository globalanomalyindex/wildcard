A wave physicist notices something about your hourly spike: you do not have a load problem, you have a phase problem.

In wave physics, the danger is never oscillators in aggregate — it is oscillators in phase. Two identical waves that would each be harmless produce a crest double the height when they align. Four thousand tenants each running a moderate export is fine. Four hundred of them running at minute zero of every hour is constructive interference in the time domain: every wave crests simultaneously, and the superposition is what kills your p99. The mechanism behind your SLA violation is not volume per se, but synchrony.

Here is what that diagnosis unlocks:

---

**Seed 1: Phase offset is a free degree of freedom**

In wave physics, the cheapest intervention against constructive interference is not amplitude suppression — it is shifting the phase of some oscillators. A wave offset by half a period destructively interferes with its twin. The energy is conserved; the superposition collapses.

The structural match: your scheduled export jobs all share the same period (one hour) and the same phase (minute zero). Phase control in your world is just: don't let all of them fire at the same phase. Assign each enterprise tenant a slot — :00, :05, :10, :15 — or even hash their tenant ID into a jitter offset within the window. No tenant loses their hourly export; no contract is violated. The energy (query load) is identical in total; the temporal superposition is gone. This intervention requires none of the missing metering infrastructure and no budget for isolated clusters — it lives entirely in the scheduler. It may be the cheapest move on your board.

---

**Seed 2: The spike is a high-frequency artifact that can be filtered in time**

A sharp impulse in the time domain is equivalent, in the frequency domain, to energy spread across many frequencies. A low-pass filter smooths the time domain. In signal terms, your top-of-hour burst is a high-frequency event relative to the one-hour fundamental — and temporal spreading is the corresponding filter.

The structural match: if you front the export queue with a dispatch buffer that does not release all accumulated export queries simultaneously but spreads their execution across a window (say, queries enter the queue at :00, but ClickHouse jobs are issued over the following fifteen or thirty minutes), you have implemented a low-pass filter on your query dispatch rate. The total work is unchanged; the spike is traded for a gentler slope. This sits at the execution layer rather than the scheduling layer, so it operates as a second line of defense even where you cannot control when tenants submit their jobs — it decouples "time of submission" from "time of execution."

---

**Seed 3: The broken attribution instrument is a phase-localization problem, not just an accounting problem**

Wave physicists sometimes need to localize where along a medium a disturbance originates — they do this by detecting the *arrival time* and *phase* of the signal at several points, then triangulating. They do not need to tag the source to find it; they infer it from the temporal pattern.

The structural match: your tenant IDs are threaded inconsistently, so you cannot reliably attribute load today. But the synchronized nature of the spike is itself a localization signal. Before the full attribution plumbing is in place, you can narrow "which tenants are causing the spike" to "which tenants have scheduled exports at :00" — that is a much smaller population to inspect in your job scheduler or contract records than the full 4,000. The synchrony of the burst hands you a coarse but immediate way to identify the cohort without the metering infrastructure. You might find the attribution problem is simpler to solve than it appears once you treat temporal pattern as a first-pass filter.
