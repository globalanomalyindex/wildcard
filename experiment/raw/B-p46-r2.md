A geologist whose work is granite failure modes here.

I study how granite -- that archetype of solidity -- actually breaks. It turns out it fails in ways that are quiet, patterned, and easy to miss until the slab is already off the wall. A few of those failure modes map onto what you're describing.

---

**1. Surface exfoliation: the most recently added layer is the most decay-prone**

In granite, it is the surface sheets that spall first. Pressure relief at the exterior causes parallel fractures that propagate inward, layer by layer, while the deep core stays intact. The failure is not random -- it is concentrated at the outermost, most exposed material.

Your collection fails the same way. The old binder cards are inaccessible but durable; they will be there whenever you get to them. The recently saved items -- the bookmarks, the Instagram saves, the screenshot links -- are your surface layer, and they are already spalling. The paywalls and deleted pages are exfoliation events. The failure is not distributed evenly across the collection; it is concentrated at your most digitally-mediated, most recently-acquired layer.

What transfers: the conservation priority is the *deep core*, not the surface. The binder cards represent the highest-value, highest-risk single point in your collection -- one physical copy, off-site, irreplaceable. Digitizing those first is the structurally correct move, because the surface layer (bookmarks, links) is lossy by nature and no single copy of it matters much. You might find it useful to think about which layer in your collection is load-bearing versus which is inherently ephemeral, and sequence your consolidation accordingly.

---

**2. Kaolinization: false apparent volume**

Feldspar in granite hydrolyzes slowly into kaolin clay. From the outside the rock looks intact. But the binding mineral has been silently converting from hard to soft for decades. You cannot tell from the surface area how much of the structure is still granite and how much is already clay.

Your collection has this problem. It looks large -- many saves, many bookmarks, many screenshots. But a meaningful fraction of it is hollow: dead links, image-based text that can't be searched or found, pages behind paywalls. The apparent volume of the collection is not the actual recoverable volume. You may be operating as though you have 300 recipes when you have something closer to 180 accessible ones and 120 clay-soft pointers to nothing.

What transfers: a triage pass before building any system. Separate what is genuinely accessible from what is just a stored reference to nothing. That number -- the real recoverable count -- is the one worth organizing. In my work I don't design around apparent rock strength; I test for actual intact material. You might find it clarifying to run the same audit before investing in an organizational scheme.

---

**3. Depth-to-surface inversion: same material, different context, different failure mode**

Granite's properties that make it excellent at depth -- massive, impermeable, pressure-bearing -- become liabilities at the surface, where thermal cycling, water infiltration, and expansion forces act on it. The material hasn't changed. The context has. The repair is not to change the granite; it is to recognize that the surface environment demands something different.

The grandmother's cards are your depth-layer material. They work perfectly for what they were made to do: stable, permanent, richly annotated, rich with handwritten context. The problem is not that the cards are defective. The problem is that the new context -- searchable, remote-accessible, dietary-filter-aware, Sunday-planning-deadline-driven -- demands properties the format cannot provide. Same collection, same value, different surface environment.

What transfers: the intervention is a bridge, not a replacement. You don't need to abandon the cards or reconceive them; you need a thin layer of digitization that preserves exactly what makes them valuable (her notes, her corrections, the handwriting if you care about it) while adding what the surface context demands (searchability, a photo, a tag for gluten-free). In my field we don't quarry out the deep granite because it doesn't suit surface conditions -- we build the structure that connects the two.

---

**4. Brittle fracture under point load: no plastic deformation at the moment of use**

Granite distributes compressive load excellently across a large area. But under a focused point load it does not bend or warn; it fractures immediately. No gradual deformation, no signal that failure is coming -- just intact, then broken.

Your system has this failure mode too. Under low-stress conditions -- casual browsing, adding a save -- the fragmented collection functions tolerably. But Sunday planning is a point load: a deadline, a family, a specific memory you're trying to retrieve under time pressure. Under that load the whole system tends to go from functional to completely broken, and you default to something you already know by heart rather than recover the thing everyone liked three weeks ago.

What transfers: a structure that distributes the search load *before* the point-load event, not during it. In load-bearing granite applications, engineers ensure the load path is wide, not narrow. For your planning, the equivalent is some small weekly record made *right after* the meal -- two lines, what it was called, where the source lives, how it landed -- so the Sunday search is consulting a log of 4-5 entries rather than probing a fragmented corpus under pressure. The load path is built incrementally, not under deadline.
