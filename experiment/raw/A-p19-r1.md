I'm a materials engineer who has spent my career in production ceramics, specifically slip casting - the process where liquid clay is poured into a porous plaster mold, the mold pulls water out and builds up a solid wall, and then you pour out the remaining slip and release the piece. The thing I think about constantly is the translation between two measurement systems that never speak the same language: the mold is defined by its porosity and water-absorption rate, but the piece I'm making is defined by its wall thickness. Neither is directly visible at the moment that matters. You have to translate constantly, or you lose the piece.

Looking at your problem, I notice three places where the same structure shows up in your work.

---

**The two-unit translation problem, and the conversion table that solves it.**

In slip casting, the mold and the finished piece live in incompatible units: the mold measures porosity and elapsed time; the piece measures wall thickness. Neither unit can simply replace the other - they're both real, they both matter, and you can't collapse them into one without losing something important. The solution isn't to abolish either unit. It's to build a reliable translation that lives inside the workflow - a lookup baked into practice: "with this slip, in this mold, after twelve minutes, you'll have approximately 4mm of wall." The maker doesn't stop thinking in their units; they gain a bridge.

Your pricing lives in hosts. Your users think in requests per month. These are two real units that cannot easily replace each other - hosts are operationally real to your infrastructure and your finance cycle, requests are experientially real to your trial users. The mismatch isn't confusion that better copy will fix; it's a genuine two-unit system that needs a bridge. In my world we build that bridge into the workflow itself - not into the spec sheet, but into the act of casting. You might find it worth asking where in your trial experience you could build a persistent translation layer: not a landing-page calculator (which, as you note, creates its own problems), but something that lives inside the product from the first session and converts the host count into the user's native unit throughout. A running "at your current request volume, this maps to N hosts" - the kind of thing you see when you know both units cold.

---

**The pour-out threshold: surfacing an invisible accumulation before the cliff.**

The most critical moment in slip casting is knowing when to pour out the remaining liquid. Too early and the wall is too thin - it collapses when you release it. Too late and the piece bonds to the mold and tears. That window is not marked on any visible clock; you infer it from elapsed time, visual inspection, and knowing your slip. The failure mode I dread is the piece that looks fine during casting and reveals the problem only when it comes out of the kiln - because the damage was done at a step where everything appeared normal.

Your trial has the same encoded-at-an-invisible-step structure. The host limit cliff at week one isn't where the trial fails; it's where the failure becomes visible. The damage is done during onboarding, when nothing has gone wrong yet. In my practice, the move against this failure mode is to surface the invisible accumulation: I mark mold cast-counts, I time my fills, I probe the wall before I pour out. I make the hidden variable visible while there's still time to act on it. In your trial, the proximity to the host limit is accumulating invisibly all week - a running gauge that showed the user where they stood in the unit you're both tracking might convert that cliff into a ramp. This doesn't require changing the unit or the tier structure; it only requires making the hidden accumulation a live signal. Whether that's a dashboard element, an email sequence, or something in your onboarding flow is your call.

---

**The fired piece is a lagging indicator - the mold is where the problem lives.**

Plaster molds wear out. Each casting cycle degrades the mold's porosity slightly, and eventually the mold stops pulling water correctly and you start getting thin, malformed pieces. The critical thing: this degradation is invisible on the surface of the mold. An inexperienced caster discovers the failure only when pieces start coming out wrong - at the kiln stage, which is too late. An experienced one tracks cast-count, spots the degradation before it expresses, and replaces the mold while it can still be done cleanly.

Your exit survey data ("felt tricked") is the fired piece - the visible, late-stage expression of a problem that was encoded earlier, at the mold stage. The mold, in your case, is the trial's trust infrastructure: what a user is led to expect, and what they discover. The structural point from my field is that the lagging indicator (churn rate, exit survey sentiment) is a reliable signal that something upstream is degrading - but by the time it appears, the piece is already lost. The useful question my field would ask is: at what earlier, less-visible step is the mismatch between expectation and reality being formed? In casting that's the pour-in, not the pour-out. In your trial it might be something that happens before the host limit is ever hit.
