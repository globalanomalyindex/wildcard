I'm a surveyor who specializes in establishing geodetic baselines - the foundational measured distance from which an entire triangulation chain is computed across a landscape. My particular obsession is with the *material* properties of the measuring tape itself: I use invar steel, chosen for its near-zero thermal expansion coefficient, and I spend most of my professional attention on what happens to that material under tension, temperature, and repeated handling. I have to, because any error introduced at the baseline multiplies as the chain extends. A 1 mm error over 10 km can become a meter-scale displacement 500 km away, and - this is the part that keeps me up at night - that error is invisible. Every subsequent triangle looks internally consistent. The chain looks right. It's just wrong, and it stays wrong.

Looking at your situation, three things genuinely rhyme.

---

**The tape that looks fine but carries hidden stretch.** In my field, a baseline tape can develop cumulative creep - microscopic elongation from previous campaigns - while still appearing precise. It gives readings with the full formality of precision, but the readings don't correspond to true distance. Your six memorized pieces are structurally this: genuine knowledge, established by a method (finger patterns, muscle memory) that bypasses the calibration instrument you actually need, which is notation reading. From the outside the pieces look like musical competence - and in one sense they are - but they were laid down without the invar tape, so they can't propagate. Every piece learned this way is a baseline measurement taken with the wrong instrument. More pieces learned the same way extends an already-compromised chain further without correcting the foundational reading.

What this might suggest for you: the memorized repertoire isn't the problem to solve, and adding more of it isn't progress toward reading fluency. In my world I'd identify specifically what properties of the tape are wrong - not "the tape is bad" but "the tape has stretched by this amount per meter, under this tension." You already have an unusually clear version of this: you know it's bass clef and syncopation specifically. That's the error characterization. A surveyor with that diagnosis doesn't re-survey the whole country; they find the minimum correction that re-establishes the baseline on firm ground.

---

**Re-measuring a segment that wasn't broken.** When a triangulation chain has an error, the surveyor's instinct has to resist one specific trap: going back to re-measure the segments that were fine. It feels like starting over is the responsible move. It isn't. Re-measuring accurate segments costs time and produces no new information, because the error didn't enter there. Your cycle of restarting beginner books is structurally this: the early chapters of the book correspond to a part of the chain you already measured correctly. You know those notes, that hand position, those rhythms. Re-working them doesn't improve the chain; it just delays arrival at the point where the chain actually broke. The question a surveyor asks is: *at exactly which triangle did the cumulative error first exceed tolerance?* In your case, you already know: it's the clef you can't read fluently, and the rhythmic patterns that fall apart. Starting there, not at the beginning, is the measurement that would actually change the error budget.

---

**The uncertainty budget as working knowledge.** In precise surveying, the honest deliverable isn't a number - it's a number with a documented uncertainty budget: which error sources contributed, at what magnitude, so you know exactly how far the chain can be trusted before it needs re-checking. Most practitioners skip this documentation and just report the final coordinate. The result is confidence without calibration. You're in an unusual position here: you've done this work already, unsolicited. You have a real uncertainty budget - bass clef reading is slow and error-prone; syncopation collapses; rhythm in novel contexts is unreliable; the six pieces are solid. That is a precise characterization of where your chain is trustworthy and where it isn't. In my world, that documented budget is what enables a targeted correction campaign rather than a wholesale re-survey. If you find yourself restarting before you reach the parts you need, the budget you already have might be worth pointing at explicitly when choosing what to practice in a given 20-minute session - you know exactly which segment needs measurement, and the fact that the early chain is already solid is information, not a problem.
