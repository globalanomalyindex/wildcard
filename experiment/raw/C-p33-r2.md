Here are four angles from outside the usual frame of dispatch and inventory management:

**1. The "milk run" logic from lean manufacturing**
In factories, parts replenishment was historically triggered by demand at each station, which caused the same crisscross waste you are seeing. Toyota replaced it with a fixed-route milk run: a small vehicle circles the floor on a set schedule, dropping off what each station needs and picking up empties. Applied to your situation, this flips the assumption that techs should travel to jobs in arrival order. Instead, you zone the metro area into four or five geographic clusters and assign each tech a home zone for the day. A separate parts runner on a fixed circuit resupplies vans at midday. The tech stops driving across town; the runner becomes a predictable asset. You might try sketching one test zone with two techs for a week and measuring actual drive time versus call count.

**2. The pharmacy formulary model for parts**
Hospital pharmacies have too many possible drugs to stock everything everywhere, so they use a tiered formulary: a short list of high-probability drugs stocked in every satellite location, a longer list at the main pharmacy available same-day, and rare drugs sourced overnight. Your parts problem is structurally identical. Right now every van is a random sample of history. If you pulled the last 18 months of "second-visit" call reasons and ranked parts by frequency, you could define a van formulary (the 30 items that resolve 80 percent of callbacks) versus a staging kit a tech picks up at the shop for known complex jobs. The second-visit rate is likely concentrated in a small number of SKUs, not spread across everything.

**3. Pre-shift intelligence from emergency medicine triage**
Emergency departments learned that waiting until a patient arrives to decide severity creates cascade delays. They moved triage to the door: a quick assessment before the patient is in a room determines resource allocation before the clock starts. Your dispatch analogy: right now a tech arrives and discovers the call is a control board replacement, not a capacitor swap. What if a brief structured phone screen (three questions, two minutes) before scheduling allowed you to pre-classify calls into "van stock resolves this" versus "needs staged parts" versus "needs your best installer"? That pre-sort would let you route the complex calls to specific techs with the right parts pre-pulled, rather than discovering complexity on arrival. You might trial this on new calls only, not emergency calls, and see whether callback rates shift.

**4. Decentralized cache placement from content delivery networks**
CDNs put copies of popular content in edge servers close to users, so the request never has to travel to the origin server. The insight is that proximity of the resource to the point of need is more valuable than central consolidation. Your "best installer spends half his day driving" is a centralization problem. If that installer is genuinely the constraint, you might consider whether a small satellite staging point (a rented bay, a partner shop, even a locked trailer in a central parking lot) in the dense half of your service area would let him restock mid-day without returning to the main shop. The investment threshold to test this is low: one month of tracking where his jobs cluster geographically versus where the shop is.
