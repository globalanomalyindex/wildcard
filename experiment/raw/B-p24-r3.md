I measure silk fiber quality — not the kind of work that makes it onto a loom, but the diagnostic work that happens before: grading raw thread by its mechanical properties, testing how it behaves under load, and finding where the failure is hiding inside what looks perfectly fine on the outside. Let me share a few things that seem to rhyme with what you're describing.

---

**The sericin problem: you're measuring the coating, not the fiber.**

In raw silk, the filament is encased in a natural gum called sericin. It looks like silk, it handles like silk, but it's not the quality you're actually selling — sericin is stiff and dull, and it has to come off before the fiber underneath can be assessed. A common grading error is evaluating raw thread and never realizing you're measuring the gum layer. The actual fiber has different properties entirely.

The structural parallel: your first 45 seconds of footage is probably accumulated naturally — the way a project opens when you're setting up and getting started. That opening is sericin. It's not fabricated or wrong, it's just the coating that forms before the quality material underneath. The 30% dropout isn't a judgment on your woodworking; it may be viewers encountering the gum layer and leaving before the fiber. In my world the fix isn't to make the sericin prettier — it's to ask whether you can move the degumming point earlier in the sequence, so the quality-signal the viewer came for is the first thing that touches them.

---

**Confounded variables in quality assessment.**

When a silk batch comes back with a defect, I have to figure out whether it's the fiber count, the twist, the fiber itself, or the finishing treatment — because they're all measured in the same output (a breaking strength test or a drape evaluation) and they all co-vary. If you don't isolate them, you can chase twist when it's actually count, and fix nothing. The diagnostic discipline is to change one variable at a time, or at least to have a mental model of which variables move independently.

Your impression problem has the same shape: thumbnails, titles, and topics are confounded in your outcome metric. But they operate at genuinely different stages of the funnel — a topic determines whether the algorithm surfaces the video to someone who wasn't looking; a thumbnail determines whether a person in a feed stops scrolling; a title determines whether the person who stopped decides to click. They look like one variable ("the discovery package") but they're mechanically distinct inputs with different levers. In measurement, the first move with confounded variables is to map the causal chain before testing anything — once you know which mechanism drives which, you can at least make a hypothesis about which one to hold constant and which to change.

---

**The stress-strain curve: one number lies, the shape tells the truth.**

I never report just a breaking strength. I report the full stress-strain curve, because the single-point number hides the mechanism. A fiber that breaks at the same force as another can have a completely different failure character — one has a long graceful elongation before it goes; the other snaps suddenly at low extension. The shape of the curve tells you where the weakness actually is.

The 30% figure is a single-point reading. But your platform's retention graph has a shape inside that first 45 seconds — it's not a flat cliff. Something is different at second 5 than at second 35. The dropout concentrated at the very first seconds (before an image or voice registers) is a different failure from dropout at second 30 (halfway through an intro that's going long). If you haven't looked at the granular shape of the curve inside that window — not just the scalar at 45 seconds — that's where the diagnostic information lives. I'd be curious whether the retention is decelerating smoothly or whether there's a discontinuity at a specific point, because that locates the defect.

---

**Reelability: you can't discover internal discontinuities at the loom.**

In sericulture there's a quality dimension called reelability — how cleanly a cocoon unwinds into a continuous filament. A cocoon can look perfect externally, but have internal structural breaks that only appear when you try to reel it. You discover this at the worst moment: mid-production. The pre-screening test is a controlled reel under measurement conditions before committing to the full run.

The structural parallel to your situation is the footage irreversibility constraint. Once a piece is glued up, the shooting window is closed. If you filmed six hours and the key joinery sequence is underlit, or the second camera missed the moment, you find out during editing — at the loom, not at the pre-reel check. A reelability pre-test is a structured check of the cocoon before committing; the equivalent here is a shot-coverage checklist run through at the end of each filming day (before the garage is cleaned up), specifically targeting the moments you know are irreplaceable. Not a full review, just a quick structural audit: did both cameras capture the moment that can't be re-done? That's the pre-reel pull.
