I work with the procedural logic of forensic evidence — specifically the structural rules that govern what makes a piece of evidence *trustworthy enough to act on*. The concept that runs through everything I do is chain of custody: an unbroken, time-indexed provenance record of every transfer of an object, established contemporaneously, so that at any moment you can reconstruct exactly whose hands it passed through — and bound the contamination precisely when something goes wrong.

Looking at your situation through that lens, three things genuinely rhyme.

---

**1. The "rotate everything" response is structurally correct for a broken chain — and that's the diagnosis, not the symptom.**

In forensic work, when custody documentation has a gap, the contamination propagates *forward from the break point*. If I can't prove who had an exhibit between step 3 and step 6 of a ten-step chain, I cannot selectively trust steps 7–10 — the object is tainted from step 3 onward. The correct response is to treat everything from that point as compromised.

Your laptop-theft response — rotate almost everything because you couldn't say which credentials needed it — followed the same structural logic, and it was right. The problem isn't that you rotated too much; it's that the undocumented chain *forced* you into that position. Without a break-point timestamp (when was the contractor provisioned each credential?), you have no floor to stand on. The blast radius is unbounded by definition.

This reframes the SOC 2 prep goal: you aren't building a better incident response. You're building the chain so that next time, the blast radius has a documented floor.

---

**2. Chain of custody cannot be reconstructed retroactively — it must be established at the moment of transfer.**

This is the structural rule that makes contemporaneous documentation non-negotiable in evidence law. Post-hoc testimony about what probably happened is not a substitute; the moment passes and the record either exists or it doesn't.

When your contractor was onboarded, the credentials they received weren't logged as *transfer events*. You tried to reconstruct that inventory after the incident and found it unknowable — because it is, structurally, once the moment of provisioning has passed without a record. Engineers who push back on provisioning overhead are implicitly arguing for retroactive reconstruction. The forensic argument is that retroactive reconstruction doesn't work; it isn't an equivalent alternative. The overhead belongs at provisioning time *because* there is no other time.

That might be a useful framing when the engineering pushback comes: this isn't process for its own sake, it's the only window the record can be written in.

---

**3. Every store that holds a credential is a custodian — and each custodian is a link that must be maintained.**

A core principle in evidence handling is *minimal handling*: every additional transfer creates a new custodial link that must be documented and defended. The secure design reduces the number of custodians, not just audits them.

Your .env files, CI variables, shared password vault, and hardcoded strings aren't just "messy credential hygiene." Each one is an undocumented custodian — a node in a chain that was never written down. The question isn't only "where do our credentials live?" but "why does each store need custody, and which custodians can be eliminated entirely rather than audited?" Auditing all of them is expensive and fragile; eliminating custodians is structurally cleaner. In my world, the fewer hands an exhibit passes through, the shorter the chain you have to defend. Consolidation isn't just tidier — it's fewer failure points in the record.
