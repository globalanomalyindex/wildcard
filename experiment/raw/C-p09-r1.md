Here are four angles from outside the usual frame:

---

**1. Air traffic flow management — metering, not blocking**

Air traffic controllers don't tell a plane it can't land; they issue a slot time and adjust the sequence. The key insight is that demand is often negotiable when you give the requester a committed future window. Your enterprise tenants run exports at the top of the hour because that's the default — nothing more. You could expose a lightweight "schedule negotiation" endpoint: a tenant sends a desired window, and your system returns the earliest slot where their projected query cost fits under a per-window budget. No hard rejection, no SLA conversation — just a committed time they can rely on, and load that spreads itself. You don't need tenant isolation to implement this; you need a small queue planner and a cost estimator per query shape.

---

**2. Epidemiological contact tracing — reconstruct the graph retroactively**

When tenant IDs are threaded inconsistently, the instinct is to do a big refactor. But epidemiologists rarely get clean data either — they reconstruct transmission chains from partial records after the fact. You might try a similar approach: instrument at the edges you do control (the load balancer, the ClickHouse query log, the export job trigger) and build a probabilistic attribution layer that correlates timing, client IP ranges, query fingerprints, and whatever tenant fragments exist. You don't need perfect threading to get 80% attribution confidence. That lets you build a cost heatmap and enforce soft limits today, without waiting for the threading refactor to finish.

---

**3. Electrical demand response — paying peak users to shift, not penalizing them**

Utilities can't always build more capacity, so they offer interruptible tariff contracts: customers who accept flexible delivery windows pay less and get a financial incentive to shift load. You have a contract problem and an incentive problem at the same time. Rather than adding a hard rate limiter (which creates a support incident every time it fires), you might propose a "scheduled export credit" to your enterprise customers — they commit to a staggered export window and receive something concrete in return (a price concession, a higher API quota outside peak windows, a guaranteed p95 target instead of p99). This reframes the SLA violation conversation into a joint optimization conversation, which is a much easier sell given that sales is already in the room.

---

**4. Shared irrigation canal management — upstream users set the rules, but gates are physical**

In traditional acequia systems, water rights are allocated by time slot, not by flow rate. Enforcement is structural: gates open and close on a schedule, removing the need for anyone to police consumption in real time. The analog here is pre-execution query budgeting at the ClickHouse layer itself — using resource groups or query complexity limits tied to a per-tenant token that's attached at ingress (even imperfectly, via the methods in angle 2). The "gate" is the query planner, not application code. This sidesteps the threading inconsistency: you don't need tenant ID in every request hop if you can attach a resource group label at the one point where the query actually enters ClickHouse. It also means enforcement survives a deploy or a code path you forgot to instrument.

---

These are offered as directions to pressure-test, not prescriptions. Any one of them could be a dead end depending on constraints you haven't named yet.
