Here are a few angles from outside the usual "recipe app" frame that might help:

**1. The naturalist's field journal — log the meal, not the recipe**

Field biologists don't catalog every plant species in advance; they record what they actually encountered, with notes on conditions. You might try flipping your habit: instead of filing recipes before you cook them, keep a running log of meals you actually made. A simple weekly note — dish name, rough source, who liked it, any substitutions — creates a searchable personal record that lives in one place and captures the thing you made three weeks ago far better than a saved link ever could. The log becomes your real collection; the bookmarks are just raw material you may or may not consult.

**2. The pharmacist's inventory method — count by depletion, not by looking**

Pharmacies don't rely on someone eyeballing shelves to reorder; they track what gets dispensed. For your spice problem, you might treat your pantry like a dispensary: each time you use something up or open the last of it, you add it to a standing reorder list rather than trying to audit the cabinet before shopping. A shared note called "gone or almost gone" that anyone in the household can add to costs almost nothing to maintain and would cut most of the duplicate-spice problem without requiring a full pantry audit system.

**3. The repertoire model from jazz — distinguish your "standards" from your "deep cuts"**

Jazz musicians separate a small set of standards they can play any night from a much larger repertoire they know but don't keep active. For meal planning, you might explicitly divide your collection into two tiers: a short list of maybe fifteen to twenty dishes your family reliably likes (standards), and everything else. Weekly planning then becomes "pick from standards with occasional deep cuts" rather than trying to search an undifferentiated mass of saves. The grandmother's cards and the three-weeks-ago hit belong on the standards list; the Instagram saves are deep cuts you may never need to organize at all.

**4. The archivist's finding aid — describe the container, not every item**

Archivists dealing with large collections often write a "finding aid": a description of what a box contains and how it's organized, rather than cataloging every document. For your grandmother's binder, you might create a single index page — a handwritten or typed one-pager that lists what's in there by category (soups, pastas, sides, etc.) — without digitizing the cards themselves. The binder stays at your parents' house; the index travels with you. A photograph of the index page on your phone might be enough to remember what's there and decide when it's worth the trip to retrieve a specific card.
