Here are four angles from outside the usual "recipe app" frame:

**1. The lab notebook model (from scientific field research)**
Field scientists working without reliable infrastructure keep a physical "master index" — a single handwritten page at the front of a binder that maps every item by date and keyword, even if the item itself lives elsewhere. What you noticed: your problem isn't that the recipe isn't saved somewhere, it's that you can't triangulate back to it. What to try: keep one running doc (even a notes app page) where every time you cook something you like, you write one line — date, dish name, one flavor word, and where it lives. Not the recipe itself, just the pointer. Three weeks of Sunday planning and you'd have a working index with about 20 lines.

**2. The costume designer's continuity sheet (from film production)**
Continuity departments photograph every actor's costume before wrapping a scene so the next shoot day matches exactly. The photo isn't the costume — it's just proof of state. What you noticed: your screenshots and Instagram saves are trying to be both the document and the proof, which is why they fail at both. What to try: treat the screenshot as a continuity card, not the recipe. Add one line of text to the photo (iOS lets you annotate) or drop it in a folder named by month. The goal is traceability, not completeness — you can always find the full recipe later once you know it exists.

**3. The mechanic's parts manifest (from auto repair)**
A good shop doesn't try to remember what's on the shelf — they run a quick inventory check before ordering anything, because reordering a part you have costs more than the count. What you noticed: your spice-rebuying problem is a parts manifest problem, not a recipe problem. What to try: separate it entirely from meal planning. A single photo of your spice shelf, taken Sunday morning before you shop, is faster to reference than any app. Some people keep a standing list of the five or six spices they routinely run out of and just eyeball the rest — the manifest doesn't need to be complete to cut most of the waste.

**4. The oral tradition compression technique (from folk culture)**
Before writing, recipes spread as compressed mnemonics — "enough fat to coat the pan, enough acid to wake it up." The whole dish lived in a few memorable phrases, not a procedure. What you noticed: the dish you made three weeks ago that everyone liked probably has one or two sensory signatures you actually remember, you just can't search for them. What to try: when something works, record it in your own compressed language first — "the crispy chickpea thing with lemon and harissa" — and let that be the search key. You don't need to archive the recipe if you can reconstruct it from a strong enough description, and a three-word note is something you'll actually write.
