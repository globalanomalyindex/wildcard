I'm a corporate restructuring lawyer. My obsession is the moment a capital stack — layers of senior debt, mezzanine, equity — encounters insufficient assets to satisfy all its claims. I study what the ranking of those claims does to behavior *before* the default event. Here's what I notice when I look at your problem.

---

**1. Your trial users don't know where they rank in the waterfall until they hit the wall.**

In a distressed capital structure, the most dangerous condition is not default itself — it's informational opacity *before* default. A junior claimant who can't see the waterfall will behave as if their claim is senior. They commit capital against a position they don't actually hold. Then the default event arrives and the waterfall becomes visible all at once.

What you're describing is structurally identical: a trial user picks a tier against a mental model (requests/month) that doesn't correspond to the actual claim structure (hosts). They commit four weeks of onboarding effort against a position they don't hold. The host limit is the default event. The mismatch was always there; the event just made it legible.

In restructuring, we don't solve this by rewriting who owns what — we issue a *disclosure statement* before the creditor vote. A document that makes the waterfall legible without changing the capital structure at all. The obligation is on the issuer to surface the conversion math before the creditor commits, not after.

You can't change your unit before the board cycle. But you might find it useful to surface the host-to-request conversion math prominently during trial signup — not as fine print, but as the primary frame: "based on your expected request volume, you'll likely need X hosts, which puts you in tier Y." Finance's model doesn't move; the waterfall just becomes visible before the commitment rather than at default.

---

**2. The trial itself could be structured as a placement, not a wire-and-discover.**

Mezzanine financing exists because of a structural gap: the senior creditor won't lend enough, the borrower needs more, and bridging that gap requires a custom placement — the issuer and the investor agree on terms *before* committing, rather than the investor discovering the real terms at the first covenant breach.

The structural gap in your trial is the same: the cheapest tier undershoots real need; the customer won't commit to a higher tier without evidence. The current design is "wire the money, discover the terms." The placement structure reverses that: a guided discovery period ends in a *placement conversation* — "here's what your actual usage pattern looked like over two weeks, here's where you actually land in the stack, here's the tier that fits." The customer isn't upgrading; they're completing their placement.

This reframes the upgrade conversation from "you exceeded the limit" (default) to "here's your term sheet" (placement). The pricing unit doesn't change; the moment of commitment does.

---

**3. Covenants exist to trigger a conversation before default, because waiting for default is too expensive for both parties.**

A debt covenant is not a hard cap. It's a threshold that triggers a mandatory renegotiation window — the borrower and the lender talk at 80% of the financial test, not at 101%. This is the core engineering insight: default is binary and adversarial; a covenant converts it into a graduated, negotiated event. Crucially, it's triggered while both parties still have something to protect.

Your host-limit hit is a binary default. Everything bad happens at once: monitoring degrades, the user feels deceived, exit happens. A covenant-equivalent here would be a usage alert at 50% of host capacity — framed not as a warning but as a proactive signal: "you're tracking toward your host limit by week two; here's what your usage looks like and what the next tier would cost you." At 50%, the user still has something to protect (their onboarding investment), you still have something to protect (the conversion), and both parties are in a negotiation rather than a termination.

The covenant threshold and its framing ("here's what this means for your monitoring continuity, not just a wall") are within your control regardless of the pricing unit.

---

These are three separate moves — disclosure legibility, placement structure, covenant-triggered renegotiation — and any one works independently. Happy to pull any of them into a more concrete thread, or step back out and leave you to it.
