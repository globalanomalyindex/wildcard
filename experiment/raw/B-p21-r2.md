I work in photomask engineering and signal processing — the discipline of using precisely patterned barriers to control where energy lands and where it is withheld. A mask does not rewrite the substrate; it gates access to it. I think about masks as selectivity devices, and everything I notice about your situation comes through that lens.

---

**1. The archive isn't missing content — it's missing an access layer, and those are separate problems with separate costs**

In signal processing, a window function (a mask in time) lets you analyze any segment of a recorded signal without touching the waveform. The recording is complete; the mask is what gives you a handle on a particular region. Your 410 episodes are in the same position: the content is fully intact on the drives, what's absent is the time-indexed access layer over it — the transcript that lets you reach a particular moment. That distinction matters practically because it tells you the bottleneck isn't storage or content preservation — it's generating the window. Local speech-to-text tools like Whisper run entirely on your machine, produce word-level timestamps, and impose no upload-size constraint, because the audio never leaves the drive. You could run a batch across one drive overnight, with no 250 MB ceiling involved, and wake up with a timed text layer over every file that processed. The mask is cheap to cut once you have the right tool; the cutting is the whole job.

**2. Two different retrieval pressures call for two different mask resolutions — you don't need uniform precision across all 410**

In lithography, the expensive act is cutting the mask to a given resolution; each subsequent use of it is cheap. A coarse mask is fast to cut and good enough for some purposes; a fine mask takes longer but enables precise patterning. Your archive has two distinct retrieval pressures operating at different resolutions: guests who want to find roughly where they said something (fuzzy, topic-level recall) and a publisher who needs exact wording with timestamps (precision retrieval). These are different mask resolutions applied to the same underlying material. The key question for your three-week window: does the publisher need all 410 episodes, or a defined subset? The publisher's specific licensing list is an alignment mark — it tells you exactly which episodes require fine-grained, word-accurate transcription. You could apply coarse processing (keyword spotting, speaker-change detection, rough chapter markers) broadly across the catalog to serve the guest use-case, and invest fine-grained transcription only where the publisher's alignment mark says to point. In my field, flooding the whole substrate with high-energy exposure when only part of it needs fine patterning wastes the expensive resource. The same principle applies here: ask the publisher for the episode list first; that's what gates where you invest precision.

**3. The inconsistent folder naming is a mask misalignment problem, and a manifest file is the fix**

In patterned exposure, throughput isn't limited by the energy source — it's limited by mask alignment. If the mask isn't aligned to the substrate, more energy doesn't help; it just exposes the wrong places. Your two drives with inconsistent naming conventions (date-based on one, guest-based on the other) are a mask-alignment failure: when you go to retrieve a file, you can't aim precisely because the coordinate system isn't consistent. Any transcription pipeline you build will hit this before it hits anything else. The structural fix is what I'd call cutting a master mask first: a one-time inventory that maps every file on both drives to a common set of fields — guest name, episode number, date, duration, drive path. This manifest costs a few hours once, and every downstream operation — transcription batch jobs, the publisher's timestamp lookups, guests' requests — becomes a lookup against a known coordinate, not a scan of a heterogeneous physical structure. In lithography we call this the registration step; you can't expose accurately until you know where everything is.
