I'm a disaster medicine educator — I teach the START and SALT mass-casualty triage protocols at regional training programs. My field is about allocating scarce treatment capacity under time pressure when the cost of treating the non-critical is measured in preventable deaths among the critical. The thing I've spent years watching go wrong at incident scenes is the same structural failure, over and over. A few observations from that work that might be worth something here.

---

**The sorting problem is prior to the treatment problem.**

In a mass-casualty incident, responders arrive at a field of casualties and — unless they've drilled triage discipline into muscle memory — they start treating the nearest person. That is the instinct. And it is exactly the wrong move, because treatment before sorting means you spend your scarcest resource (skilled responder time) in proportion to proximity and noise, not in proportion to survivability benefit.

The first protocol action is always to walk the field and tag. Not treat. Tag.

What maps here: you said nobody can confidently say which alerts actually protect the regulated paths. That is not a threshold problem — it is the field being un-walked. You can't route selectively, can't suppress safely, can't set different thresholds for different lanes, because the classification gate doesn't exist yet. Every alert is in the same undifferentiated pile. The blocked action (selective loosening on the noise, preserved sensitivity on the compliance paths) and the reason it's blocked (no pathway label on each alert) are the same structure as an untriaged field.

The first move your triage protocol says to make isn't tuning anything. It's walking the field: a short, one-time labeling exercise that attaches a pathway tag — regulated-flow guardian: yes/no — to each alert, ideally in a two-hour session with the person who originally wrote it or the service owner who owns the path. That tag is what unlocks all the downstream routing, and it costs almost nothing compared to what you've already spent on false pages.

---

**The unsorted pile is the most dangerous state — specifically because salience is anti-correlated with criticality.**

In casualty fields, the victims who make the most noise and draw the most attention are not systematically the ones whose survival depends on immediate action. Responders learn this the hard way: the "walking wounded" — screaming, visible, mobile — consume attention while the quiet crush injuries die in the corner.

In your case: the CPU and disk threshold alerts firing on batch jobs are the walking wounded. High frequency, loud, immediately identifiable as noise with zero diagnostic effort, demanding a page and a response. The payment-flow failures you actually care about are lower frequency, potentially quieter, and sitting in the same pile. The structural danger isn't just wasted work — it's that high-frequency noise trains responders to *expect* noise, so a genuine signal in the same alert channel starts to feel like more noise.

The triage intervention for this failure mode isn't turning down the volume on the screaming. It's creating a visual lane separation before the responder ever picks up the phone — so the cognitive pull toward the high-frequency alerts is structurally interrupted rather than resisted by willpower per page.

---

**Black-tagging requires an explicit criterion and institutional authorization — ad-hoc tagging is the most dangerous state of all.**

The hardest act in triage is the expectant tag: marking a casualty as "do not treat now" because the resources required would save more lives elsewhere. It is not abandonment — it is the act that protects the immediate pool. But it requires two things: a clear, auditable criterion (not "probably won't make it" — something objective) and institutional authorization. A responder who informally black-tags on their own judgment, without the criterion and without the tag being visible to anyone else, has created an unlabeled expectant: a casualty whose deterioration will be invisible, who may spontaneously survive without help, and whose status no one will know has changed.

Your two engineers who quietly muted their phones did exactly this. That is not a criticism of them — they found their own triage discipline because the system provided none. But ad-hoc individual suppression is the same structural failure as an unlabeled expectant: if one of those muted alerts fires on a genuine regulated-path failure on a Tuesday night, no one will know the page didn't land.

The legitimate version of what they already did is an institutional suppression criterion: "this alert class has zero confirmed true positives over the last 90 days, batch-job correlation is confirmed, and it does not touch a tagged regulated path — and this suppression carries an automatic re-triage trigger if the batch job exits abnormally." That criterion is auditable, it's reversible, it satisfies your compliance requirement (the label proves you know which paths it doesn't protect), and it converts the informal muting into something defensible. In my field we call that closing the loop on the tag: the expectant patient doesn't disappear, they just move to a watched-but-not-treated lane with a condition that re-routes them.

---

Three things, then, if any of them are useful: walk the field first (the labeling exercise before any threshold work), create a lane separation that interrupts the salience pull before the page lands, and convert the informal suppression that's already happening into an authorized criterion that's visible and reversible.
