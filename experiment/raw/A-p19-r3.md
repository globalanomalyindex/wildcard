I'm a geochronologist - specifically, I work on the IntCal radiocarbon calibration problem. My job is converting raw isotope-ratio measurements (what the accelerator mass spectrometer actually reads) into calendar years. The instrument is precise; the trouble is in the transfer function between the quantity you can measure and the quantity you actually want to know. That transfer function - the calibration curve - has its own shape, its own uncertainty, and its own pathological regions. I've been looking at your problem from that lens.

---

**1. The plateau problem: the curve shape is the constraint, not the instrument.**

In my field there are stretches of the calibration curve called radiocarbon plateaus - regions where many calendar years map to nearly identical measured isotope ratios. A sample measured at, say, 2,800 ± 30 radiocarbon years might correspond to three distinct calendar intervals spread across a century. No improvement in instrument precision resolves this - the ambiguity lives in the shape of the curve, not in measurement noise.

The structure in your problem is the same: hosts and requests don't have a linear relationship, and at small scale they diverge sharply. A team with 5 hosts and a team with 40 hosts may have very similar request-per-day profiles early in a trial - but the pricing curve treats them as worlds apart. The unit isn't just a different label for the same thing; it has a non-linear transfer function, and that function is steep exactly where your trial users live. What looks like a "simple unit mismatch" is actually a region of the curve where the instrument (host count) has poor discriminating power over the quantity of interest (customer value / scale). You can't fix a plateau by measuring more carefully.

In my world, when I know a sample falls on a plateau, I report the *probability distribution* of possible calendar ages - multi-modal, with visible peaks - rather than collapsing it to a false point estimate and letting the reader think I know more than I do. There may be an analogue here for onboarding: not "here are three tiers" but "here is where your current usage pattern sits on the host-to-request curve, and here is what the next step looks like from where you are." Making the non-linearity visible at trial start - not as a warning, just as an honest map - is not the same as changing the unit. You might find that worth exploring.

---

**2. Wiggle-matching: when a single measurement can't place you, the shape of a series can.**

When a sample falls on a plateau and I can't date it from one measurement, I look for wiggle-matching opportunities: if I have a sequence of samples with known relative spacing - tree rings are the classic case - I can match the shape of the sequence's wiggle pattern against the calibration curve and land a precise date that no single measurement could give me. The relative structure carries information the absolute value can't.

Your trial users don't just generate a snapshot; they generate a time series - request growth velocity over their first seven days. A user who starts day one at 200 requests and doubles daily is on a very specific trajectory that maps to a predictable position on your host-limit curve, and you can see it by day three. That's a wiggle pattern. Rather than waiting for the host-limit trip in week one, a behavioral trigger on trajectory shape - not a threshold warning, but a "here's what your growth shape looks like relative to where tiers begin" moment surfaced proactively - is the same move as wiggle-matching: you're using the shape of the series to place the user on the curve before the ambiguity resolves into a collision. This transfers a concrete timing intervention: the signal is available earlier than the symptom.

---

**3. Calibration curve uncertainty is separate from, and often larger than, instrument uncertainty.**

This one is for the finance problem specifically. In geochronology, there's a persistent error I see in published dates: investigators report a tight measurement error (± 30 years from the AMS) and omit the calibration curve's own uncertainty, which can easily be ± 150 years over that same interval. The reported precision is real; the implied accuracy isn't. The model looks tighter than it is because one source of uncertainty isn't being propagated.

Your finance model runs on hosts-per-customer as its revenue signal. The model has a built-in assumption: that host count is a reliable proxy for value delivered and revenue realized across all customer scales. That assumption carries its own uncertainty - and at small scale, where the curve is steep and non-linear, that uncertainty is probably large. Asking to change the unit runs into the board cycle. But presenting the model's own confidence interval at small-scale customer segments - not "the unit is wrong" but "the model's accuracy at this segment is lower than its precision suggests, because the calibration assumption breaks down here" - is a different kind of conversation. Finance already thinks in error bounds; showing them where their calibration error exceeds their measurement error works inside their frame rather than asking them to leave it.
