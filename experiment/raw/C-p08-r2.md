Here are four angles from outside the usual on-call frame that might be worth considering:

---

**1. Ecological false-positive budgets (the "crying wolf" effect in wildlife monitoring)**

Researchers tracking endangered animals deal with exactly this: motion sensors fire constantly on wind, birds, shadows. The fix they settled on is not raising thresholds uniformly but partitioning alerts by what ecologists call "detection specificity" — separating sensors that guard a critical migration corridor from ambient-coverage sensors, and applying completely different response rules to each tier. The connection to your problem: instead of asking "which alerts can we raise thresholds on," ask "which alerts sit on the regulated payment paths?" Annotate only those as high-specificity. Everything else can default to a review queue rather than a page. The mapping exercise itself — walking each alert to a payment flow — gives compliance the audit trail it needs, and probably reveals that most of your CPU/disk alerts are in the ambient tier.

---

**2. Anesthesia's "alarm fatigue" literature**

Hospitals documented this exact dynamic in ICUs in the 1990s: up to 90% of bedside alarms were clinically irrelevant, nurses habituated, then missed the real ones. The intervention that worked was not silence but *alarm specificity scoring* — for each alert class, calculating positive predictive value (how often does this alarm precede a true event?) and displaying that score alongside the alert. Two things happened: staff trusted high-PPV alarms more; and low-PPV alarms were restructured as informational rather than actionable. You already have the data to do this — your postmortem records are a labeled dataset. For each alert type, you can compute: pages fired vs. pages that required action. That ratio, surfaced to the team, reframes the conversation from "are we overloaded" to "this alert has a 3% action rate — what would make it worth keeping?"

---

**3. Manufacturing's two-alarm principle (process control)**

In chemical and refinery process control, operators distinguish *process alarms* (something is trending wrong — take note) from *safety instrumented system alarms* (the regulated hard limit — act now, no debate). These are wired separately, logged separately, and subject to different audit regimes. The insight for you: your compliance requirement is essentially a safety instrumented system constraint. The CPU/disk threshold alerts are process alarms. If you treat them as the same class of signal, every process alarm carries implicit compliance weight it doesn't deserve, and engineers rationally mute to survive. Separating them physically — different PagerDuty services, different escalation policies, different on-call rotations — does not require changing thresholds at all, and gives compliance a cleaner surface to audit.

---

**4. Garbage collection "stop-the-world" as a batching frame**

This is a softer conceptual angle. JVM garbage collectors have a "known-noisy window" problem: during a full GC, latency spikes in ways that would look catastrophic to a naive monitor. The standard fix is not to raise thresholds but to teach the alerting layer that certain metric excursions are *expected and bounded* during specific known operations. Your batch jobs are the equivalent of a scheduled GC pause. If your infrastructure can emit a structured event when a batch job starts and ends, alert evaluation during that window can be suppressed or reclassified automatically — not permanently, not for all alerts, but for the specific metrics (per-host CPU, disk throughput) that the batch job predictably moves. The window closes, normal evaluation resumes. This sidesteps the threshold question entirely for the noisiest class.

---

The thread connecting all four: your problem is not that thresholds are wrong, it is that all alerts are being treated as equivalent signals requiring equivalent responses. Any of these angles could give you a vocabulary to separate the regulated from the routine — which is probably the upstream thing compliance actually needs to see documented.
