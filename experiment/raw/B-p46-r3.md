I'm a sedimentary petrologist. My work is reading sandstone: figuring out what ancient rivers, winds, and seas were doing from the rock they left behind. The lens I carry everywhere is energy-and-flow — how things move, deposit, and become readable (or don't).

Your scattered recipe corpus looks immediately familiar to me. Let me share four things I notice.

---

**Bioturbation: when later activity mixes the strata, you lose the column**

In stratigraphy, a deposit is a temporal archive — each layer is a frozen event, readable in sequence. The catastrophic failure mode isn't that the record wasn't made; it's *bioturbation*: burrowing organisms (or later floods) mix the layers so the column can't be read anymore. You know something is in there, somewhere, but the sequence is gone.

Your recipe corpus has a complete temporal record — every save was a real depositional event. The problem is that the column is fragmented across five separate rock faces (five substrates) with no correlation. "I made something three weeks ago and can't find it" is classic bioturbated stratigraphy: the event happened, it's in the rock, but you can't read the sequence.

In my field, the answer to a bioturbated outcrop is *correlation* — you match the same event horizon across multiple outcrops to reconstruct the column. A unified index that ties records across substrates by date-cooked or date-saved gives you your column back. You might find it useful to think about what your event horizons actually are — not "where did I save it" but "when did I use it" — because date-of-cooking is a correlatable horizon that cuts across all five substrates simultaneously.

---

**Pore connectivity: you can have a full reservoir and nothing flows to the well**

Petrologists know that a hydrocarbon-bearing sandstone can be useless if the pore network is poorly connected — the resource is there, but the plumbing doesn't let it flow to your well. Permeability isn't obvious from the surface; it's controlled by invisible barriers: cementation, grain packing, heterogeneous diagenesis.

Your archive has full pore networks per substrate but zero connectivity *between* them. Sunday planning is your well — and retrieval pressure drops to near zero because nothing flows across the substrate barriers. Before my field drills more wells, it maps the barriers: which pore throats are cemented shut, which ones are just poorly connected, which zones are high-value but inaccessible.

The diagnostic question I'd ask from my world: which substrate contains your highest-value records (the recipes that actually got made and liked), and which barriers are structural (just disconnected) versus diagenetic (actively decaying — like dead links)? The map comes before the fix, because the fix depends on which kind of barrier you have.

---

**Provenance: every grain already carries its origin story**

Sandstone grains, even after transport and deposition, retain mineralogical fingerprints of their source terrane. A quartz grain from a metamorphic source looks different from one eroded off a granite. Provenance is *already in the record* — it just has to be indexed rather than reconstructed from scratch later.

Your recipes are the same: each one carries visible provenance (grandmother's card, Instagram screenshot, bookmark) that is already there but has never been captured as structured metadata. In my field, provenance workflows are run at the time of sample collection — not after burial, when diagenesis has had time to obscure things. The structural parallel is a minimal capture protocol: a small fixed set of fields added at save-time (source, date saved, date cooked, household reaction) rather than attempted reconstruction during Sunday planning. The grain carries the information; it just needs to be logged before burial obscures it.

---

**Diagenesis: post-burial processes overwrite the original record**

This is the one that concerns me most for your situation. In my field, diagenesis is the suite of chemical changes that happen after a sediment is buried — recrystallization, cementation, dissolution — and it can completely overwrite the original depositional texture. A heavily diagenetically altered sandstone has lost its primary record. You're reading the recrystallized rock, not the original sediment.

Your dead links and paywalled pages are diagenesis. Post-capture processes (site deletions, paywalls, content reorganizations) have overwritten the original record, and the pointer you saved leads nowhere. The petrologist's response to diagenesis risk is primary preservation — making a record that survives alteration, not just a pointer to where the original lives.

In my world this means saving the content itself at capture time, not just the URL. A screenshot or a copied recipe text is a rock cast of the grain before burial; the link alone is a notation of where you *found* the grain, and if the outcrop erodes, the notation is useless. You might find it interesting to consider whether your capture habit distinguishes between a pointer (vulnerable to diagenesis) and a copy (primary preservation).
