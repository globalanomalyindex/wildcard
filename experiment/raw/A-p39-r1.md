I maintain ILS ground stations - the instrument landing systems that guide aircraft down to the runway in low visibility. My world is narrow: keeping localizer and glideslope transmitters radiating a signal precise enough that an aircraft miles out, flying in cloud, follows it to the centerline. The failure I lose sleep over is called Course Structure Error: the transmitter is nominal, the monitor reads nominal, and yet the signal in the physical space where the aircraft actually is has bent. The aircraft flies the bent course. The monitor never saw it.

I looked at your yard, and I kept seeing the same architecture.

---

**1. Your system shows "available" the way my monitor shows "nominal" - both are reading the transmitter, not the field.**

In ILS, the monitor antenna sits close to the transmitter. It catches transmitter faults beautifully. What it cannot see is a course deformation that lives out in the propagation space - a reflection off a hangar, a taxiing 737 refracting the lobe. The aircraft is in that space. The monitor is not.

Your system marks an asset available when it is returned. What it is reading is the return event, not the physical condition of the machine. The inspection gap lives in the space between the return scan and the completed check - exactly where the corruption is. Counter staff read the system (the monitor). Customers receive commitments built on that read. The distortion is real, it lives between the system state and the yard, and it is invisible to anyone who never goes out there.

The ILS answer is a hard architectural rule: the course is not certified by the monitor. It is certified by a flight inspection aircraft that physically flies through the propagation space and measures what the aircraft will actually see. No monitor reading, however clean, substitutes. You might find it useful to ask whether your system has an equivalent - a state that can only be written by a completed physical inspection, never by a return event alone, so the gap is visible rather than masked by a false positive.

---

**2. High-traffic periods don't just overload my system - they degrade the quality of each individual measurement.**

During peak traffic, large aircraft taxiing near the localizer antenna cause transient deformations. This is not just a capacity problem. Each approach conducted during those periods is being guided by a noisier signal. ILS handles this with a defined critical area - a zone that is kept clear of taxiing traffic during low-visibility approaches, because the measurement itself requires protection from the interference that high traffic introduces.

Your Saturday 7-9am window is not just more volume on the same process. It is a known, scheduled event that degrades the signal quality of each individual inspection performed in it - because inspections rushed under that pressure are noisier than unhurried ones, and noisier inspections propagate more errors into the system. The thing worth asking is not only "how do we clear more units in that window" but "what is the minimum valid check sequence that remains signal-clean even under that pressure" - the critical area equivalent, not just a faster version of the full sequence.

---

**3. My aircraft flies the ratio of two signals, not the strength of one - and that is why a single aggregate looks healthy while the course bends.**

An ILS receiver does not track signal strength. It tracks the depth-of-modulation ratio between two overlapping lobes - one at 90 Hz, one at 150 Hz. When the ratio is equal, the aircraft is on course. The failure mode this creates is subtle: if one lobe grows and the other shrinks, the ratio drifts, the course bends, and the aggregate signal power at the monitor shows perfectly fine. You are measuring the wrong thing, and the wrong thing can be healthy while the right thing fails.

Your utilization metric is, I suspect, doing something similar. Aggregate turns per period stays healthy. What is bending underneath it is the ratio of inspection-cleared available to nominally-available within each asset class. Your excavators have accumulated inspection-pending time that the aggregate absorbs without registering as a fault. If you tracked cleared:total separately by asset class, and watched that ratio rather than the aggregate, the course deformation would show. The three excavators sitting for weeks would look like what they are: a lobe out of tolerance, not background noise.

---

**4. The only way to certify a signal is to physically traverse the space it occupies.**

This is not a principle I chose. It is a constraint the physics imposed. No amount of monitor sensitivity, no proxy measurement, no remote telemetry replaces flying the approach. The thing being measured and the measuring instrument must co-locate. Period.

This holds in your yard in a way that might be worth naming plainly: the only input that should flip an asset from "returned" to "available" is a completed physical inspection. Not a return scan, not a timer, not a counter-staff override. The verifying agent must traverse the subject. Everything downstream of that gate - the promise to a customer, the utilization figure, the dispute resolution photo - is only as clean as that gate is strict.
