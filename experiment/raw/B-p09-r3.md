I'm a professor of tropical meteorology. My work is measurement: how to know what a cyclone is actually doing inside a structure you can only partially observe, and how to act on that knowledge before the window closes.

Three things from my field map onto what you described - I'll offer them as optional seeds.

---

**The eyewall vs. the outer bands.**
In a tropical cyclone, most of the system is relatively benign - wide spiral bands of convection that look dramatic from a satellite but aren't where the damage lives. The destructive load concentrates in a narrow annular zone: the eyewall, a tight gradient band that occupies maybe 10-20% of the system's radius. Analysts who instrument the outer bands get a lot of data and miss the story.

In your system, the population of 4,000 tenants has the same structure. The load gradient isn't uniform - a handful of enterprise tenants generating top-of-hour batch exports are your eyewall: a small fraction of the population, a narrow temporal band, and that's where essentially all of the spike damage concentrates. You already know this intuitively, but the measurement implication may be useful: you don't need to fully instrument every tenant to solve the attribution problem. You need to instrument the structural feature - scheduled/batch query patterns - because that's the zone where the gradient lives. A partial instrumentation targeted at the high-load structural signature (e.g., queries above a duration threshold, or queries arriving in a burst within a short window of the hour boundary) would give you attribution at the point of impact before you've solved the full tenant-ID-propagation problem end to end. If that framing is useful, you might find it worth exploring what a "batch pattern detector" would look like that doesn't depend on consistent tenant ID threading everywhere.

---

**Inner-core reconnaissance versus peripheral proxy.**
The most dangerous thing in tropical meteorology is making intensity decisions from peripheral data. Satellite cloud-top temperatures, outer rainband structure, environmental pressure fields - all abundant, all cheap, all systematically misleading about what's actually happening in the eyewall. The only reliable intensity signal is direct inner-core measurement: flying a reconnaissance aircraft into the eyewall, dropping dropsondes, getting a direct reading from inside the structure.

Your current observability is peripheral proxy. Fleet-wide p99, cluster-level latency - these tell you the storm is intensifying, but not where the eyewall is or how fast it's spinning. Aggregate metrics are outer-band readings. The direct inner-core measurement in your architecture would be per-query attribution at the point the query hits the cluster: who asked for it, when, what shape. That's the dropsonde. Without it you're predicting intensity from cloud-top temperatures and wondering why your forecasts keep missing. This doesn't mean you need full tenant-ID propagation everywhere before you can act - reconnaisance meteorologists don't instrument the whole atmosphere before flying in. They target the structure.

---

**The lag that turns manageable storms into surprises.**
There's a known failure mode in cyclone intensity forecasting called rapid intensification: the system deepens much faster than the observation network's re-sampling interval, so the reported intensity at any given moment is actually the intensity from several hours ago. Decisions get made on a reading that is structurally stale - not because the data is wrong, but because the measurement regime wasn't designed to catch a fast-moving inner-core change.

Your system has the same lag structure, but it's architectural rather than physical. When a spike hits, you have no real-time signal of which tenant is responsible. The post-mortem runs after the SLA window has already closed. In tropical meteorology, the answer to rapid intensification is to build the always-on inner-core sampling regime *before* the next storm season, not after the landfall event. The reconnaissance flights happen during quiet periods precisely because the window to act during intensification is too short. You might find it useful to think about tenant attribution instrumentation the same way: the window during an active spike is too narrow to retrofit attribution into the path. The instrumentation needs to be in place during the quiet hours, so the next top-of-hour event is already being tracked in real time as it begins.
