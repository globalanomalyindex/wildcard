Here are four angles from outside the usual frame that might help:

**1. The film archive model: scene detection as a free index**
Film archivists dealing with unlabeled reels first run scene-detection passes to produce a rough index before any human ever watches the footage. The same logic applies to audio: tools like Whisper (open-source, runs locally) treat your audio files as a series of "scenes" and output a time-stamped transcript for each. The inconsistent folder naming becomes irrelevant because the transcript file inherits whatever filename the audio had. A single overnight batch job on your laptop could produce rough transcripts for all 410 episodes without uploading anything, sidestepping the 250 MB cap entirely. For the publisher's three-week deadline, you would only need to clean up the episodes they actually want to quote.

**2. The card catalog model: a shallow index beats no index**
Before full digitization, libraries made card catalogs work by indexing only the metadata, not the full text. For your back catalog, even a one-line description per episode in a plain spreadsheet (guest name, date, rough topics, one memorable phrase) would let guests search 90% of what they actually need. The catalog could be built incrementally by whoever transcribes each episode, so it compounds rather than requiring a single heroic effort. A shared Notion or Airtable table with a public read link would answer most of the "where did I say X" emails without any additional tooling.

**3. The ship-in-a-bottle model: use the constraint as a scope filter**
The 250 MB cap looks like a bottleneck, but it also narrows the problem usefully. A typical compressed podcast episode (128 kbps MP3, 45 minutes) is roughly 40–45 MB. That means you can upload five or six episodes per batch to a transcription API like AssemblyAI or Rev. Rather than trying to solve all 410 episodes at once, the cap naturally enforces a triage discipline: run the publisher's requested episodes first, then proceed in order of guest email frequency. The constraint tells you what actually needs to be done now versus later.

**4. The oral history model: crowdsource timestamps from the people who were there**
Oral history projects discovered that the fastest way to index recordings is to route segments back to participants, because people remember their own words and can locate them almost instantly. Your guests are already emailing you asking where they said things — that is free indexing labor sitting in your inbox. A simple reply template ("I'm building a searchable index — if you remember roughly when in the episode you said X, reply with the minute mark and I'll verify it") turns inbound support requests into inbound catalog contributions. Over time, the most-requested episodes get indexed first, which is probably the right priority anyway.

None of these require committing to a full solution before the publisher deadline. The Whisper batch pass is the most time-efficient move for the three-week window; the card catalog and guest crowdsourcing are slower burns that compound after the deadline passes.
