I'm an industrial hygienist who maps noise dose across factory floors — specifically, occupational hearing conservation for environments where twenty machines run simultaneously, some continuously, some in bursts. My daily obsession: distinguishing which noise events actually accumulate hearing damage from which ones are loud but brief and irrelevant to long-term dose. The binding constraint of my entire field: certain regulatory exposure limits are non-negotiable — I cannot raise those thresholds no matter how many false exceedances they generate, same as you can't raise thresholds on your payment-flow failures.

Four things from my toolkit that seem to genuinely rhyme with your situation:

---

**1. Peak count is a lying proxy for dose.**

In my field, the single most destructive diagnostic mistake is using peak-exceedance counts — how many times did the dosimeter spike above 85 dB? — as if they measure accumulated harm. A pneumatic press that fires every 30 seconds generates hundreds of exceedances per shift but a modest dose; a continuous mid-level hum is boring on peak charts but devastating over years. The switch that changed everything: stop asking "did the level exceed X?" and start asking "is energy integrating in a pattern that predicts harm?"

Your CPU and disk alerts are peak-exceedance counters. The action rate of 10% tells you that exceedance is largely decoupled from the thing you actually care about (payment-path risk). The frame worth trying: for each alert type, instead of "did the value cross the threshold?", ask "does this host's pattern over the last N minutes look like the patterns that historically precede payment-flow events?" — that is, dose rather than peak. You don't have to solve this immediately; the observation is just that the current instrument is measuring the wrong thing.

---

**2. Two populations require two criteria, not one unified threshold — and your law already knows this.**

OSHA's permissible exposure limits operate on exactly the two-population structure you described. Certain exposures are hardcoded to criterion A: non-negotiable, no engineering workaround, mandatory response. Everything else is criterion B: subject to flexible engineering controls, administrative schedules, and tuning. The solution is never to find a single unified threshold that satisfies both — it is to formally separate the populations and give each its own policy.

You already have criterion A: compliance requires payment-flow failures page within five minutes. The problem is that criterion B — everything else — has silently inherited criterion A's threshold policy by default, because nobody drew the line. The CPU and disk alerts on batch hosts are criterion B. They don't need to meet criterion A's response guarantee; they need their own policy, which could include: suppression windows during known batch jobs, slower escalation paths, or much higher thresholds. None of that touches criterion A. The prerequisite is just making the separation explicit.

---

**3. Before you touch a single threshold, establish the exposure map.**

Before I change anything about noise controls in a factory, I run a noise map: which machines are contributing dose, at what times, to which workers? This is the evidence base. Without it, any intervention is a guess.

Your equivalent would be a cross-reference you probably haven't run yet: take every alert that fired in the last quarter and join it against your payment-path outcome telemetry — not just your postmortem records. Postmortems only capture cases where someone took action; they can't tell you whether the alerts that got no response corresponded to real payment degradation. What you want to know: when alert type X fired and nobody responded, did payment-path health degrade anyway? If the answer is almost never, that alert is noise by evidence. If the answer is sometimes, that alert is criterion A in disguise, and you now know it without anyone having to guess. This is the method that empirically answers "which alerts actually protect the regulated paths" — from the data that's already there.

---

**4. Your engineers muting their phones is PPE. PPE appearing means the engineering controls have failed.**

In hearing conservation, the hierarchy runs: eliminate the noise at source, then reduce it, then schedule around it, then — last resort — have workers wear earplugs. When workers start habitually wearing earplugs because nothing upstream has worked, that is a formal audit trigger. The earplugs are a symptom that tells you to go back upstream, not a solution you accept.

Two engineers muting their phones overnight is structurally identical to reaching for earplugs. It is rational individual self-protection in the face of a broken system, and it is a signal — not about those engineers, but about how far upstream the problem has been left unaddressed. The resignation letter makes it explicit. In my field, we track PPE adoption rates as a lagging indicator of whether the engineering controls are working; when the rate goes up, we investigate the source, not the ears.
