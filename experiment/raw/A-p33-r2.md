I work airline irregular-operations recovery - the narrow discipline that kicks in when a mechanical fault or weather event cascades across a network and I have ninety minutes to rebuild the day's schedule before costs compound. My obsession is constraint propagation: every crew assignment is not just a body-to-flight match, it is a node in a graph where duty-time limits, type ratings, base positions, and downstream domino effects all interact simultaneously. A fix that looks legal in isolation can make the recovery unrecoverable an hour later.

Your problem has the same bones. Here is what I notice:

---

**Multi-attribute matching collapses to a proximity sort.** In my world the single costliest error is dispatching an available pilot who lacks the type rating, or has one hour of duty time left, for a four-hour leg. The assignment is locally valid - someone is going - but it breaks two downstream legs when they time out. Your first-come dispatch is doing the same collapse: "nearest available tech" hides the multi-attribute truth that the right tech for a capacitor swap is not the same as the right tech for a control-board replacement, and a van without the specific part is not equipped regardless of how close it is. Airline operations builds a legal-pairings matrix before dispatch opens: for each inbound job, only techs with the matching skill and van inventory are in the candidate set. The proximity sort runs inside that filtered set, not across the whole pool. Whether that prefilter pays off for you depends on how differentiated your job types actually are - but if the 25% callback rate is concentrated in a handful of job classes, the matrix is probably small and cheap to maintain.

---

**Every assignment is also a repositioning decision.** My crew members do not teleport home at midnight. Their last assignment of the day determines where they start tomorrow, and greedy dispatch that ignores terminal position accumulates a geometric repositioning debt - it shows up as extra deadhead flying on day two, and it compounds. Your vans end the day wherever their last call was, across a fifty-mile metro, and start tomorrow from home or depot. A first-come dispatch that scatters them randomly is spending the first hour of every morning repaying that debt as drive time. Airline ops addresses this with a soft constraint on the last leg of each crew segment: the assignment should close the loop toward domicile, not open it further. The equivalent move is asking, for the last one or two jobs of each tech's day, whether a geographically closer-to-home job is available - not as a hard rule, but as a tiebreaker when jobs are otherwise equivalent. The compounding stops without requiring full route optimization.

---

**Re-entrant demand is a capacity leak, not a second job.** In a recovery scenario, an incomplete flight leg that gets re-queued as a new assignment is the most dangerous object in the system - it consumes the same capacity as a fresh leg but it is already costing delay penalties and passenger disruptions upstream. Treating it as a normal new demand item lets it drift to the back of the queue and amplifies the damage. Your callbacks are structurally identical: a second visit is not a new job, it is a failed first job that is now competing with genuine new demand during the surge period when capacity is most constrained. Airlines flag incomplete legs as priority-above-new-demand for re-dispatch. If your callbacks were flagged and held in a separate, elevated queue - assigned as the first job of the closest van with the correct part on board, not queued behind new calls - the capacity leak shrinks, and you get a measurement you currently lack: exactly which job types and which part gaps are generating the drain.

---

**Pre-computation at low-stakes time beats improvisation under pressure.** When recovery is imminent I don't solve from scratch - I pull from a library of pre-validated swap patterns that were stress-tested at 2 a.m. on a quiet night, not during the cascade. The structural property is doing the constraint-checking work before the time pressure arrives. Your parts-matching problem is a version of this: the night before, given tomorrow's scheduled calls and each tech's van inventory, a simple pre-kitting pass could flag which jobs are mismatched and either rebalance the assignments or trigger a parts pull from the parts room. You already know the job types from the booking. The pre-computation is cheap; the improvised callback is expensive. This also gives your parts room its first reliable demand signal - not a count you trust or don't trust, but a forward-looking pull list generated from actual scheduled work.
