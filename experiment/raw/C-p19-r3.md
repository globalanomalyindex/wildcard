Here are four angles from outside the usual pricing/growth frame:

**1. The wrong-unit problem as a translation layer, not a pricing problem**

Linguists talk about "false cognates" — words that look identical across languages but mean different things. Your host-based pricing isn't wrong, it's a false cognate: you say "host," customers hear "request." What if instead of changing the unit, you built a public-facing translation layer? A tiny tool — one page, no branding — that takes "I have X apps making Y requests/day" and outputs "that's roughly N hosts." You're not exposing your unit as unfavorable; you're teaching customers to speak your language before they hit the pricing page. The unit stays, finance is unbothered, and arriving users are already oriented. You might try writing the translation copy first and testing it in onboarding email before building anything.

**2. Onboarding as "soft metering" — the ski rental model**

Ski rental shops discovered that customers who rent first are more likely to buy, and buy correctly, because they've felt the fit. Your trial users churn at the host limit — but what if the trial itself was structured so they naturally encounter the host question before they hit the wall? Ski shops ask "how often do you ski?" before showing you equipment. A two-question onboarding gate — "how many services do you monitor?" and "are they on shared or dedicated infrastructure?" — lets you surface the right tier recommendation before signup, rather than after. This isn't a dark pattern; it's friction that protects the user from under-buying. The churn signal you're getting ("felt tricked") suggests they were never asked.

**3. Treat exit surveys as a product constraint, not a reporting artifact**

Industrial designers use "failure mode and effects analysis" — they examine every known failure to redesign the component, not the warning label. Right now exit surveys are feeding a report. What if they fed a constraint into the product? Specifically: "felt tricked" is the failure mode; the root cause is a mismatch between the mental model at signup and the reality at week one. One design move: show a live host-count indicator during the trial, the way a gas gauge works — not an alarm at empty, but a visible level the whole time. Users who see "you've used 3 of 5 hosts" on day two have the information to self-upgrade rather than churn and blame you. This requires no pricing change and no finance approval.

**4. The "anchor in their unit, close in yours" sales pattern**

Negotiators sometimes use "speaking the other side's language" to build rapport, then gradually reframe toward terms they can actually close on. You can't change the pricing unit, but you can change what gets anchored first. Landing page, calculator, and trial emails could lead entirely in requests-per-month — "designed for teams handling up to 50M requests/month" — and only introduce hosts as a technical implementation detail in the FAQ or setup flow. You're not hiding the unit; you're sequencing it. Customers build desire in their own mental model, then learn the translation once they're motivated. The risk to test is whether "hosts" later feels like a bait-and-switch — that's what a two-sentence explanation ("each host is a server or container your agent monitors; most teams with 50M requests run 2–4 hosts") is designed to preempt.

---

Any of these could be tried as a copy test or a single onboarding screen before touching the pricing page or finance models.
