Here are four angles from outside the usual frame:

**1. The video game skill-tree model — patch the gaps, don't replay the tutorial.**
In games, when you're stuck you don't restart from level one, you grind the specific mechanic blocking you. Your problem isn't that you need a beginner book; it's that you have two isolated skill deficits (bass clef reading, syncopation). You could treat each one as a separate, tiny daily quest: five minutes of bass-clef flashcard drilling (apps like Note Rush let you do this silently on headphones after 8pm), completely detached from playing pieces. The idea is to decouple the reading skill from the performance skill so they can level up on different schedules. You already know the pieces — use them as the reward tier, not the training tier.

**2. The language immersion shortcut — passive exposure before active production.**
When people learn to read a second language, heavy listening before speaking accelerates pattern recognition. Bass clef has the same shape-to-sound mapping problem. One thing you might try: for a week, don't play any bass clef. Just look at it. Take a printed score to breakfast and trace the lines with your finger, naming notes aloud. No keyboard required, no noise. The claim from reading research is that the brain builds the map during low-stakes exposure, and the fingers catch up faster afterward. It costs nothing and fits the 8pm quiet constraint perfectly.

**3. The constraint-as-teacher idea from jazz — syncopation as conversation, not as counting.**
Most people try to fix syncopation by subdividing harder (counting sixteenths). Jazz pedagogues often describe syncopation differently: the off-beat note is the one that is "calling out," and the on-beat silence is the one "listening." If you mentally flip the figure so the rest is the active moment, not the dead moment, your internal timing often settles. You might pick one syncopated measure from something you already know and clap it as a two-person conversation with yourself — right hand clapping the rhythm, left hand clapping back on the silences — before ever touching a key. Costs zero dollars and works fine in a living room.

**4. The "surgery rotation" model from medical training — targeted problem-first, curriculum second.**
Medical residents learn by rotating through the problem, not by reading every chapter before encountering a patient. The beginner-book loop you describe (bored -> quit -> restart) is the opposite structure. One alternative: identify the exact three measures in an existing piece you love where syncopation falls apart, treat those as the entire curriculum for two weeks, and use YouTube slow-down tools or free apps like Musescore to isolate just those bars. The motivational problem with beginner books is that they sequence content for theoretical completeness, not for your specific gap. If the gap is three bars long, the curriculum is three bars long.
