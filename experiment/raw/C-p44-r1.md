Here are four angles from outside the usual frame:

**1. The speedrunner's route — skip the tutorial, grind the hard room**

Speedrunners do something counterintuitive: they practice the hardest section of a game first, in isolation, for hours, before ever playing the game start to finish. Your problem is that beginner books force a linear path through territory you already own. What if you extracted just the two specific problems (bass clef reading, syncopation) and treated each as a tiny, standalone skill sprint? Fifteen minutes of pure bass-clef flashcard drilling — not a piece, just note identification — done nightly for two weeks is different from playing through a graded book hoping you reach the relevant page. You might try something like: write 20 random bass clef notes on index cards, flip through them before you touch the keys. The skill gap is specific; the practice doesn't have to carry the weight of a whole book.

**2. The martial arts kata reversal — learn the form backwards**

In many martial arts traditions, a student who can perform a kata only forward has not learned it — the test is whether they can enter the form at any point and in any direction. Your six memorized pieces may actually be an asset here. Since you already own the finger patterns cold, you could use them as bass clef reading material rather than new pieces. Pick one piece, print the score, and trace only the left-hand staff while the right hand does its remembered thing. You're reading notation you already know motorically, which removes performance anxiety and focuses your attention on the reading itself. The piece becomes a reading exercise rather than something to be "played."

**3. The 8pm constraint as a design prompt, not an obstacle**

Industrial designers sometimes say that a tight constraint — a weight limit, a material restriction — is a gift, because it eliminates a huge search space and forces creative specificity. Your silent-after-8pm window is actually pointing at a category of practice that piano pedagogy undervalues: mental practice. Sports science has documented that pianists who vividly imagine playing a passage — hearing it internally, feeling the finger movements — show measurable retention gains comparable to physical practice. You have the living room, a keyboard you cannot touch loudly, and a problem that is partly perceptual (reading bass clef). After the kid is down, fifteen minutes with a score and a pencil, hearing the left hand in your head and tracing the fingering, might convert time that currently goes to nothing into genuine practice.

**4. The foreign-language immersion shortcut — comprehensible input slightly above your level**

Stephen Krashen's theory of language acquisition holds that you acquire fluency not from grammar drills but from massive exposure to input that is just slightly beyond your current level — you understand most of it, but there is a small edge of novelty. Beginner piano books fail you because they are far below your level, so there is no acquisition happening. Syncopation collapses because you encounter it rarely and only in formal exercises. One thing to try: find YouTube transcriptions of a single song you love that has natural syncopation (a lot of pop, jazz, and soul piano has this), something simple enough that you can nearly play it but not quite. Learn only that song, badly and slowly, for a month. The rhythmic pattern you need will appear hundreds of times in a context you care about, which is closer to how immersion works than a graded exercise is.
