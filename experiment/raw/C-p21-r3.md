Here are four angles from outside the usual frame:

**1. Subtitle-track archaeology (from broadcast captioning practice)**
Television archives face the same problem at scale: thousands of hours, no transcripts, deadline pressure. The workflow broadcasters use is to treat the audio as a byproduct of time, not content — run a batch speech-to-text pass not to produce polished transcripts but to generate raw SRT subtitle files, which are essentially timestamped word lists. The "accuracy" bar is lower than you think for this purpose: even a rough whisper.cpp or AssemblyAI pass at 70-80% word accuracy gives you a searchable index where a human can jump to the approximate timestamp and verify the exact quote in ten seconds rather than scrubbing an entire episode. For the publisher's need specifically, you'd be producing "locator files," not transcripts — a distinct, lighter deliverable. You might try: batch-process everything offline with Whisper (runs locally, no upload size limit, no hosting cost) and treat the output as a timestamp map rather than a finished document.

**2. Archivist triage logic (from special collections librarianship)**
When a university archive gets a large donation of unprocessed materials with an imminent access request, they use a technique called minimal-level processing or "More Product, Less Process" — the principle that a rough finding aid covering everything is more useful than a perfect catalog covering 10% of the collection. Applied here: instead of transcribing 410 episodes, you could create a one-sentence "episode summary + notable quote" record for each episode using the audio's first and last three minutes plus any show notes or guest emails you have. The publisher wants specific quotes — most of those quotes probably came from memorable moments a four-year host can locate quickly by memory or guest name. A light metadata layer (guest, date, rough topic, one pull-quote) built episode by episode in a spreadsheet might surface 80% of what the publisher needs in a fraction of the transcription time. You might try: spend one evening building a sparse but complete index before touching any audio.

**3. Mesh network search (from P2P indexing)**
The inconsistent folder naming across two drives is actually a common data-recovery scenario, not a podcast-specific one. File recovery and digital forensics tools treat folder structure as irrelevant — they index by content fingerprint rather than path. The equivalent here is to run a single recursive ingest regardless of naming convention, tagging files by actual audio duration and creation timestamp rather than filename. Tools like Whisper's batch CLI or simple scripts with ffprobe can extract duration, creation date, and a first-pass transcript without caring what the file is named. The insight is: the chaos in the folder structure only matters if you navigate it manually. An automated pass that ignores the structure entirely collapses the "inconsistent naming" problem to zero. You might try: write or find a one-liner that recursively finds all .mp3/.wav files on both drives and outputs a manifest (path, duration, file-modified date) before doing anything else — that manifest becomes your ground truth.

**4. The "clip reel" framing from oral history curation**
Oral history projects — the kind that collect interviews with aging veterans or civil rights witnesses — have a practice of building a "highlights reel manifest" before full transcription: researchers listen at 2-3x speed and log only the segments that contain quotable material, with in/out timecodes. The full transcript comes later (or never). For a publisher licensing quotes, this is actually the right deliverable: they need exact wording and timestamps for a finite set of notable moments, not full transcripts of 410 hours. If you could identify the 30-40 episodes most likely to contain licensable quotes (your most prominent guests, your highest-download episodes, any episodes you already remember producing a strong exchange), you could apply focused human review at 2x speed to produce a short-list manifest in a weekend. You might try: ask the publisher to share whatever search terms or themes they're interested in, then use that to pre-filter which episodes to process rather than working chronologically through everything.
