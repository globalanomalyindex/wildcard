A Quaternary sedimentologist steps in — someone who cores lake beds to read environmental history layer by layer, and whose main professional anxiety is a record that *looks* intact but has been quietly corrupted before you ever pull it out.

---

**1. Every grain needs a source signature**

In my field, the most important thing about a sediment grain is not what it's made of but *where it came from*. We call this provenance. Without provenance you can't correlate layers across different sections of the same core, and you can't tell an in-situ deposit from a reworked one. A grain without a source signature is analytically inert — it's in the record but you can't do anything with it.

What I notice in your collection: items enter from five distinct "depositional environments" (bookmarks, Instagram, screenshots, notes, handwritten cards), but provenance is stripped at capture. Every recipe lands in the pile without its source signature — not just *where* it came from, but *when* you actually made it, whether it passed the family test, and whether it needs a gluten check. Later, all you have is an undifferentiated mass. 

In stratigraphy we've learned that source-environment tagging at capture is non-negotiable — you cannot reconstruct it reliably from the grain alone after the fact. If your collection had even a lightweight capture habit (source channel, date tried, "family liked it," "needs GF sub"), each item would carry its own correlation key. You might find it useful to think about what the minimum source signature would be for your purposes — not a full database, just the three or four fields that would let Sunday-you actually find and filter what Monday-you captured.

---

**2. A collection without a core cannot answer recency queries**

A scattered sediment deposit is interesting geology but useless paleoclimatology. The record only becomes readable when you can core it — pull a continuous vertical sample that preserves time order. Without the core, you can't answer "what was the climate three weeks ago?" no matter how good the material is.

Your problem of the unfindable "thing I made three weeks ago that everyone liked" is structurally this: you have deposition but no core. The material is there. There is no continuous index that preserves time-order across silos, so recency queries fail. The items themselves didn't disappear — the retrieval structure was never built.

What transfers from my work: in practice we don't build cores retroactively from scattered samples; we commit to a coring strategy *before* deposition continues. For you the equivalent is probably a single canonical location where anything tested gets a one-line entry (date, name, verdict, where the full recipe lives) — not to replace your existing silos, but to serve as the core that makes those silos queryable. The full recipe can stay wherever it is; the index just needs to know it existed and when.

---

**3. Reworked grains look valid until the moment they fail you**

This one I think about a lot. A reworked grain — one that was deposited, eroded, and redeposited — looks identical to a primary deposit in the core. It will pass a quick visual inspection. The failure mode is: you base an interpretation on it, and only when you try to date it or trace its chemistry do you find it's an artifact. By then you've wasted analysis time, and if you trusted it, you may have published a wrong conclusion.

Your dead links are reworked grains. Half your saved bookmarks now hit paywalls or deleted pages — they look like valid entries in the collection, but at the moment you actually need them (Sunday planning, 6pm, family waiting) they return nothing. The structural parallel: the artifact is indistinguishable from the real thing until you try to use it under pressure.

In my field the defense is to check provenance anomalies *at the time of analysis*, not at the time of retrieval — and increasingly we do rapid screening at sample preparation rather than discovering the problem mid-interpretation. For recipes the analogue is doing the verification work at capture (or shortly after), not at retrieval. A saved link that gets a quick check and a local copy of the key information — even just the ingredient list pasted into a note — is no longer a reworked grain. It's primary material. That doesn't fix the existing collection, but it changes the failure rate on everything going forward.
