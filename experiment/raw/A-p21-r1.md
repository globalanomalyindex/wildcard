I date inks for a living — not handwriting analysis, but the narrower problem of extracting a reliable age signal from a document that has been sitting in some drawer, in some humidity, under some light, for an unknown stretch of time. Everything I do is a reconstruction: the document arrived as it is now, and I have to work backward to when it was made. My daily obsession is separating the genuine temporal signal from the noise that storage conditions lay on top of it, and I fear most the masking artifact — the decay curve that looks clean and reliable but is tracking the wrong variable.

Looking at your problem from that position, three things genuinely rhyme.

---

**The corpus is not your evidence — the derived layer is.** In my field, I never treat the document itself as the evidence I'll present. The evidence is what the document *leaves behind* when I run it through my instruments: emission spectra, migration profiles, degradation products. The primary record and the useful artifact are decoupled, and the artifact is what gets indexed and queried. Your audio files are in exactly this position: the audio is the primary record, but the useful artifact for the publisher is what the audio leaves behind when transcribed — a timestamped text layer. The structural move in my field is that I don't re-examine the whole corpus every time a query arrives; I examine the derived artifact. For your 3-week window, the transcript layer is the thing to build, and it can be built file-by-file, independently, without touching your master audio or your hosting plan at all. Nothing about local transcription requires an upload.

---

**Triage by provenance before you examine anything.** I never start an aging analysis at random. I work from what I know about a document's custody history first: who had it, where it was stored, what period is even plausible. That constrains the examination scope before I spend time on any instrument. You have provenance evidence already sitting in your inbox — the guest emails asking where they said a particular thing are pointing at specific episodes. The publisher presumably has candidate quotes or episodes too. In my practice, the custody trail narrows the case before costly work begins; your existing correspondence is that trail. Starting the transcription pipeline at the locus those emails point to — rather than at episode 1 of 410 — is the same move. The license deadline becomes more tractable if the scope is the publisher's actual candidate set, not the full catalog.

---

**The inconsistent naming structure is a storage-noise confound — reconcile provenance first, then measure.** In ink dating, storage conditions introduce variation that rides on top of the age signal and can look like genuine data if you trust them uncritically. The naming inconsistency across your two drives is structurally the same problem: different schemes applied to the same underlying corpus, so you cannot safely assume the folder path tells you anything reliable about episode identity, date, or sequence. Before running any automated transcription pipeline across the whole archive, the move I'd make in my work is to build a reconciliation layer first — a manifest that re-establishes what each file actually is, using the surviving signals you can trust (audio length, embedded metadata, any consistent element in the filename) rather than the folder structure. That manifest becomes the canonical index that every downstream tool reads from, so you're not re-solving the provenance problem every time you need a file.

One corollary worth noting: if you do break files into segments to stay under the 250 MB hosting cap, do it on copies and keep the original file's start time as the canonical timestamp reference. A transcript timestamp relative to a segment start is unreproducible against the master recording — which is exactly the kind of masking artifact that causes trouble later when someone tries to verify a quote.
