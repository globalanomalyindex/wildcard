A thermodynamics professor walks in - someone who thinks daily about how energy flows, why it flows, and what stops it. Here are three things I notice from that vantage point.

---

**1. Your alert channel has no gradient, so flow has no direction.**

In thermodynamics, temperature doesn't move energy - *differences* in temperature do. Collapse the gradient and heat stops flowing entirely; you get a system at uniform temperature with zero capacity to do useful work. What I notice in your setup is that all thirty alerts arrive in the same channel at the same apparent urgency - they look identical from the perspective of the person whose phone is buzzing at 2am. The regulated payment-failure alerts and the batch-job CPU spike carry the same weight. There's no gradient expressed, so there's no mechanism for differential routing. The person receiving the page can't flow toward the important ones any faster than toward the noise.

The structural match: a gradient must be made visible before directed flow is possible. If you could restore the criticality gradient - even a simple two-tier separation so that regulated-path failures land in one channel and threshold-check noise lands in another - you'd have a system capable of doing work again. Not because the alerts changed, but because the gradient is now expressed and routing can follow it. Your call entirely on how to implement that, but I'd look at whether the gradient exists in your alert metadata (it probably does somewhere) and just isn't being surfaced to the people on call.

---

**2. Your aggregate statistic is concealing bimodal structure.**

Temperature is a statistical aggregate - it averages over the kinetic energies of billions of molecules, and two gases at the same temperature can have wildly different per-molecule distributions. The average masks the microstate. "30 alerts per week, 10% actionable" is doing the same thing: it's an aggregate that may be masking a bimodal population underneath. Some alert types are probably close to 0% actionable (the batch-job CPU checks you already identified); others are probably close to 80% or 100% actionable. But you're working from the population mean, which obscures that structure entirely.

The method that transfers: decompose the aggregate into per-alert-type actionability rates. Your postmortem records already contain this microstate - which specific alert IDs fired, and which ones generated action. Read that distribution out, not just the mean. My strong prior is that you'll find a small cluster of alert types that are reliably actionable and a larger cluster that are nearly always noise. Once that structure is visible, you can act on it surgically rather than trying to tune the whole population at once.

---

**3. Your thresholds aren't set relative to the noise floor - they're set in absolute terms.**

In electronics, thermal noise (Johnson-Nyquist noise) sets a floor below which no signal can be reliably distinguished. Every resistor at nonzero temperature generates random fluctuations, and if your signal threshold is near that floor, you'll trigger on the noise constantly. The right move isn't to pick an arbitrary absolute threshold - it's to characterize the noise floor first and set your detection threshold above it.

Your batch jobs are thermal activity: routine, non-pathological energy running through the system on a schedule. The CPU and disk thresholds were likely calibrated without a model of what the system looks like during normal batch-job operation - so those thresholds sit near or inside the noise floor of regular activity. Every batch run looks like a signal, because the threshold doesn't know what "background" looks like.

The transferable method: rather than raising thresholds globally (which you can't do anyway without risking the regulated paths), characterize the distribution of CPU and disk *specifically during known batch windows* - that's your noise floor. Then apply time-gated thresholds that are elevated during those windows and return to sensitive defaults outside them. This is narrower and more defensible than a blanket raise: it doesn't touch the sensitivity on your regulated paths, it just teaches the alert what background looks like when background is noisy.
