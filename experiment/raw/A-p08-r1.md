**Noticing 1 — model the noise floor before you hunt for signal**

In a degraded recording, the first thing I do is not search for the contested event — it's characterize the *structured* noise sources and model them out explicitly. An HVAC compressor, a crowd cheer, a car passing — these are not random noise, they are periodic, predictable, *characterizable* signals with their own time-frequency signature. I build that model first, as its own thing, separate from any detection task. Then what doesn't fit the baseline model is worth looking at.

The mapping: your CPU and disk threshold alerts during batch jobs are not random noise. They are a known, periodic, characterizable event class with a time signature — the batch schedule. They behave the way an HVAC compressor behaves in my recordings: recurring, predictable, and occupying the same channel as the events that matter. The move I'd suggest borrowing isn't tuning thresholds at all — it's building a forward model of the batch-correlated alert pattern (which hosts, which metrics, which time windows, what amplitude envelope) so that any alert *outside* that model stands out structurally. You haven't changed a threshold; you've separated the noise floor from the signal band. If that's useful to you, the immediate question is: do you have enough postmortem data to characterize the batch pattern empirically, or do you need to instrument it fresh?

**Noticing 2 — you can't raise the global threshold, so stratify the source types into separate channels**

The argument I face most often from defense attorneys is exactly the one you're under: "you can't raise the threshold without losing the real shots." They're right that I can't raise it *globally*. My answer is never to raise the global threshold — it's to stop treating all impulsive events as one class. I build **separate detection channels for separate source types**, each with its own criterion calibrated to that source's characteristics. A car backfire and a muzzle blast are not the same class; they shouldn't share a detection rule.

The mapping: your compliance-critical payment-flow alerts and your batch-job-correlated infrastructure alerts are not the same source class. They shouldn't share a threshold policy. The constraint "cannot raise thresholds across the board" dissolves the moment you stop treating the board as one surface. The question becomes: can you classify each current alert by source type — is this alert *capable* of being triggered by a batch job, or can it only fire when a payment flow anomaly occurs? Once you've done that classification, you can tune aggressively on the first class without touching the second. The classification doesn't require changing any thresholds; it's a causal sort.

**Noticing 3 — provenance tracing: the causal audit before any detection decision**

When I'm handed a recording I know nothing about, and I can't characterize the noise sources directly because I have no clean reference, I do provenance tracing. I work backwards: what was happening at that location at that time? Is 11:43 PM when the HVAC compressor kicks on? I check the maintenance log. I'm not analyzing waveforms yet — I'm building the causal map of what *could* have fired and what *couldn't* have. That map is the prior, and without it any threshold decision is guesswork with authority attached to it.

The mapping: the problem you described — "nobody can confidently say which alerts actually protect the regulated paths" — is the absence of that causal map. Your team is analyzing waveforms without a maintenance log. The forensic move here is to do the provenance work as a one-time audit before any tuning decision: for each alert type, trace it backward to its triggering mechanism and ask whether that mechanism can be activated by a routine batch job. If yes, it belongs in the "characterizable noise" class. If the only trigger is a genuine payment-flow failure, it belongs in the "protected signal" class. You end up with the very thing compliance needs — a written causal trace from alert to regulated path — and you end up with it as a byproduct of the work you'd have to do anyway to tune intelligently. The engineers who've been doing postmortems already have fragments of this map; it may be closer to recoverable than it looks.
