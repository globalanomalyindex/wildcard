Here are four angles from outside the usual HVAC dispatch frame that might be worth turning over:

**1. Ambulance system demand forecasting**

Emergency medical services discovered decades ago that "respond when called" burns resources and kills response times. The fix was predictive positioning: park units near where calls statistically cluster before the calls come in, using time-of-day and neighborhood heat maps. Your version would be simpler. You already have years of service records by address, equipment age, and season. You could plot a heat map of where summer AC failures concentrate and pre-stage a tech there each morning, loaded with the five capacitors that account for 60% of your no-parts returns. The first-come dispatch rule is the assumption worth questioning - it treats all locations as equally likely, when they are not.

**2. Fishing vessel "set and wait" versus constant trolling**

Offshore captains learned that constantly moving to find fish burns fuel and catches less. The better pattern is to read water temperature and current charts, commit to a spot, and let fish come to you. The analogy for your best installer: he might accomplish more if he anchored in a commercial corridor or apartment complex for a full day, batching all nearby calls to him, rather than being routed across town. The driving is not incidental to dispatch - it is what dispatch is currently optimizing for by accident.

**3. Hospital pharmacy par-level stocking on nursing floors**

Large hospitals solved a version of your parts problem by not keeping inventory in one central room. Instead, each nursing floor has a small cabinet stocked to a par level - only the 30 items that floor uses most. A central system tops it up each night. Nobody counts; the system just restores par. Each of your nine vans could carry a personalized par kit of the 10 parts that tech's route and typical call mix actually uses - not a generic list, but derived from their last two years of completed work orders. The second-visit rate is almost entirely a parts-prediction problem in disguise.

**4. The Missoula smokejumper crew rotation model**

Wildland fire crews rotate through positions based on fatigue and skill match, not seniority or who showed up first. A crew boss assigns the skilled climbers to the steep ground and reserves generalists for the perimeter. Your best installer is being used as a delivery vehicle. One reframe: designate him as a "complex call only" tech who never takes a standard tune-up or filter swap. Less-experienced techs handle the volume; he handles the jobs that actually require his skill. His driving drops because his call list shrinks to the work only he can close in one visit.
