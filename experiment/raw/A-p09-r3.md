I'm a transportation engineer — specifically, I spent twenty years on arterial signal coordination: the problem of timing traffic signals along a corridor so that a platoon of vehicles released at one intersection arrives at the next one on green. The central obsession of this work is that **periodicity is information, not just a nuisance** — and uncoordinated periodic actors always produce synchronized contention, not as a failure of any individual actor but as a structural consequence of the absence of cross-actor timing authority.

Looking at your system through that lens, four things genuinely rhyme.

---

**1. Your contention is a desynchronization problem, not just a throttling problem.**

In my field: when independent signals run on their own cycle with no offset between them, platoons from adjacent streets arrive simultaneously at the same intersection. No individual signal is misbehaving. The pile-up is a structural consequence of having no cross-signal timing authority. We call this "negative progression" — the uncoordinated state.

The mapping: your four enterprise tenants all fire at :00 because that schedule is individually rational for each of them. There is no cross-tenant timing authority requiring offset. The p99 spike is not evidence that anyone is abusing the system; it is evidence that nobody is offsetting them. A throttle addresses the symptom; desynchronization addresses the cause. You might find it worth negotiating (or writing into large-tenant contracts going forward) a staggered export-start window — not to slow anyone down, but to phase-shift their cycles by 10-15 minutes so the cluster sees a distributed ramp rather than a simultaneous wall.

---

**2. Scheduled periodic load is an asset for pre-admission, not only a liability.**

In my field: a green wave works because arrival is predictable. The coordination is not about reacting faster — it is about knowing *when* the platoon will arrive and pre-clearing the path in advance. Predictability is the lever; the period is the input.

The mapping: your burst is not random — it is scheduled, clockwork, same shape every hour. That periodicity is actionable information. In my world the move would be: use the known burst window as the coordination anchor. Accept that the :00 window will be a constrained lane for non-scheduled tenants (or pre-queue export jobs into a rolling 10-minute smoothing window before :00), rather than treating every hour as a surprise. Interactive-query SLAs might actually be easier to protect if you distinguish "inside the known burst window" from "outside it" — the SLA clock behaves differently in those two states. Your call entirely on whether that distinction is worth building into your contracts or your runbooks.

---

**3. Saturation flow rate is the ceiling; the only lever is how you allocate green time across competing movements.**

In my field: each intersection has a maximum throughput per cycle, and it does not move. The only thing a signal engineer controls is how green time is distributed across competing traffic movements — through traffic, left turns, pedestrians. When one movement goes unbudgeted, it eats time that was implicitly allocated to others.

The mapping: your ClickHouse cluster has a hard query-concurrency ceiling. Right now, scheduled export queries and interactive queries compete for the same slots with no differentiated allocation — so the enterprise export movement simply consumes whatever it needs, and interactive queries absorb the remainder (which is why your p99 doubles). This does not require full per-tenant attribution to fix at a first pass. A coarser partition — a dedicated concurrency budget for "export-class queries" vs. "interactive-class queries," distinguished by a query tag or a request header — would protect the interactive lane even if you cannot yet attribute precisely which tenant is in the export lane. Green time allocation, in other words, can precede perfect traffic counting.

---

**4. The upstream occupancy probe is what gives you lead time before saturation — and you can run it before per-tenant attribution is complete.**

In my field: adaptive signal systems (the ones that adjust offsets in real time) do not wait for the intersection to jam. They measure queue occupancy upstream — a loop detector 200 meters back — and adjust the downstream timing before the platoon arrives. The probe is upstream of the bottleneck, and it is coarser than a full count: it detects *that a wave is forming*, not the identity of every vehicle in it.

The mapping: your tenant-ID attribution problem means the fine-grained probe is broken. But a coarser upstream signal is available right now: query queue depth at the API gateway, before requests reach ClickHouse. That signal does not require per-tenant attribution — it just tells you that a wave is forming. An admission-control gate at the API layer, triggered by upstream queue depth exceeding a threshold, could shed load before the cluster saturates — even before you have completed the attribution instrumentation work. The full attribution effort is still worth doing (it is the prerequisite for fine-grained per-tenant limits and SLA accounting), but the upstream occupancy probe gives you a blunt instrument to stop the bleeding in the meantime.
