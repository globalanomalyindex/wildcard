A geomorphologist who studies slope dynamics and mass-movement timing here. My whole field is the gap between the event everyone points to and the condition that actually caused it. Let me show you what I see.

---

**1. Your summer surge is not the cause - it's the trigger landing on an already-loaded slope.**

In landslide science, a slope accumulates stress gradually and invisibly - rainfall seeping in over weeks, slow soil creep, micro-fracturing that no one measures. Then a rainstorm that would have been unremarkable on a healthy slope tips it. Everyone blames the rain. But the collapse was set by the accumulated condition, not the trigger.

Your 5-day summer queue looks like a demand spike, but the structural mechanics are the same: the fragility was building all year - FIFO routing that doesn't cluster spatially, van inventories nobody tracks, a 25% first-visit failure rate bleeding capacity into second trips. Summer heat is just the rainstorm that finally tips it. The wait you're fighting in July was loaded in April.

In slope stabilization, the intervention that matters is *pre-season drainage* - reducing stored pore pressure before the trigger season arrives, so the slope is in a stable state when the rain comes. You might find it interesting to ask what the equivalent pre-season drainage move looks like in your operation - what fragility could be discharged before June so that summer demand lands on a stable system rather than a loaded one.

---

**2. Your best installer is the steep slope with weak substrate - he fails first, and failure propagates.**

Geomorphologists know that in heterogeneous terrain, the steepest slope with the most fractured substrate fails first. In a human system this is the high-throughput node under the most stress. Your best installer is that slope: highest value, but FIFO routing exposes him to the same cross-city travel as everyone else, which means his capacity is the first to erode when routing is inefficient. When he spends half his day driving, his afternoon queue slips, those customers reschedule into tomorrow, and the next-day queue is already harder before anything new arrives.

In slope engineering the move is *selective reinforcement* - you don't reinforce all slopes equally; you identify which failure first cascades and you protect that node preferentially. Whether that's worth doing in your scheduling is entirely your call, but the structural question it raises: does your dispatch have any mechanism to protect your highest-leverage person's time, or does FIFO treat all nine nodes as equivalent?

---

**3. A parts room with no count is a slope with no deformation instruments - you're reading the failure, not the approach.**

In pre-failure monitoring, the signal we trust is not absolute displacement but *creep rate*: how fast is the slope moving, and is that rate accelerating? A slope that moved 2mm last month and 8mm this month is far more alarming than one that has moved 50mm uniformly over five years. The rate of change is the stability margin.

Your 25% second-visit rate is a displacement reading, but you can't tell if it's accelerating because you have no inventory count - no instrument. You're learning about the failure after it happens, not tracking the signal that would let you intervene before it does. The equivalent of the deformation instrument here is not complex: it's a per-van, per-part count that's updated at job close. Not because inventory management is interesting, but because *rate-tracking* - is this van's capacitor gap getting worse or better month over month? - is what gives you the early-warning window that slope collapse takes away when there are no instruments.

---

**4. The system has hysteresis: it doesn't spring back when summer load eases.**

A slope that has reached near-critical state doesn't return to its pre-loaded condition when the rain stops. The water is still in the soil. The micro-fractures are still open. It has a *memory* of its loaded state, and the next trigger arrives onto a slope that hasn't recovered.

Your operation shows the same thing: once the summer queue builds, reducing call volume by 10% doesn't clear it. Techs are already cross-town. Van inventories are already picked through on the commonly-needed parts. Customers from last week are still waiting. The system remembers its loaded state, and the queue accumulates interest. This is the structural reason why a correction you might expect to work fast (adding one more call per tech per day) doesn't discharge the backlog - the state of the system, not just the flow through it, has to change before the queue drains. In slope terms, you'd need to drain the pore pressure, not just stop the rain.
