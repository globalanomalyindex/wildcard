I study stress in mountain hares without touching them. My material is fecal pellets — I extract glucocorticoid hormones from scat and read the animal's stress history from what it left on the hillside. The lens I work from is the material itself: a pellet is not a neutral carrier. Its age, its moisture exposure, its freeze-thaw history all change the hormone ratios I measure, and if I don't account for that degradation gradient, I'll misread the animal. The craft is partitioning variance correctly — knowing which differences belong to the hare's physiology and which belong to the substrate.

Three things from that practice genuinely rhyme with your situation.

---

**The substrate has a signal gradient; read it before you compress.**

In my field, scat collected within an hour of deposit gives a different ratio of cortisol metabolites than scat that sat overnight in rain. Both pellets look like pellets. But one carries a clean signal and one carries a degraded signal, and treating them as equivalent is where most of the bad readings come from. Before I run any assay, I characterize what I have: fresh, aged, partially degraded. That characterization is the first step, not an afterthought.

Your 4-6 hours of raw footage is not uniform substrate either. Within that session there are high-signal moments — the one where your hand hesitated on a tricky cut, the moment the joint seated, the point where you had to improvise — and low-signal moments that are just elapsed time. If the editing workflow starts from the timeline's beginning and works forward, the compression decisions are being made before the signal gradient has been read. The transfer: before opening the timeline, do one fast pass that isn't editing — just mapping where the session's energy is. Mark the moments that carry tension, decision, or reveal, regardless of when they happened chronologically. That map becomes the compression guide. You might find the hook for your opening 45 seconds is sitting 90 minutes into the footage.

---

**Variance you can't explain usually has a matrix variable you haven't isolated.**

When I see an unexpected spike in cortisol readings across a cohort, my first instinct is not "the hares are more stressed." My first instinct is to look for a matrix variable — a weather event, a change in field technician, a different freezer used for storage — that changed beneath the biological signal. Most unexplained variance in my data turns out to belong to the substrate, not the organism.

You have a five-video impression drop and three candidate explanations: thumbnail, title, topic. But those three changed together, and there may be a matrix variable beneath them — something that shifted in your upload cadence, your day-of-week timing, or simply the algorithmic landscape for your topic category over those same weeks — that's carrying most of the variance you're seeing. The diagnostic move in my field is to find the thing that is most like a background condition (not a deliberate choice) before attributing the drop to the intentional levers. If you can identify any background variable that changed over those five videos — even something like a shift in when you post — isolating it first gives you a cleaner read on whether thumbnail or title or topic is actually doing work.

---

**A degraded sample still contains the hormone; you just need the right extraction step.**

Sometimes a pellet comes in that's been rained on for 48 hours. Standard enzyme immunoassay reads it as low signal. But the glucocorticoid is still there — just conjugated in a form the standard protocol doesn't reach. Adding an enzymatic hydrolysis step before the assay recovers the signal from material that would otherwise look like noise. The molecule was there the whole time; the extraction protocol wasn't designed to find it.

The 30% abandonment in your first 45 seconds is being treated as a hook problem — which implies you need better opening footage. But your 4-6 hours of raw material almost certainly contains the high-value hook signal somewhere inside it; the question is whether your editing process is set up to extract it from wherever it actually lives, or whether it defaults to the chronological opening as the only candidate. The moment of highest viewer tension in a woodworking video is almost never "here's what I'm building today" — it tends to be the visible problem, the near-miss, the thing that could have gone wrong. Those moments exist in your footage. The extraction-protocol question is whether your cut prioritizes finding them across the whole 4-6 hours and surfacing them first, treating time-of-filming as irrelevant to placement.
