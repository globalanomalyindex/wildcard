---

I'm a physicist who studies indirect dark matter detection - specifically, I work on interpreting anomalous signals in astrophysical data and distinguishing genuine dark matter signatures from instrumental artifacts and conventional astrophysical sources. My obsession is the confounded observation: a signal that might be real, or might be three other things wearing the same coat. My whole practice is designing observations that isolate one explanation at a time.

Three things from that work seem to genuinely rhyme with what you're describing.

---

**The confounded observation problem**

In my field, when an anomalous observation has multiple plausible candidate causes - dark matter, modified gravity, unresolved baryonic mass - no amount of staring at that single observation makes progress. The only move that separates the candidates is designing the *next observation* to be differentially sensitive to exactly one of them: hold everything else constant and vary the suspect. You don't conclude "dark matter" from an ambiguous reading. You engineer the test so the reading is unambiguous about one hypothesis.

You have three suspected causes for the impression decline - thumbnail, title, topic - and they've been varying simultaneously across five videos, so the observation is fully confounded. In my world, the move would be to hold two constant for a deliberate test window and vary the third alone. A run of three or four videos with identical topic and title treatment but varied thumbnail, then a run varying only title, gives you a reading that is actually diagnostic rather than ambiguous. It won't feel efficient, and it won't feel like your most creative period - but it's the only observation design that separates the signal. Whether that trade is worth it is entirely your call; I just notice the confound structure is the same one we fight constantly.

---

**Modeling the background instead of auditing each frame**

Dark matter detectors operate in a constant soup of background noise: cosmic ray muons, radioactive decay in the detector walls, neutrino interactions. You can't silence the background. The entire experimental architecture is built not to eliminate it but to *model it* - to characterize what background events look like with enough precision that any deviation from the model stands out as signal. The detector isn't asking "is this a good event?" about each flash of light. It's asking "does this event deviate from my background model?"

Your 4-6 hours of footage is dominated by what I'd call background: the walk to the bench, the tool setup, the glue cure waiting, the second take on a measurement. You can't eliminate it - it's just what shooting in a garage produces. But I notice my field would approach that pile differently: build a crisp taxonomy of what "background footage" looks like in your specific workflow (the classes of shots that are always cut, the durations that are always too long), and then recognition becomes the task rather than evaluation. The cut happens on contact with a known class rather than at the end of a deliberative watch-through. Whether that speeds up your assembly pass is yours to find out; it's just the posture I'd bring to a background-dominated dataset.

---

**Reading the shape of the curve, not just the endpoint**

When we see an anomalous signal in a gravitational lensing observation, we don't just record that an anomaly exists - we read its *geometry*. The spatial shape and curvature of a distortion discriminates between cause types: a dark matter halo and a clump of ordinary baryonic matter produce differently shaped distortions even when they produce the same total deflection. The shape encodes the cause.

Your retention graph presumably shows not just "30% left in the first 45 seconds" but the specific second or five-second bin where the drop is steepest. That shape is diagnostic in the same structural way. A cliff that begins at second 3-8 points at expectation mismatch - the thumbnail or title promised something the opening image didn't confirm. A cliff that starts at second 15-25 points more at pacing or intro structure - the viewer is present but the material isn't holding them yet. Reading where inside the window the slope is steepest gives you a different kind of information than the endpoint alone. The geometry of the loss curve may already be telling you which part of the 45 seconds is doing the work.
