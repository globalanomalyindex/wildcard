Here are four angles from outside the usual frame that might be worth considering:

**1. Power-grid demand-response contracts**

Utilities do not try to eliminate peak demand — they pay large consumers to shift it. They run "interruptible tariff" programs where heavy users agree, contractually, to move load on request in exchange for rate discounts. What you noticed: your enterprise tenants already have contracts, and those contracts only specify latency SLAs, not scheduling rights. The connection: you could offer a small fee credit or expanded API quota in off-peak windows in exchange for those tenants agreeing to stagger their exports — 2am instead of the top of the hour. You sidestep the budget problem entirely because the lever is commercial, not infrastructure. What to try: draft a one-page "cooperative scheduling" addendum and float it with your two SLA-violating customers before engineering touches anything.

**2. Ambulance-dispatch preemption logic**

Emergency dispatch systems do not queue ambulances fairly with civilian traffic — they broadcast a signal that causes other vehicles to yield. The insight is that preemption is not the same as isolation. What you noticed: your tenant IDs are inconsistently threaded, so you cannot do rate limiting today — but you might be able to do the inverse. Instead of limiting the big tenants, you could mark their scheduled-export queries with a low-priority tag at the entry point (the scheduled job itself, not mid-flight) and let ClickHouse's workload management bump them behind interactive queries. What to try: add a single `?priority=batch` query parameter in the export scheduler, then map that to a ClickHouse workload group with capped concurrency — this is much shallower than fixing tenant attribution end-to-end.

**3. Restaurant reservation yielding and walk-in pressure**

High-end restaurants solve a version of this problem without policing customers: they hold a fraction of tables as walk-in only, so reservation holders never crowd out spontaneous demand, and they communicate expected wait times honestly. What you noticed: your SLA contracts promise a latency number but say nothing about when load is measured. The connection: you could reframe the SLA as a rolling average over a 15-minute window rather than instantaneous p99, which smooths the spike mathematically without changing infrastructure. What to try: audit the contract language with your legal team — "p99 latency" is rarely defined to the millisecond measurement window, and a defensible restatement might buy you time while you fix attribution.

**4. Wicket-gate flood control (hydraulic backpressure)**

Old mill races used adjustable wicket gates upstream to throttle flow before it reached the wheel — not to stop water, but to keep the wheel turning at a safe speed. The insight is that the right place to absorb variance is upstream, not at the bottleneck. What you noticed: the exports are scheduled jobs, meaning they are generated by code your tenants wrote (or that your platform runs on their behalf). That code has to call something to start the export. If you own that trigger point — an export API endpoint, a UI button, a cron runner — you already have a natural gate. What to try: add a simple token-bucket at the export-initiation endpoint, not at the query layer. A bucket of 3 concurrent export starts per tenant, per hour, costs almost nothing to implement and does not require tenant-ID attribution inside ClickHouse at all.
