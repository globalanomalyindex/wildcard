The wildcard that landed here is thermohaline circulation — the global ocean conveyor belt, driven not by wind at the surface but by density differences from temperature and salinity at depth. I'll think from it through a physical oceanographer's lens, specifically one who studies the *timing and rhythm* of the conveyor's behavior.

---

**Seed 1: the fast variable and the slow variable read the same output on different clocks**

In the ocean, temperature changes fast (a storm, a warm current intrusion) while salinity changes slowly (evaporation over months, river discharge over seasons). The density that actually drives circulation is a *function of both*, but a sensor measuring only the slow variable — salinity — will miss what the fast variable is doing right now. You'll be measuring a stable baseline while the water is already stratifying.

Your pricing page shows customers only the slow variable: hosts, which are an infrastructure-provisioning unit that changes on the rhythm of infrastructure decisions (days to weeks). Requests are the fast variable — they ramp in hours and can outrun the host topology in a trial's first week. The pricing page freezes the slow-variable reading at purchase time and never surfaces the fast variable at all. Customers walk into the trial with a stable-salinity map of a thermally stratifying ocean. You might find it useful to surface the fast variable — running request velocity — alongside host consumption somewhere in-product during the trial, so both clocks are visible. Your call whether that's on the pricing page, an in-trial dashboard, or an email at hour 48.

---

**Seed 2: the forcing happens at the surface; the mechanism plays out at depth — you need a tracer that follows the water**

Thermohaline circulation is forced at the surface (cooling, evaporation, ice formation), but the density plume that actually drives the conveyor doesn't form at the surface — it forms when surface water becomes dense enough to sink. The two events are spatially and temporally separated. If you instrument only the surface, you cannot know what the conveyor is doing. Physical oceanographers use chemical tracers — dissolved gases, isotopic signatures — that travel with the water parcel through the column and report what happened at depth, long after the surface event.

The structurally parallel problem here: the pricing page is the surface. The host-limit collision is depth. They are separated by a week of trial usage, and the only instrumentation you currently have is at the surface (what tier they selected). You don't have a tracer that follows the user through the host-consumption column. A "tracer" in your system would be something like a host-utilization rate calculated per user from day one, that predicts days-until-limit-hit in real time and can trigger an intervention before the collision. The tracer doesn't require changing the pricing unit — it runs on the data you already have.

---

**Seed 3: the conveyor has a precursor signature before it halts — if you can read it, you can act before the threshold**

Thermohaline circulation doesn't halt suddenly without warning in the data; there are precursor signals — weakening of the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation measurable years before any full stoppage, shifts in sea surface salinity in the North Atlantic, changes in heat transport — that, if you're watching them, give you a window to act before the tipping point. The problem historically has been that no one was watching those variables; they were watching the surface for weather.

The host-limit collapse in your trial funnel is a threshold event. Users don't gradually churn — they hit the limit and exit, attributing it to deception. But host-consumption velocity in the first 72 hours of a trial is almost certainly a precursor signal: a user who adds three hosts in day one is on a trajectory the slow-variable pricing page never anticipated. In oceanographic terms, that's the freshwater pulse that warns you the conveyor is about to halt. You might find it worth asking whether that early-velocity signal is already in your data — and whether a well-timed in-product message at, say, 60% of the host limit (rather than 100%) could reach users before the trust collapse, not during it.

---

**Seed 4: the cost of preventing the halt is structurally lower than the cost of restarting the conveyor after it stops**

This is the lesson that makes thermohaline research practically urgent: maintaining the conveyor requires continuous small adjustments to the density gradient — modest reductions in freshwater forcing, modest thermal stabilization. Restarting it after a full halt requires reversing a global state change, which is qualitatively harder and may not be fully reversible at all. The two interventions are not symmetric in cost.

Your exit survey is telling you something about hysteresis: "felt tricked" is a trust-state, not a service-state. Removing the host limit after the fact doesn't restart the conveyor — you'd have to undo a concluded attribution of bad faith, which is a different kind of intervention than preventing it. This is an argument about *where to spend the intervention budget*, not about the pricing unit at all: a dollar spent on in-trial host-limit legibility (the prevention side) is likely worth more than a dollar spent on win-back campaigns targeting churned users who filed "deception" in an exit survey. Whether that's useful framing for the finance conversation or just for where you invest engineering effort — entirely up to you.
