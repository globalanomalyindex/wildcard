A door, measured precisely, is not its size - it is the fit between door and frame. The diagnostic number is the *gap*, not the absolute dimension. And a door placed at the wrong threshold doesn't just fail to open - it hides which room you're actually trying to reach.

Three things caught my attention in your situation.

**The compound metric problem.** In my field, the classic measurement mistake is placing the measurement threshold at the wrong level of the system - measuring whether you've exited the building when the question is whether you've crossed the door of one specific room inside it. "Can I play this piece" is an exit-the-building metric. It combines bass-clef reading, rhythmic internalization, and finger pattern recall into one signal. When the piece works, you can't tell which components are contributing; when it doesn't, you can't tell which one is blocking. Your plateau isn't a mystery - it's a measurement problem. The stuck skills (bass clef, syncopation) are being evaluated inside a compound signal that masks whether they're improving at all. You might find it useful to measure each one separately, however crude the measure: not "did the piece work" but "did I read that bass note before playing it, or did I guess." One door at a time.

**The frame-and-door mismatch.** A door only fits if the gap between door and frame is inside tolerance - not zero, not wide open, but a specific range. The beginner-book restart loop looks structurally like a measurement error about fit: the question you keep implicitly asking is "am I a beginner?" (an absolute measurement) when the useful measurement is the *gap* - how many pages, how many sections, between where you currently hit a ceiling and where the book gets hard enough to matter. If that gap were measured directly, the restart might resolve: you wouldn't start from page 1, you'd open to the page where the book's difficulty first exceeds your current ceiling, and start there. The book hasn't failed you; you've been measuring at the wrong threshold.

**The detection floor.** Measurement instruments have a lower limit below which they can't resolve a signal - not failure, just the floor of what they can detect. Some skills accumulate per session only if the session exceeds a certain length; below it, the exposure is real but it doesn't cross the threshold where consolidation begins. Bass-clef reading fluency is the kind of skill that probably has a longer consolidation window than finger-pattern work, because it's building a symbolic lookup table, not reinforcing a kinetic sequence. Twenty minutes is a real constraint - and it might be above the floor for pattern work while being below the floor for symbol-reading. If the 20 minutes gets distributed across everything, no single component may be getting above its own detection threshold. Concentrating all 20 minutes on bass clef alone, for a week, might cross the floor where none of the mixed sessions were reaching. Your call entirely - but the 8pm hard stop could be a feature if it forces that kind of concentration rather than fighting it.
