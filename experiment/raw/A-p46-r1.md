I'm a maritime archaeologist. My corner of the field is artifact provenance recording - specifically, excavating shipwreck sites in low-visibility conditions where I have a narrow dive window and every action is irreversible. The discipline I live inside is this: an artifact's meaning is almost entirely relational. Where it was, what was next to it, what layer it came from - that's the evidence. The object itself is almost secondary. And the thing I fear most is unprovenanced lifting: someone raises a beautiful pewter spoon without recording its position first, and in that single act, the story is gone. The spoon is present. Its context is permanently severed.

I've been looking at your problem through that lens, and a few things genuinely rhyme.

---

**Unprovenanced lifting.** In my world, the failure isn't losing the artifact - it's losing the *relational record* that made the artifact meaningful. You're doing the same thing every time you save a recipe. The screenshot survives; the context doesn't: when you made it, who ate it, what you changed, whether the gluten-free version worked. The recipe exists in your collection the way a spoon exists in a museum's uncatalogued storage - present, but unnavigable. The move in my field is to record at the moment of contact, because that's when the relational information is free. After the dive, reconstruction is always degraded. You might find it useful to ask: is there a post-meal moment, maybe thirty seconds while you're clearing the table, where one sentence of context is still free - "lamb thing from the binder, everyone liked it, used the smoked paprika" - and would cost real effort to reconstruct on Sunday?

---

**The site matrix.** A shipwreck isn't a pile of objects - it's a spatial argument, and the *relationships between artifacts* are the evidence. A pewter spoon in the cook's quarters and the same spoon in the captain's cabin are different facts. Your recipe collection is currently a pile: five heterogeneous formats with no shared relational structure. But your recipes have a natural matrix that your problem has already described - they relate to each other through pantry overlap (which recipes share the same staple spices), through dietary fit (which are safe as-is for the gluten-free child, which need a swap), and through household reception (which got requested again, which didn't). A site record becomes navigable not because you sorted the objects, but because you mapped the relations between them. You might find it useful to think about what the *matrix* of your collection looks like - not "all pasta dishes" but "dishes that clear the pantry as it currently stands" or "reliable weeknight meals the gluten-free version of which I've already tested."

---

**The asymmetry of irreversibility.** Everything I do on a site is shaped by one asymmetry: disturbance without recording is permanently destructive, and the window for recording at zero cost is the present moment. The cost of recording before lifting is bounded; the cost of not recording is unbounded. Your collection has the same asymmetry - saving is frictionless (bookmark, tap, screenshot), but the relational information is only live at the moment you're cooking. Once the Sunday planning session is over without a record of what you made and how it landed, that information either costs real effort to recover or is gone. The archaeological move is to respect that window. The window isn't long; the question is just whether you have a capture habit tuned to it.

---

**Making site-independent copies.** When we excavate a fragile artifact - something that might deteriorate, crack, or become unavailable once removed - we record it in full before we trust the original to survive. The record is the backup, and we treat it as primary. About half your saved links will eventually hit paywalls or 404s - that's ordinary link rot, and it's outside your control. But the original had a window when the content was fully accessible. The archaeological move is to copy the artifact's properties while you still can: the actual recipe text, ingredient list, technique. Not the link to it. The copy survives what the original doesn't. You might find it useful to treat "save this recipe" as meaning "copy the content," not "bookmark the URL."
