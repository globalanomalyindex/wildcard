I set type by hand - lead sorts, composing stick, long-form work where the form might hold ten thousand characters before it sees the press. The lens I work from is signals-and-noise: out of ten thousand pieces of metal, most are right, and my job is finding the ones that are wrong before they print. Specifically, I work on jobs with mixed founts - legal briefs, technical manuals, anything where you are pulling from multiple cases at once and the error rate is uneven across them. Let me think from that corner.

---

**Seed one: fount classification comes before character inspection**

In my work, the first thing I do when I pick up a galley proof is not start reading. I identify what founts are in the form - which typefaces, sizes, and sorts are present, and which of those are high-risk (recently reset, pulled from a case I haven't worked in a while, unusual ligature combinations). Errors don't distribute evenly: they cluster in the risky founts and passes. A form with eight lines of stable roman and two lines of freshly set small-cap Latin gets its attention weighted accordingly. I give the small-cap lines three passes; the stable roman one.

The structural match here: your alert stream is a form, and you do not yet have a fount classification. "Nobody can confidently say which alerts actually protect the regulated paths" is exactly the state of picking up a galley without knowing what founts are in it. Before any threshold decision, a one-time classification pass - tagging each alert by whether it sits on the regulated payment flow or on the batch infrastructure - would split your population in a way that makes everything else legible. Not to mute anything yet, just to know: which founts are in this form?

In my world, that classification pass takes an afternoon and changes everything about how you read the next six months of proofs. You might find it useful to do the equivalent before any threshold is touched.

---

**Seed two: the turned letter - noise that trains you to discount a whole character class**

The failure mode I fear most is the turned letter. A turned "n" reads as a "u" at casual inspection; a turned "d" looks like a "q." They are not obvious errors - they mimic valid characters. The dangerous thing about them is not just that they print wrong; it is that if you see too many of them in a form where "u" is common, you start discounting all u-shapes slightly. Your eye habituates to the corruption in that position. You stop reading that glyph carefully.

Your per-host CPU and disk threshold alerts are turned letters. They fire on a pattern - elevated CPU - that during batch jobs is noise, but that in other contexts is the shape of a real incident. The consequence is not just that the current pages are wasted; it is that the engineers who read this form every week are now habituated to discount that alert shape entirely. When a real CPU incident fires outside batch context, it arrives in a form that has trained its readers to skim it.

The compositor's move is not to ignore all characters that look like that glyph - it is to find and fix the specific turned sorts. The alert equivalent: make these alerts batch-context-aware (suppress during known batch windows, or replace the measurement with one that doesn't fire on batch load). The goal is not fewer alerts globally - it is restoring legibility for that class, so when one fires out of context, it reads again.

---

**Seed three: proof fatigue is a composition problem, not a reader problem**

When a proofreader goes blind to the type - stops discriminating errors from expected variation - the compositor's corrective is not to train the proofreader to concentrate harder. It is to fix the composition before it reaches them. If the form is too noisy, you clean up the form. You do not ask more of the reader.

Two engineers muting their phones overnight is proof fatigue. Asking them to stay engaged is asking the proofreader to concentrate harder - which fails because fatigue is physiological and attentional, not motivational. But there is a further thing the compositor does that is worth naming: we do not route all proofs to the same reader. Fine legal text and rough working proofs go to different people, or to the same person through different channels at different urgency levels. A working proof does not wake anyone up.

The structural move here: the alerts that are noise (batch CPU, disk thresholds) are not wrong to exist - they may have a legitimate use, like a working proof does. They are wrong to be routed through the same channel as the regulated-path pages. Moving them to a lower-urgency channel (a daytime Slack digest, a weekly summary) is not lowering your guard on the regulated paths; it is routing different fount classes to the appropriate reader posture. The compliance-mandated five-minute pages stay exactly as they are. What changes is that the form arriving at 2 a.m. is worth waking up for.
