I'm a sigillographer — I authenticate wax seals by studying the material relationship between the matrix (the metal die the issuer holds) and the wax impression (what the recipient gets). My specific obsession is what happens when fine detail fails to transfer: why a perfectly good matrix produces an unreadable blob, and how historical practices compensated without remaking the die.

---

**The matrix and the substrate are inverse languages of the same information.**

In my field, a seal's matrix speaks in negative relief — everything is reversed, recessed, mirrored. The wax speaks in positive. They encode identical content but in opposite form, and a recipient who can't perform the inversion mentally just sees a vague shape and uses rough heuristics — size, color, general outline — to decide whether to trust the document.

Your pricing is structured the same way. The company holds the matrix: hosts per tier. Your trial users arrive holding wax: requests per month. These aren't different things — they're inverse encodings of the same underlying infrastructure reality. But the customer is being handed a seal and asked to read it in negative, when they only know how to read positive. So they collapse on the fine structure and use the only heuristic left: cheapest option.

What transfers: the historical response to this wasn't to recut the die. It was to add a *legend* — text running around the seal's circumference that translated the visual symbol into words, for recipients who couldn't read heraldic form. The legend is additive. It wraps the impression without changing the matrix. In your world, a legend is a small translation layer on the pricing page — something that maps each tier's host limit to its approximate requests-per-month equivalent, derived from your actual customer usage data. Finance doesn't touch the billing unit; you're only wrapping the impression with words the recipient can read. You might find it worth asking: what does your p50 customer at each tier actually consume in requests, and can that number live on the page alongside the host count?

---

**The moment of impression is when all information transfer happens — and it cannot be redone.**

In wax-seal practice, the substrate has to be at the right temperature and viscosity when the matrix strikes. Too cold and it cracks; too fluid and fine detail collapses before it sets. You get a blob either way. The condition of the substrate at the moment of impression is as load-bearing as the quality of the die — a perfect matrix against unprepared wax still fails.

Your trial sign-up is an impression moment. The user arrives at the pricing page, forms a tier choice, and that choice sets. If they arrive without a mental model that can receive host-based pricing — if the substrate isn't warmed to the right frame — the fine structure (which tier fits their actual scale) collapses and they buy on price. The exit-survey language "felt tricked" is the experience of discovering, post-impression, that the seal didn't say what they thought it said.

What transfers: in seal authentication, substrate preparation happens *before* the matrix strikes — you warm the wax, you assess the surface. The intervention isn't on the pricing page; it's earlier. If a trial-signup flow asked one orienting question ("roughly how many API calls do you make per month?") and then said "at that volume you'd be running about N hosts — here's what that means for each tier," the substrate would be warm before the impression. Not a calculator in the competitors' sense — no rate comparison, no unfavorable unit exposure — just a single orienting translation that prepares the mental model before the tier choice is made. You might find it worth asking: is there a moment in the trial-entry flow where you could ask one question that does this framing work?

---

**The legend only needs to cover the range where you want it read.**

This is a smaller observation. In historical sigillography, legends weren't always complete translations — they covered the parts of the matrix that were ambiguous to the intended recipient, and left the heraldically-literate parts unlabeled. Selective translation served the authentication function without exposing the full grammar of the seal to parties who might misuse it.

Your constraint is that a full calculator exposes unfavorable unit economics at small scale. But your trial users aren't, in aggregate, at small scale — they're churning in week one because they exceeded a limit they didn't see coming, not because your per-unit economics are bad for them. A legend that covers only the mid-to-large bands (where your unit is favorable) still solves the problem for the majority of churning trials, without publishing the small-scale comparison your competitors can exploit. You'd be offering a partial tabula, not a full one. Whether the uncovered band matters enough to leave it out is your call.
