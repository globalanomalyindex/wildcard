I'm a military and mass-casualty physician. My whole practice is organized around one discipline: when resources are scarce and irreversible, you sort before you treat. The first responder's hardest lesson is not how to treat - it's learning not to stop at the first patient you encounter. You survey the whole field, tag everyone, then allocate. A responder who starts treating the first wounded person they see will be kneeling in the dirt when the highest-yield cases deteriorate unchecked twenty meters away. Triage is not a medical procedure. It's a prioritization architecture.

A few things in your situation caught my eye.

---

**The missing sort-before-edit pass.** In mass-casualty response, the worst outcome comes from treating without triaging first - pouring capacity into the first problem encountered while the highest-yield problems remain untagged. Your editing workflow, as you describe it, sounds like it starts treating on contact: you open 4-6 hours of footage and begin cutting through it. The sort hasn't happened yet. In my world the fix is a rapid coarse pass before any treatment begins - not fine-grained assessment, deliberately degraded, moving fast. You tag the whole field into three bins (use it / maybe / don't touch it) without doing any actual editing, then close your laptop. Only then do you go back and treat what you tagged. The ratio of "don't touch it" footage to "use it" footage in a 24:1 compression job is probably enormous - most of those hours are expectant before you even look hard. The sort pass costs twenty minutes and makes the edit session a function of the "use it" pile, not the raw total. Your call whether that changes anything in your workflow, but it's the structural move my field makes under exactly this constraint: irreversible input, fixed pool, no reshoots.

---

**The 45-second bleed is a triage failure, not a footage problem.** When 30% of viewers leave before the build, the natural response is to want better opening footage - ideally, a reshoot. But reshoots are ruled out. In triage terms, this patient is not re-shootable; it is "delayed" at best given your constraint. The triage move for a "delayed" patient is not better diagnosis - it is to accept the coarse tag and allocate to what is accessible. Your opening isn't a footage problem, it's an assembly problem: you have existing clips somewhere in that 4-6 hours that open strong, they just haven't been sorted to the front. In my field, the primary assessment is deliberately coarse because comprehensiveness is the enemy of throughput. A dedicated "find the hook" pass - five minutes, looking only for clips that drop the viewer into motion or tension immediately, ignoring everything else - is accessible right now, from existing footage. The wound is reachable; treat it first.

---

**Thumbnail, title, topic - sort by reversibility, not by diagnosis.** You have three candidate causes for the impression stall and no way to tell which is responsible. The diagnostic instinct is to wait until the cause is clear before treating. In triage, that's the exact wrong move when the candidates have different accessibility. Thumbnails and titles can be changed after upload; topics are fixed once the video is filmed and glued up. The triage principle here is not "diagnose the cause, then treat" - it's "sort by reversibility, treat in that order." Thumbnails first: they are the most accessible variable, they can be swapped on your last five videos today, and a/b-testing them closes one variable in a week rather than waiting months for a topic-level pattern to emerge from new videos. You don't need a diagnosis to act on the most accessible wound. The sequencing itself generates the evidence that tells you whether to move to the next variable.
