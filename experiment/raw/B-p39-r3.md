I'm a geothermal geochemist. My whole professional life is the measurement problem: a hot spring vents at the surface, but what I actually want to know is the state of the deep reservoir, and the surface reading is a lagged, mixed, transformed signal of that. The craft is finding proxies that encode the real state at origin and survive the transit intact, so you can read the deep truth from the surface without being fooled by what the upwelling does to it.

Here are four things I notice from that corner.

---

**The geothermometer problem: your system status is a surface reading that has decoupled from reservoir state.**

In my field, vent temperature is not reservoir temperature. The water is mixed and modified on the way up. The fix is a geothermometer — a chemical ratio that is set at formation depth and preserved through the entire flow path, so it still encodes the true condition at origin even when the surface appearance has been altered. The geothermometer is only useful if it is consistently captured at the right moment.

The "available" flag in your system is a vent reading. It decoupled from physical readiness as soon as the return exceeded your inspection throughput. What encodes the real state is the condition *at the moment of return* — fuel, mud, visible damage — because that is set at origin and does not change. The inconsistency of your check-in photos is destroying your geothermometer. A structured capture at return (same fields, every unit, every time) is not just better record-keeping; it is restoring a signal that is currently scrambled. You might find it useful to think about which three or four fields, if captured consistently at check-in, would let you answer most dispute questions without touching the photos at all.

---

**Recharge/discharge pairing: the return reading is only meaningful against a locked dispatch baseline.**

A hot spring system has a recharge zone (where water enters the ground) and a discharge zone (the vent), separated by years of transit. The measurement insight is that a discharge reading means almost nothing without knowing what was put in at recharge. Geochemists pair the two: the isotopic signature of the input water is the baseline against which the output chemistry is read.

Your dispute problem has this same structure. A damage claim at return is only resolvable if there is a locked record of condition at dispatch. If the dispatch record is inconsistent or absent, the return reading is floating — you have the discharge chemistry but you lost the recharge baseline. The unit of measurement is the *pair*, not either event alone. If you standardize dispatch condition records with the same structure as return records, each unit carries its own paired baseline through the rental, and disputes become a diff against a locked reference rather than a memory contest.

---

**Pre-pulse pressure: the Saturday surge is a geyser, and what matters is Friday's gauge reading.**

Geyser systems alternate between a quiet recharge phase and a violent discharge pulse. The surface is misleading during recharge — the system looks quiet while pressure builds. The practical measurement move is not to measure the pulse itself but to instrument the *pre-pulse state*: conduit water level, ground tilt, microseismicity in the hours before. That gives you lead time to respond rather than trying to manage during the eruption.

Your Saturday 7–9 am window is a geyser pulse: high-volume, fixed labor, no slack. Managing it during the pulse is hard. What you have is Friday afternoon. A Friday-afternoon queue-depth audit — how many units are currently between returned and inspected, and which ones have pickups Saturday morning — is the pre-pulse pressure gauge. It gives you lead time to triage: which units must be cleared tonight, which can slot to Monday, who needs to be called. In my world you do not measure the geyser; you measure the conduit fill the evening before.

---

**Residence time as a credibility weight: the excavator problem is a stale surface reading.**

In hydrothermal geochemistry, residence time — how long water has been in the reservoir before emerging — is used to *weight* the credibility of a surface reading. A fast conduit delivers fresh information; a slow one is showing you the system state from months ago. The age of the reading is a built-in discount on its reliability.

Your three excavators that have not turned in weeks are showing you a surface status that is weeks old. The "available" flag is not lying — it is just very stale. In my field we would say the signal has high residence time; you have to discount it heavily. The useful move is to make residence time visible in your system: not just "available" but "available — last confirmed inspection: 23 days ago." The age of the last confirmed physical check is exactly the credibility weight on the status. Units with high residence time in the limbo state are the ones your mechanics should be able to flag in a single sort, not ones you discover by walking the yard and noticing the excavators haven't moved.
