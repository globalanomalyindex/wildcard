Here are four angles from outside the usual dispatch-and-inventory frame:

**1. Ant colony foraging — stigmergy instead of centralized routing**

Ant colonies solve a similar geometry problem without a central dispatcher. They deposit pheromone trails that reinforce good routes and fade on bad ones, so the swarm self-organizes around dense demand. The connection here is stigmergy: indirect coordination through shared marks in the environment. What you might try is a lightweight version of this — give each technician a simple end-of-day log that marks which zip codes they were in and which parts they used. After a few weeks that record becomes a heat map, and you can pre-position techs at the start of each day toward the zones where calls have been clustering, without a dispatcher deciding anything. The routing emerges from the trace.

**2. Hospital pharmacy par-level logic applied to each van**

Hospital pharmacies do not track every pill in real time. They set a par level for each medication by location — this ward carries exactly three of X, and when it drops below one, a replenishment trigger fires. Your vans are essentially rolling pharmacy carts covering different patient populations. The insight is that each van does not need the same inventory; it needs a par level calibrated to its usual territory and call type. If your best installer primarily does new installs in one corridor of the metro, his van's par list should look nothing like a van that runs maintenance calls. You would not need a trusted inventory count to start this — you could derive initial par lists just from whatever your technicians can recall about their last thirty calls, then adjust as patterns emerge.

**3. Blood courier networks — small depots defeat the distance problem**

Some medical blood-bank networks deal with exactly your summer-surge geometry: demand spikes, product has a short shelf life, and sending couriers back to a central depot costs too many hours. Their solution is to seed a handful of small satellite depots at the geographic midpoints of demand, stocked only with the fast-movers. This translates directly to your situation: even one unmanned cache — a rented storage unit or a trusted supply-house with a will-call arrangement — positioned near the densest summer call cluster would cut your best installer's repositioning time substantially. The parts do not have to live on the truck; they have to be closer than the shop.

**4. Theater prop management — knowing what is "on stage" versus "in stock"**

Theater production managers track props at two levels: what is committed to an active show versus what sits available in the warehouse. This two-status view is the thing your parts room likely lacks. Right now everything in your parts room probably exists in one undifferentiated pile, so when a tech pulls a board it leaves no mark. If you introduced even a paper "committed" tag — a technician writes their name and the job on a card and clips it to whatever they take from the shelf — you would have a real-time visible-committed layer without any software. The count you actually trust comes from what remains untagged. This also surfaces the most common failure mode you described: the part is on a truck, committed to no current job, invisible to everyone else.
