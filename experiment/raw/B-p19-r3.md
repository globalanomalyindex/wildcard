The concept that landed: **recycling** -- specifically through the lens of measurement.

There are three places where recycling's measurement problems genuinely rhyme with yours.

---

**1. Contamination readout at collection time**

In recycling, what goes into the bin and what actually gets processed are two different things -- the collected volume and the recoverable yield are decoupled. Facilities that figured this out early started giving collectors a contamination signal *at the point of collection*, not after the batch failed at the plant. The design move: surface the hidden measurement where it can still change behavior, not after the cost has already landed.

The structural match here: your trial users' host count and their experienced load (requests) are similarly decoupled. They can fill their "bin" -- spin up monitoring, feel like things are working -- and not know their host allocation is nearly full until the week-one wall hits. A contamination readout in your world would be a visible host-utilization signal during the trial: not a warning at 100%, but a running readout from day one. The metric that will matter at billing time is legible before it becomes a cliff. You might find it interesting to check whether surfacing that signal earlier changes how users self-select tiers -- without changing the unit at all.

---

**2. Wish-cycling: sorting at the wrong moment with the wrong information**

A well-documented failure mode in recycling is wish-cycling -- people place items in the recycling bin based on what they *hope* is recyclable, not what they *know* is. The root cause is that the signal enabling correct sorting (is this item recyclable?) isn't present at the point of the sorting decision. The mismatch between intent and outcome is baked in at the moment of action.

Your trial users are wish-tiering: they pick the cheapest plan based on hope (I probably don't have that many hosts), not measurement. The signal that would enable correct tier selection -- their current or expected host count -- isn't present at the point of signup. In recycling, programs that put the sorting signal at the decision point (labels, bin-side guides, even simple yes/no lists) substantially reduce downstream mismatches. The parallel isn't a pricing calculator that exposes unfavorability; it's a small diagnostic at trial signup that asks "how many servers or containers are you monitoring?" and says "based on that, here's which tier fits." The unit doesn't change; the moment of measurement does.

---

**3. Throughput vs. yield: optimizing the metric you can easily count**

Recycling programs spent years reporting on tonnage collected -- the easy-to-count metric -- while yield (tons actually recovered and sold into markets) quietly diverged. High collection numbers, low recovery rates. The proxy metric captured a real quantity but not the economically meaningful one, and the divergence was invisible until the downstream economics broke.

The structural match: finance models per-host revenue (easy to count; the unit you bill in) while trial-to-paid conversion (the yield) is what's actually failing. Both are real numbers; one is the proxy the system optimizes for. In recycling, the programs that corrected this built dashboards showing both numbers in tandem -- collection rate *and* yield rate -- so the gap between them was legible. You might find it worth surfacing both metrics side-by-side for finance: host revenue per cohort alongside trial conversion rate, so the cost of the current unit's misalignment with user mental models is visible in terms finance already speaks. Not as a board proposal for changing the unit, but as a joint readout that makes the gap impossible to ignore.
