I'm a forensic evidence technician - I train prosecutors on why cases collapse, and the answer is almost always the same: not bad actors, but missing records. Chain of custody isn't a paperwork ritual. It's a provenance graph where every node records who touched this, when, under what authorization, and in what condition they received and transferred it. When a link is missing, the whole chain downstream becomes unverifiable - and in court that means inadmissible, regardless of whether anything was actually tampered with.

Here's what I notice in your situation.

---

**The stolen-laptop blast radius is not a rotation-cadence problem - it's a broken chain problem.**

In my field: when a piece of evidence is challenged, contamination is precisely scoped. "Which items passed through handler X between dates A and B" is a computable query - but only because the custody log recorded which handler touched which item at the moment of each transfer. When that log exists, a compromised handler means a bounded, enumerable set of affected items and a surgical response. When it doesn't, every piece of evidence in the building is potentially suspect.

Your two-day staging outage maps this exactly. The contractor had access, the laptop was compromised, and the blast radius was unbounded because there was no record of which credentials had been distributed to that identity. So the response had to be total - scorched earth - because "everything touched by this identity" was not a computable set. The friction isn't in rotating credentials; it's that rotation had to be exhaustive because scoping was impossible.

The transferable move: before SOC 2 fieldwork, the highest-leverage artifact isn't a rotation schedule - it's an identity-to-credential map built at issuance time and maintained as a log. When the next incident happens (and there will be one), "what needs rotating" becomes a query against that log rather than a reconstruction from memory and chat history.

---

**Post-hoc reconstruction is the forensic equivalent of inadmissible evidence.**

In my field, documentation created after the fact to explain a transfer is inadmissible - not because we assume bad faith, but because retroactive reconstruction can't be distinguished from confabulation. The integrity of the chain comes precisely from attestation at the moment of transfer.

Your current state: credential distribution happens without event-time logging. .env files get shared in Slack, CI variables get set by whoever had access that week, vault entries get added without audit trail. When the incident happened, you had to reconstruct access from memory. That reconstruction is your inadmissible evidence: it can't be verified, it can't be scoped, and an auditor examining it three months from now is in the same position a defense attorney is - they have to take your word for it.

SOC 2 fieldwork is going to ask for evidence of access controls. Reconstructed logs, after the fact, are a hard conversation. Logs that were written at issuance time are a different conversation entirely.

---

**You cannot establish chain of custody for an item that was never inventoried.**

This is step zero in evidence management, and it's prior to everything else. An item that was never logged into the system has no chain - its provenance is permanently opaque regardless of how carefully you track it afterward. The forensic equivalent of your hardcoded strings found in repo history: these are items that were never entered into evidence. They exist, they have been in someone's possession, and there is no record.

The structural implication: the rotation cadence, the vault, the CI variable hygiene - these are all custody-management practices that only work on credentials that have been inventoried. The untracked ones sit outside the system entirely. In my field the first thing you do before establishing a custody process is an evidence audit: what exists, where is it, who has a copy. That audit is unglamorous and engineers will push back, but the audit has to precede the process - you can't track the custody of things you don't know exist.
