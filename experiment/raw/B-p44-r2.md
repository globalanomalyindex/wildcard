Something from physics landed here that might be worth a few minutes of your attention.

**The physicist's obsession with centripetal force** is not the force itself but what it maintains: a closed orbit against a constant tendency to escape along the tangent. Take away the centripetal constraint for even an instant and the object doesn't spiral gradually away - it exits immediately, straight, on whatever trajectory momentum was already carrying it. The circle isn't a natural shape; it's a continuously enforced one. Three structural things in your situation rhyme with this honestly.

---

**1. The quit-and-restart pattern is a tangential escape, not a motivation problem.**

In my field: when the centripetal constraint fails, the object follows the tangent - the path momentum was already on. The failure isn't gradual; it's instantaneous, and it exits onto the line the object was always trying to travel anyway.

The structure maps: each time you reach familiar territory, the centripetal constraint (forward engagement) drops, and you exit onto the tangent - which in your case is "start over from measure one." The object of your restarts isn't laziness; it's the structural exit point. Familiar material removes the constraint and the exit happens automatically.

Worth exploring: can you enter the orbit at a different point - past the familiar zone - so the tangent you'd exit on if engagement drops points *through* the hard material rather than around it? A book opened to the bass clef chapter, not page one.

---

**2. The "felt resistance" peaks exactly at the point of highest curvature - and that peak is structural, not motivational.**

In my field: from inside a circular orbit, the "outward pull" (the pseudo-force, the felt urge to go straight) is not a real force. It's entirely a consequence of being constrained to curve. It is felt most intensely at the tightest part of the path. It is not a signal that something is wrong - it is what curving feels like from the inside.

The structure maps: the felt difficulty at the junction between what you know and what you don't - bass clef decoding, syncopation internalization - is the felt experience of curvature at that junction. It is not evidence you are failing. It is what being exactly at the edge of competence feels like from the inside. The pseudo-force is most intense there because the required change of direction is sharpest there.

This might be worth something when you notice the felt pull toward familiar pieces. It isn't telling you to turn back; it's reporting that you are at the hardest corner of the orbit.

---

**3. The orbit has a natural period that depends on radius - shrink the radius, not the speed.**

In my field: the period of a circular orbit (how long one complete loop takes) is set by the radius and the available centripetal force. You cannot simply hurry the object around a large orbit with insufficient force - the path breaks. But a smaller orbit, completed at lower speed, is a genuine full loop, and the object returns to its starting point changed.

The structure maps: a "complete orbit" in your learning has a specific shape - you start from your current edge of competence, move into unfamiliar territory, push against it until something shifts, and *return* to a stable footing with the new thing partially absorbed. Twenty minutes might not be enough centripetal resource for an ambitious arc (say, working through an entire new movement pattern for syncopation). But twenty minutes might be exactly enough for a very tight orbit: one bar of bass clef, read slowly, until the notes are not mysterious. That is a closed loop. The bass clef fluency you're missing isn't one large orbit away - it is accumulated through many small closed orbits, each one tight enough to complete in the time you have.

The calibration question my field would ask: given your available centripetal resource (20 focused minutes, noise-constrained), what is the largest orbit radius you can complete a full period in? Not the most ambitious arc you can start - the one you can *finish and return from*.
