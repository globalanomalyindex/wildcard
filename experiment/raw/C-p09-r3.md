Here are four angles from outside the usual frame:

**1. Air traffic flow management — metered departure slots**

Air traffic controllers face the same problem: many flights want the same airspace at the same peak window, and clearing the backlog late is expensive. Their solution is not to block flights but to issue "departure slots" — a flight gets a precise takeoff window so the sky never saturates. Applied to your exports: stop thinking about rate limits and start thinking about reservation slots. Each enterprise tenant's scheduled export job requests a 10-minute window from a lightweight scheduler rather than firing at :00. The scheduler spreads them across the hour. No code changes to the query path, no tenant isolation needed. You get load leveling and a paper trail that makes the SLA conversation with sales much easier: "your contract includes a guaranteed execution window, not a guaranteed instant."

**2. Power grid demand pricing — time-of-use signals**

Utilities have customers who prefer cheap off-peak power but will pay more if they insist on peak. They don't enforce limits; they change the price. You can do something analogous without enforcing anything: surface a latency forecast to enterprise tenants through a thin API or dashboard — "expected p99 at :00 is 4.2s; at :20 it is 0.8s" — and let their schedulers self-optimize. Many enterprise data teams run exports via dbt, Airflow, or similar tools that accept cron-style offsets. A few will voluntarily shift once they can see the congestion. This approach also generates internal leverage: when sales promises unlimited access, engineering can point out that the signal already exists and the product respects it — you are just not yet charging for priority windows.

**3. Emergency medicine triage — acuity tagging at intake**

ERs handle heterogeneous load on shared resources by tagging every patient at the door before they reach any resource. You have the opposite problem: tenant ID is inconsistently threaded, so you cannot triage at all. The ER lesson is that the tagging step is the single investment that makes everything else possible — staffing predictions, resource routing, SLA honoring. Even a one-sprint effort to reliably attach a tenant ID and query-weight estimate at the HTTP gateway (or ClickHouse's query settings / initial_query_id) — before touching any throttling logic — gives you retrospective attribution. Once you have 30 days of attributed load data you will have the business case that moves isolated clusters into next year's budget.

**4. Ecological succession — pioneer species and a stable canopy**

In forest succession, fast-growing pioneer species exploit open resources aggressively until slower, shade-tolerant species build a stable canopy that regulates the whole system. Your enterprise tenants are running pioneer-species behavior: they found an open resource and are exploiting it at maximum rate. The canopy equivalent is a lightweight query queue in front of ClickHouse — something like ClickHouse's built-in workload management or a proxy like chproxy — that imposes a per-tenant concurrency cap without changing the query path or requiring tenant ID threading to be perfect. Even a coarse cap (e.g., any single source IP is limited to N simultaneous queries) would blunt the spike immediately while you build the proper attribution layer in parallel. The point is that the canopy does not need to know every species' name to moderate the ecosystem; it just needs to exist.

---

All four are optional starting points, not prescriptions. The triage angle (3) is probably the forcing function for everything else: without reliable attribution, the others are harder to justify or tune.
