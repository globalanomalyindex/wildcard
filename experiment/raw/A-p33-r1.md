I work in air traffic flow management - specifically the Traffic Management Unit side of en-route sector capacity balancing. My job is never to move individual aircraft. It is to keep demand from exceeding sector capacity before the aircraft are even airborne, because ground time is cheap and airborne corrections are expensive. The elegant solution looks like a normal day - nothing dramatic happened because the imbalance was absorbed before it materialized.

Four things from my world rhyme with yours.

---

**1. Ground delay costs nothing; in-the-air vectoring costs everything.**

In flow management: once a flight is airborne, your only tools are vectoring and holding - both burn fuel and time. The cheap intervention is ground delay: hold the aircraft on the ramp until the receiving sector can absorb it. The iron rule is resolve the imbalance *before* commitment.

In your operation, dispatching a van in response to a call is "putting the aircraft in the air." The van is now committed to a trajectory - and every other call it could have taken cheaply (because it would have been nearby) is now an expensive cross-town vector. The cheap intervention is pre-positioning: at start-of-day, stage vans in geographic sub-zones based on predicted demand - historical call volume by neighborhood, time of year, yesterday's temperature. The van is already proximate when the call arrives. The cost of getting this right is a routing conversation before 7 a.m. The cost of getting it wrong is what you have now.

A secondary beat from my world: the failure mode I fear most isn't a busy sector - it's one sector's delay propagating as compression to every downstream sector. That five-day wait queue in summer isn't just doubled volume; it's a compression cascade running through a system with no pre-season absorption mechanism. The equivalent of a ground delay program here is pre-season: a seasonal hire, a parts pre-stock, a zone assignment - done before June, not in response to June.

---

**2. You cannot work a demand-capacity balance against a number you don't trust.**

In flow management, if my sector-capacity figure is unreliable, every tool I have becomes useless. I am allocating against a phantom ceiling. The minimum viable information object I need is a sector capacity number I can trust - not perfect, just trusted.

Your parts room is that sector capacity number. Without a reliable count, every parts-demand match is a guess, and roughly one in four calls confirms the guess was wrong. But the harder version of this problem is that your capacity is *distributed*: it lives across nine moving inventory points (the vans) plus the room. The thing you actually need is not a perfect ERP - it is a van-inventory manifest, verified and updated after every job, so you know which of your nine "sectors" holds the capacitor the next call will need. That manifest is the minimum viable sector-capacity number. Everything that depends on it - routing, pre-staging, second-visit avoidance - is blind without it.

---

**3. Miles-in-trail is a spatial metering rule that prevents bolus congestion downstream.**

When I expect a fix to get hit with too much demand at once, I issue a miles-in-trail restriction: I space aircraft through a geographic waypoint at a controlled rate so the downstream sector never receives a concentrated burst. I am not blocking aircraft - I am metering flow through a spatial constraint to prevent cascade. Overrides exist when absorption allows; the restriction is a default, not a wall.

In your operation there is no spatial structure at all. Each van is free to take any call anywhere - effectively, no sector assignment. The result is the crisscross pattern, which is the routing equivalent of every aircraft converging on the same fix at once. Soft geographic zones - not rigid territories, but default home-sector assignments, with dispatcher override when local capacity is genuinely exhausted - are the miles-in-trail equivalent. The van nearest the call handles it by default. Cross-zone reassignment happens when a zone is saturated. The dispatcher remains the human TMU, calling overrides in real time. You might find it worth sketching what those zones would look like for your metro before deciding whether they fit.

---

**4. Collaborative decision-making only works when both sides can see each other's state.**

In ATFM, I negotiate ground delay with airlines through a CDM framework: the airline knows its aircraft's fuel and schedule state; I know sector capacity. Neither of us can optimize alone. The negotiation is what produces the good outcome - but only because both sides have a number they trust.

Your best installer is the high-value aircraft with a full fuel load being vectored through low-value airspace all day. The dispatcher doesn't know his current position, current job difficulty, remaining capacity for the day, or which calls match his skill level - and he doesn't know what the queue looks like. Neither side can negotiate well. The minimum CDM here might be as simple as a shared whiteboard (physical or digital) with: van location, current job status, today's remaining capacity, and one flag for "this call needs the senior tech." That is not a software project - it is an information structure that makes the negotiation possible.
