I'm a terminal radar controller who sequences arriving aircraft onto a single runway at a busy airport. My entire job is managing a stream of irreversible commitments: once I've turned an aircraft onto final approach, I cannot put it back. All the intelligence has to happen upstream — while aircraft are still far out, before my options close — because the sequence I choose now is the one that lands. The failure mode I stay awake thinking about is a compression cascade: one slow heavy creates a gap, I pull something in that isn't quite ready, the next pair compresses, someone goes around, three slots disappear. And the aesthetic of good controlling is a sequence that looks inevitable in retrospect — smooth spacing, no wasted gaps, every aircraft touching down at the right energy.

Looking at your setup through that lens, three things genuinely rhyme.

---

**The irreversibility constraint forces decision-making upstream — and yours hasn't moved yet.**

In my sector, a controller who waits until aircraft are on final to think about sequencing has already lost. The skill migrates: you read the situation while aircraft are still 40 miles out and build the sequence in your head before your options close. The structural match to your situation is exact: a glued-up piece cannot be reshot, so your edit is entirely determined by what was captured. But capture is still *before* the commitment point — and right now your capture decisions are made by whoever held the camera, not by the person who will spend six hours in the edit. The method that transfers: during the build, treat the camera as a sequencing tool rather than just a recording device. Before you glue anything, decide what the first 45 seconds of the edit need to show — what detail, what moment — and shoot it explicitly while it still exists. A 30-second tight shot of a joinery fit, or a hand planing the first face of a board, or the jig setup before the cut. Capture the hook shot *as a deliberate act*, not as something you hope ended up somewhere in six hours of footage. You might find it changes what you're willing to reach for during the shoot.

---

**You're missing a separation minimum — a designed intake rate — and that's why the edit load grows.**

In radar control, separation minimum isn't a grudging safety margin. It's the design rule that makes the whole sequence workable: without a floor on spacing, the system is brittle to any variance and collapses under the first delay. I don't try to maximize the number of aircraft; I try to land the maximum number *the floor allows*. Probe that against your pipeline: you currently have no structural constraint on footage volume. Six hours in, 15 minutes out, and no rule that limits how much enters the stream in the first place. The edit time isn't long because you're slow — it's long because there's no minimum gap between capture and cut, so the stream is whatever the cameras produced. A separation minimum analogue would be a hard shoot plan: maximum 90 minutes of total footage, shot against a pre-written sequence of five to seven named shots per video. Not a storyboard — a shot list that maps to the edit structure. This reduces edit time structurally, by designing the intake rate before the shoot, rather than by working faster after. Whether that tradeoff fits your filming style is entirely your call, but in my world the minimum is what makes throughput sustainable.

---

**The viewer's decision to stay is made at a specific gate — and the stabilization cue needs to arrive before it.**

On final approach, aircraft must be stabilized by 1,000 feet above the ground — speed set, power configured, gear down. That gate is specific and non-negotiable: if the energy isn't right there, a go-around is cheaper than a crash. Your retention graph is showing a gate at roughly 45 seconds where 30% of viewers depart. The structural shape is the same: there is a point past which a viewer's decision to stay has already been made, and if the energy — the signal that this video is worth continuing — hasn't been given before that gate, they go around. The thing I notice is that in your current structure, the build hasn't started by that gate. The build *is* the reason the viewer clicked — not the intro, not the title card, not the host talking about what they're going to do. The stabilization cue in my world is concrete and visible: gear down, flaps set, on glide path. The analogue might be that the build needs to be *visible* — not described, visible — before the gate closes. A shot of the finished piece, or the specific problem the jig solves, or the first physical action of the build, landed before 45 seconds. You already know the gate; the question is what signal makes a viewer call it stable. That's yours to answer, not mine.
