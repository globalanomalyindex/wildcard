concept=traffic signal coordination

---

I'm a transportation engineer who designs arterial signal progression — the problem of coordinating dozens of isolated intersections, each running its own timing cycle, so that vehicles released by one signal arrive at the next during a green phase rather than a red. The failure I spend my life preventing is *constructive interference*: cycles that are each rational in isolation but, when they fire together, create a concentrated pulse that overwhelms whatever is downstream.

Your system looks, from where I stand, exactly like an uncoordinated arterial.

---

**Seed 1: Your p99 spike is a temporal concentration problem, not a volume problem.**

In signal engineering, an intersection doesn't go into failure because the daily vehicle count is too high — it fails because a platoon arrives all at once. The total load may be well within capacity; it's the synchronization that concentrates it into a burst the system can't absorb. Your enterprise tenants each have a scheduler, and each scheduler independently picks the top of the hour as its trigger — the salient, round-number time anchor. None of them are coordinating. But the result is coordination: they release simultaneously, and ClickHouse sees a platoon rather than a stream. The p99 doubling isn't evidence that your cluster is undersized for the workload; it's evidence that the workload is temporally concentrated. That's a different diagnosis, and it points toward a different class of fix.

**Seed 2: The green wave is deliberate offset — same load, spread arrivals.**

The signal engineer's solution to platoon interference is not to reduce cars or widen roads. It is to add a *phase offset* between adjacent signals so that the platoon released by one intersection arrives at the next during a green window rather than stacking at a red. Total throughput is unchanged. What changes is when each intersection sees demand. In your world: the fix is not to reduce the total export workload or to provision isolated clusters. It is to spread the scheduled export windows across the hour — offset tenant A to :00, tenant B to :12, tenant C to :24 — so that ClickHouse sees a distributed arrival rather than a synchronized burst. Same total queries. Same infrastructure. Radically different peak demand. One caution that comes with this in my field: a green-wave offset that works for one speed band creates a disadvantaged band at another speed. If you design offsets around your current large tenants, you may create a new collision point elsewhere in the hour as your tenant mix grows. That's worth building some slack into.

**Seed 3: Spontaneous synchronization is the real adversary, and it regenerates.**

Here's what I'd warn you about: cycle-blind intersections will re-synchronize on their own over time. Platoons released at a red queue up, then release together on the green, and arrive at the next intersection as a new coordinated pulse — constructive interference that the signals didn't produce but didn't prevent either. In your problem: even if you broker offset windows for your current enterprise tenants, new tenants added by sales will independently choose the top of the hour again, because it's the obvious default. The offset scheme needs to be the *default* you hand every tenant when they configure a scheduled export, not a one-time negotiation with two large accounts. The mechanism that produced the problem — every scheduler independently picking the salient round number — will keep regenerating the problem unless the default itself changes. An opinionated scheduler UI that offers staggered windows (and makes top-of-hour a choice, not the pre-fill) is the structural fix. That also gives you a place to enforce the constraint without a contract conversation.
