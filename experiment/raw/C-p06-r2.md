Here are four angles from outside the usual secrets-management frame:

---

**1. From wilderness search-and-rescue: the "last known position" doctrine**

SAR teams don't start by covering all terrain equally. They start from the subject's last confirmed location and expand outward. Your laptop-theft panic was a coverage problem — you couldn't establish a last known position for each credential, so you treated everything as lost.

What you might try: before touching any vault tooling, build a simple credential registry — even a Google Sheet — that maps each secret to: where it lives, what it touches, and when it was last verified. The goal isn't perfect secrets management yet. It's giving yourself a "last known position" so the next incident has a bounded blast radius instead of a total recall. This also directly answers SOC 2's inventory requirement without adding local-dev friction.

---

**2. From epidemiology: the index case vs. the contact list**

Outbreak response separates "finding patient zero" from "tracing all contacts." Your two-day staging outage happened because you conflated the two — when you found the exposure (stolen laptop), you rotated everything rather than tracing only what that contractor had touched.

What you might try: model access like exposure tracing. For each human or machine identity, maintain a minimal contact list of which secrets it can reach. When an identity is compromised, you run the contact list, not a global rotation. This reframes the engineer pushback conversation too — you're not adding friction to their day, you're limiting how much of their work gets nuked the next time something goes wrong.

---

**3. From commercial real estate: the master key vs. the tenant key distinction**

Buildings don't give every tenant the master key. They give tenants keys that only open what they need, and the master is rekeyed on a separate, controlled schedule. Your current setup has almost everything at master-key level — contractors, CI, and prod services all pulling from the same pool.

What you might try: before introducing new tooling, do a one-time categorization: which secrets are "tenant keys" (scoped, short-lived, easily replaced) and which are "master keys" (broad access, long-lived, painful to rotate). Tackle the master keys first for vault storage and rotation policy. Leave tenant keys in .env for now to avoid the dev-friction fight — you get most of the security gain with a fraction of the disruption.

---

**4. From archival preservation: the "significant properties" framework**

Archivists trying to preserve digital objects don't attempt to preserve everything perfectly. They identify the significant properties — the ones that must survive for the object to still be itself — and accept degradation on everything else.

What you might try: treat your three-month SOC 2 window the same way. Identify the five to ten credentials where a breach would be genuinely catastrophic (prod database, signing keys, payment processor tokens) and get those into proper secrets management now. For everything else — staging keys, internal tooling, dev tokens — document them, get them out of repo history, and accept "good enough" until after fieldwork. Auditors are looking for evidence that you know your significant properties and have controls on them, not that every .env file is immaculate.
