Here are four angles from outside the usual frame:

**1. Ornithology's "indicator species" logic**

Ecologists do not monitor every species in a forest to assess ecosystem health. They identify a small set of indicator species whose presence or absence reliably signals the state of the whole system. The rest is noise. Your regulated payment-flow paths are the indicator species. Everything else is ambient data. Rather than trying to tune 30 alerts, you could run a short mapping exercise: trace each alert backward to ask whether it has ever been the first signal of a regulated-path failure. Alerts that cannot show that lineage are, by definition, not indicators. They can be demoted to dashboards rather than pages. This reframes the project from "raise thresholds" to "build a verified indicator set," which compliance officers tend to accept more readily because the argument is about signal fidelity, not laxity.

**2. Aviation's "minimum equipment list" discipline**

Commercial aircraft have a document called the MEL: a pre-negotiated, regulator-approved list of systems that can be inoperative without grounding the plane. The key feature of the MEL is that it was built before the crisis, under calm conditions, with explicit sign-off. Your team has the inverse problem: you are doing MEL negotiation during the crisis (the resignation letter, the muted phones), which means decisions are made under duress and without documentation. A practical parallel would be to run a structured "alert MEL" exercise now, in a non-urgent window, where each alert is classified as either "regulated-path-critical" or "advisory." The output is a written, dated document that compliance can review. Because it is prospective and documented rather than reactive and informal, it tends to survive audits better than threshold tweaks do.

**3. Burn medicine's debridement principle**

In treating serious burns, the counterintuitive intervention is aggressive removal of dead tissue to let healthy tissue survive. Leaving necrotic material in place does not protect the wound; it infects it. Your 27 actionless pages a week are necrotic tissue. They are not neutral; they are actively degrading the on-call system by training engineers to treat all pages as probably meaningless. The "raise thresholds carefully" framing treats alert reduction as a risk. The debridement framing treats alert retention as a risk. That reframe may be useful when talking to leadership: you are not proposing to lower your guard, you are proposing to remove the dead material that is currently preventing your guard from functioning.

**4. Staged manufacturing's "andon cord" versus continuous monitoring distinction**

Toyota's production system separates two things that are often conflated in software operations: continuous monitoring (everything is always watched on dashboards) and the andon cord (a human-initiated stop-everything signal for a defect that has actually appeared). The andon cord is pulled rarely and deliberately; it always means "stop the line." Software on-call rotations tend to wire everything directly to the andon cord, making it meaningless. A practical experiment: route your batch-job CPU and disk alerts to a shared channel that is visible but silent overnight. Reserve true pages for alerts that have been mapped, in writing, to a regulated path. Engineers then pull the cord themselves if they notice something in the channel that looks like a real failure. This does not require resolving the classification problem first; it just changes the default interruption level while the classification work proceeds.
