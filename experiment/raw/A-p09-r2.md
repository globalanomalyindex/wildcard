I'm a competitive whitewater slalom course designer. My job is to route multiple athletes through the same moving channel, placing gate-poles that demand precise line choices — all without ever being able to add water or widen the river. The lever I have is the *structure of the demand placed on the shared resource*: gate placement, start-interval timing, and knowledge of how the channel recovers between runs.

Here's what I notice when I look at your problem.

---

**1. Recovery interval as a physical number, not a policy number.**

In slalom, if two athletes run too close together, the upstream athlete's passage disturbs the hydraulic features downstream — eddies are used up, wave trains are destabilized — and the second athlete arrives at a gate in a hydraulically degraded state that no coach, watching from the bank, can see in real time. The fix isn't a wider channel. It's measuring how long each contested hydraulic feature takes to recover to a raceable state, and making that number the floor under start-interval decisions.

Your ClickHouse cluster leaves a shared resource state — cache occupancy, memory pressure, I/O queue depth, CPU scheduling latency — that persists for some window after a heavy export completes. Every tenant who submits a query inside that window arrives at the executor in a degraded state. The interesting thing is that this recovery window is a *physical number* you can measure: replay a representative heavy export against a quiescent cluster and watch how long p99 takes to return to baseline. That curve is your start-interval floor. It tells you not just that spikes are bad, but exactly how wide the blast radius is in time — which is the number that drives defensible export scheduling offsets, and the number that tells you whether two enterprise tenants staggered by fifteen minutes are genuinely clear of each other or still overlapping.

You might find it useful to measure that curve before you build the scheduling policy, rather than after. The policy is only as good as the number underneath it.

---

**2. Shaping the demand profile instead of expanding the channel.**

A course designer who can't add water has one real lever: where and how the gates are placed determines the aggregate hydraulic load athletes place on the channel. Gates in calm eddies reduce the demand per athlete. Gates on fast diagonal crossings amplify it. The designer controls the *profile* of demand, not the channel capacity.

Your enterprise tenants are all currently running their exports at the top of the hour — not because that's technically necessary, but because it's the default cron behavior and no one has given them a reason to do otherwise. The structural move that maps to gate placement isn't rate limiting or cluster isolation; it's export schedule negotiation. If attribution were fixed enough to see which tenants are firing at :00, you could offer them — even just offer — a suggested offset: run your export at :15 or :45 and we'll guarantee you a quieter cluster state. The channel doesn't get wider, but the demand profile across it changes. Tenants who take the offset get something (a more predictable window); you get something (the spikes stop stacking).

The course designer's version of this isn't enforcement; it's course architecture. You might find it worth asking whether the spikes are a capacity problem or a scheduling problem that looks like a capacity problem because no one has examined the shape of the demand.

---

**3. The eddy trap: invisible at course-walk, unavoidable mid-run.**

The failure I fear most is an eddy trap — a gate that looks fine from the bank when the course is walked dry, but creates a hydraulic pocket under race conditions at speed. Athletes discover it only when they're already in it. The cause is always a local feature interacting with the gate demand in a way only visible under realistic conditions, not under dry analysis.

Your attribution gap has this exact structure. The tenant IDs are missing from parts of the request path — but you likely don't know *which* parts, or under what conditions the context is silently dropped, because the system has mostly been analyzed at code level rather than under concurrent multi-tenant load. The missing IDs may behave differently under a heavy export spike than under normal traffic (some context-propagation paths that work fine at low concurrency fail under thread-pool saturation or async boundary crossings). The course designer's specific habit is hydraulic modeling at *race water levels* before gate-setting — you don't walk the course at trickle flow and assume it behaves the same at race volume.

The analog is running your attribution audit under realistic load: replay production traffic (or a representative load approximation) while instrumenting the request path, rather than tracing the code path statically. The eddy traps — the specific points where tenant context drops — are more likely to surface under load than under dry code reading. You might find that some of the inconsistency isn't random carelessness but emerges only under specific concurrency conditions that a static audit wouldn't surface.
