I'm a dendrochronologist — I work on historic building timbers. My whole practice is extracting reliable dates from noisy wood: a single ring-width series from a barn beam is always partly signal (the climate year) and partly noise (that tree's local quirks), and I can only trust a date when multiple independent specimens from the same structure agree. I spend my days cross-dating, gap-flagging, and building master reference chronologies so future timbers can date themselves cheaply against what I've already established. Looking at your situation, four things genuinely rhyme.

---

**Date from the content, not the container.**

In my world, when timbers arrive with no trustworthy provenance records — mislabeled crates, wrong site codes, carpenter's notes in the wrong century — I can't trust the metadata. I have to read the wood itself. Your files named inconsistently (some by date, some by guest, probably some by neither) are the same situation: the filename is unreliable provenance. But the audio is the specimen. A word-level ASR transcription reads the signal directly, independent of what the folder says. If you run two independent transcription passes on a sample — say, Whisper locally and one cloud service — the segments where both agree on wording and timing are your high-confidence anchors. Where they diverge, the signal is locally degraded and that's exactly where you'd want a human ear. The publisher's "exact wording" requirement is a high-stakes claim; two agreeing specimens make it trustworthy in a way one transcript cannot.

---

**Build a master reference first; everything else dates cheaply against it.**

I never re-date a regional timber stock from scratch. I build a master chronology from the most information-dense specimens I have, then match new timbers against it — they borrow the master's precision. You have a hard three-week deadline for a bounded set (the episodes the publisher wants) and an open-ended demand for the rest of the 410. The structural move: don't try to transcribe everything in one sprint. In week one, build a prioritized index of the 30-50 episodes most likely to be licensed — the ones guests have already asked about, recent seasons, high-profile guests — and get those to high confidence. That reference batch teaches you where your ASR tools struggle (proper nouns, technical jargon, the host's verbal habits), so every subsequent batch run gets cheaper to QA. The remaining 360+ episodes become a background job that accumulates into the master rather than a second crisis.

---

**Deliver the timber alongside the date.**

The worst outcome in my field is a false date — two ring series that happen to correlate over a short window and give a plausible-but-wrong construction year. The safeguard isn't just more math; it's keeping the physical specimen. A date without a specimen is unverifiable. For the publisher, a transcript excerpt without a playback-anchored clip is the same thing: plausible-looking text that can't be checked if the wording is disputed. The artifact worth delivering is a timestamp + a short audio clip for each licensed quote — the claim and its specimen together. Any discrepancy between what the transcript says and what the clip plays is catchable before the contract is signed, not after. That's a simple packaging step, but it's the difference between a defensible claim and a contested one.

---

**Run the gap audit before anything else.**

In a ring series, a missing ring is invisible. The series looks continuous; every ring before the gap is silently misdated by one year. My first move with a new timber set is always to count explicitly and flag the gaps before I start correlating. You have 410 known episodes, two per week, four years — the expected file count is something like 416 slots. Before you batch-transcribe, spend an hour inventorying both drives against that expected sequence. You'll likely find files that are duplicated across drives, a few that are genuinely missing or unreadable, and some whose metadata is contradictory. Flagging those explicitly now means the index you deliver to the publisher is complete-minus-known-gaps, not complete-minus-unknown-gaps. The latter will surface as a problem later; the former you can characterize honestly.
