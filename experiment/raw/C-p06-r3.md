Here are four angles from outside the usual "secrets management" frame that might open up new approaches:

**1. The Census Model: count what you have before you manage it**

Epidemiologists running a disease census don't start by treating people — they first map who exists and where. Your rotating-everything-blindly event was a symptom of having no inventory. Before choosing a vault or a policy, try a pure discovery pass: a script that scans git history (tools like truffleHog or gitleaks in audit-only mode), CI variable lists, and Heroku config vars, and outputs a flat spreadsheet — credential, service it unlocks, last-rotation date, who knows it. No changes, just counting. SOC 2 auditors will actually ask for this artifact anyway, so you get double use out of it. The insight: the contractor-laptop incident wasn't a rotation problem, it was a map problem.

**2. The Blast Radius Mental Model from Circuit Design**

Electrical engineers designing for fault tolerance think in blast radii: they physically isolate subsystems so a short in one section can't cascade. Applied here — instead of treating all credentials as equally catastrophic to rotate, you might scope credentials by blast radius: "this key, if stolen, can only write to one S3 bucket in staging." Short-lived credentials (AWS IAM roles via OIDC, Heroku's buildpack-level env injection) shrink blast radius because they expire before an attacker can pivot. The engineer friction objection often melts when you reframe it as "this token is only valid for your current session" rather than "we're adding a new tool." You might try proposing OIDC-based short-lived AWS credentials for CI first — it removes a static secret entirely and is invisible to local dev.

**3. The Kitchen Mise en Place Principle for Local Dev**

Professional kitchens solved the "every chef sets up differently" problem not with rules but with mise en place — a standard prep state that every station starts from. The engineering friction complaint is usually about setup time, not about security itself. You could adopt a project-level `.env.example` file with fake-but-valid-format values, paired with a one-command bootstrapper (a Makefile target or a short shell script) that pulls real dev credentials from a single source (a dev-only vault path, or AWS SSM Parameter Store with a shared read-only IAM role). Local dev never touches production credentials; the bootstrapper is the only thing that changes. Engineers run `make dev-env` once and get a working local setup — friction drops, and you get a single audit trail for who pulled dev credentials.

**4. The Probate Analogy: credential succession planning**

Estate lawyers think about what happens when the keyholder is suddenly unavailable — who has standing to access what, and in what order. Your contractor-laptop scenario is exactly this: an unexpected loss of a keyholder with unknown credential scope. A lightweight "succession document" — not a full CMDB, just a one-pager per service saying "these are the credentials this service needs, they live here, this person owns rotation" — would have made that incident a thirty-minute exercise instead of a two-day outage. The SOC 2 connection is direct: auditors often ask for evidence of ownership and rotation procedures. A living succession doc doubles as that evidence and as your incident runbook, without requiring you to change any tooling immediately.
