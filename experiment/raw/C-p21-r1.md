Here are four angles from outside the usual frame that might help:

---

**1. Whisper as a night-shift worker**

Transcription feels like a bottleneck because you're imagining it as a manual process that competes with your editing time. But OpenAI's Whisper (and several hosted wrappers) runs locally and handles batches unattended. The connection: your two external drives with inconsistent naming are actually an asset here, not a liability. A shell script can walk any folder structure, find audio files by extension rather than name, and feed them to Whisper one by one while you sleep. You'd wake to timestamped transcripts. For the publisher's three-week deadline, you could prioritize the episodes most likely to contain quotable material by running Whisper only on guests you remember being particularly sharp, rather than all 410.

---

**2. The 250 MB cap as a forcing function, not a wall**

Your hosting plan caps uploads, but transcripts are plain text — a full-hour transcript is roughly 60-80 KB. You could host a searchable transcript archive somewhere entirely separate from your audio (a static site on Netlify, a Notion database, even a public GitHub repo) without touching your audio hosting at all. The angle borrowed from publishing: indexes and tables of contents have always lived apart from the primary text. The transcript layer doesn't need to be coupled to the audio delivery layer. A guest searching for what they said could use the transcript archive; the audio itself stays where it is.

---

**3. The card catalog problem (solved 1876)**

Libraries faced exactly your problem at scale before digital search: vast catalogs of items, inconsistent provenance, no unified index. Their solution was to separate the finding aid from the item itself, and to staff the cataloging project as a one-time retrospective push rather than waiting to retrofit items as they came in. The practical suggestion: treat the 410-episode backlog as an archival project with a defined scope, not an ongoing maintenance task. A short burst of parallel effort — even two or three evenings using automated transcription on the highest-priority episodes — creates a usable index. You don't need all 410 before the publisher's deadline; you need the searchable layer to exist somewhere so you can query it.

---

**4. The legal deposition transcript model**

Court reporters produce verbatim transcripts with timestamps because lawyers need to cite exact moments. Their workflow doesn't require the reporter to also understand the subject matter — just accurate capture plus time-indexing. That separation suggests something: you don't need to read or curate the transcripts yourself to make them useful for the publisher. A raw Whisper output, even with occasional errors, is searchable and includes word-level timestamps. The publisher's "exact wording" requirement could be satisfied by giving them the raw transcript alongside the audio timestamp so they can verify the few quotes they actually want to use — rather than you needing to produce a polished, corrected transcript for all 410 episodes.
