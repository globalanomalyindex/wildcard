I work in antivenom production — specifically the milking and serum-quality end of the pipeline, where the lens is signals-and-noise. My obsession: when you milk a viper's venom gland, you want a sample that represents what that animal actually produces. The venom is the signal; everything else in the chain — stress response, temperature, inconsistent handler technique, undocumented storage — is an enemy of it. What makes this work hard is that degradation is often invisible at the sample level. A compromised batch can look fine until it fails efficacy testing downstream, long after you have committed to the production run.

Three things I noticed looking at your situation:

**The signal is intact; the transformation layer is missing.** In my field, the venom exists in the snake — the information is there — but it is completely inaccessible until a specific extraction step runs. There is no shortcut and no partial substitute. Your audio is structurally identical: 410 episodes of signal sitting on two drives, fully intact and fully inaccessible, because the transformation from audio to searchable text has never been run. The gap is total, not partial. What transfers from my field: the priority order matters less than just starting the transformation pipeline, because everything downstream — the publisher's timestamps, the guest self-service lookups, any future use — is gated on a step that has not happened yet and cannot be paralleled around. The first pressure in my work is always: get the venom into the tube, cold, before anything else.

**High-priority specimens get processed before the general colony.** I cannot milk all 410 snakes in a week. When I have a deadline — a pharmaceutical partner needs a specific species' serum for a production run — I triage. The high-priority specimens go through first; the general colony waits. Before I can triage, though, I need a colony map: which cage holds which animal, what their individual baselines look like. Without that map I cannot sequence at all. Your naming inconsistency (some files by date, some by guest) is the colony-map problem: before you can prioritize which episodes the publisher needs, you need to know what you actually have. In my world the fix is a fast survey — not a full analysis, just an inventory — that produces a lookup table: cage, species, last milking date, expected yield. The transfer here is to do a lightweight metadata pass over both drives first, building a flat index (filename, folder, approximate duration, detectable date or guest name from the path) before running a single transcription job. That index is cheap to produce and it is what lets you sequence the publisher's specific episodes to the front of the queue without thrashing.

**Chain of custody must be embedded in the extraction output, not reconstructed afterward.** Every batch of venom I produce carries a provenance record: which snake, which date, which handler, what the storage conditions were. If a downstream quality failure occurs, I trace it back through that record. An undated, unattributed batch is useless for linkage — it satisfies the extraction step but fails at accountability. Your publisher's requirement is a chain-of-custody problem in the exact same sense: they need timestamps and exact wording, which is a provenance claim — this quote came from this file, at this offset, and here is the evidence trail. A transcript without word-level timestamps satisfies the "we have text" check but fails the accountability check, the same way an undated venom sample fails efficacy linkage. What transfers: when you choose a transcription tool or service, the output format matters as much as the accuracy. Word-level or segment-level timestamps embedded in the output file are not a nicety — they are the chain-of-custody record the publisher is actually asking for, and retrofitting them from a plain-text transcript is significantly harder than getting them right the first time.
