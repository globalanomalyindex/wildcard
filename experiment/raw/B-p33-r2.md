I study what rock preserves - specifically, the quantitative signals locked in fossil assemblages. Isotope ratios, bone wear patterns, shell thickness gradients. My obsession is this: you can never observe the past event directly, but if you know which physical traces it leaves, and how to measure them, you can reconstruct it with real precision. I spend most of my time fighting two problems: what the record *systematically omits*, and what indirect signals are *causally connected* to the thing I actually want to know. Looking at your operation, several structural things genuinely rhyme.

---

**The van's contents are a fossil record of dispatch history - and nobody is reading it.**

In my field, the physical state of a specimen right now encodes everything that happened to it: which stresses it bore, which environments it passed through, which demands were placed on it. A femur's cortical thickness is a log of the loads applied over a lifetime. The mix of parts currently on each of your nine vans is the same kind of record - it encodes every job dispatched, every part consumed, every restocking decision made since the last full inventory. That record is sitting there physically, measurable, and right now it's being read as nothing more than "what's available today." If you measured van contents systematically across a full season - what's depleted fastest, what accumulates untouched, which parts appear on zero trucks after a surge week - you'd be reading the fossil of your own demand history. The signal is already preserved. It just needs someone treating it as data rather than as current state.

---

**Your parts-room count is taphonomically biased - the things hardest to count are the ones that matter most.**

Fossil preservation is never random. Hard, mineralized structures survive; soft tissue decays. What ends up in the record is not a fair sample of the original organism - it's the survivorship-biased subset that preservation favored. The same structure is running in your parts room. Items that are large, bulky, or already organized on shelves are probably counted with reasonable accuracy. The small, fast-moving, easy-to-misplace components - capacitors, specific control boards - are exactly the ones taphonomy would delete from a manual count. They're the soft tissue of your inventory: consumed quickly, restocked informally, rarely reconciled. Treating all inventory counts as equally reliable is the equivalent of reconstructing a whole organism from only its teeth and assuming that's a complete picture. The high-stakes parts are systematically underrepresented in whatever count you trust. That's worth knowing before you try to fix the count - because the fix for taphonomic bias isn't better counting of what's already preserved; it's finding a different medium of preservation for what's being lost.

---

**Cross-referencing nine partial records reconstructs the demand distribution that no single van contains.**

Paleontologists reconstructing a vanished ecosystem rarely have one complete specimen. They have many partial ones - some with skulls, some with limb bones, some with only teeth. The whole picture emerges from the assemblage. Each of your nine technicians holds a partial record of what your service territory actually demands. The parts that Tech 3 ran out of last Tuesday and Tech 7 ran out of last Thursday are both samples from the same underlying distribution. Right now those misses live in dispatch notes, or in a callback record, or nowhere. If you pooled the "arrived without the right part" events across all nine vans for a full summer, you'd have an assemblage - and from that assemblage you could read the demand distribution far more accurately than from any single technician's experience. The key move in paleontology is recognizing that absence from one specimen is not absence from the ecosystem. A part you've never stocked might be missing from your callback data not because nobody needs it, but because those calls got coded differently, or deferred, or refused. You might find it useful to audit not just the second-visit rate but the *category* of parts that caused it - the assemblage tells you what the territory demands, not just what the trucks happened to carry.

---

**You can measure proxies for parts demand before the truck rolls - the same logic as isotopic reconstruction.**

I cannot measure Cretaceous ocean temperature directly. But I can measure oxygen-18 ratios in foraminifera shells, which are causally connected to paleotemperature in a known way. I get the number I actually want by measuring something that preserved it indirectly. Your dispatch system faces the same inaccessibility: you cannot know exactly which parts a given address needs before a technician arrives. But there are signals causally connected to parts demand that *are* measurable in advance - equipment age (installation permit records, or just asking at intake), brand and model if you capture it at scheduling, prior service history for that address, or even the age of the neighborhood's housing stock. These aren't perfect predictors, but neither is my isotope ratio a perfect thermometer. The question isn't whether the proxy is perfect - it's whether the causal connection is real enough that measuring it shifts the prior on what to load before departure. Even rough age-of-equipment data would let dispatch make a probabilistic call: this is a 2003 unit in a neighborhood of 2003 builds - probability of needing a specific capacitor goes up, load accordingly. You're treating each dispatch as informationally empty before arrival. It isn't. You might find it useful to ask what signals are already accessible at intake that are causally upstream of parts demand.
