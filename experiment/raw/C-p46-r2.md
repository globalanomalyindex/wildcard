Here are four angles from outside the usual frame:

**1. The field naturalist's specimen log**

Naturalists don't save observations as scattered field notes — they create a single standardized record card per specimen: common name, date, location, conditions, what made it notable. What you noticed about that dinner three weeks ago (everyone liked it) is exactly the kind observation a field naturalist would have flagged in a card. You might try treating each meal you actually cook, not each recipe you save, as the specimen. A simple note per cooked meal — dish name, rough date, who ate it, one-line verdict — creates a personal "what I actually made" log that's far smaller and more searchable than a recipe archive. The grandmother's binder already works this way: those cards represent things she actually cooked, not things she bookmarked.

**2. A pharmacist's formulary instead of a library**

Libraries collect everything; a formulary is a curated short list of what your practice actually uses, with substitutions noted. Hospital pharmacists don't stock every drug — they maintain an approved list and reorder from it. Your spice-rebuying problem and the "can't find the hit from three weeks ago" problem are both symptoms of having a library when you need a formulary. You might try building a small "active rotation" of 15-20 recipes your household has actually eaten and liked — the ones that make the formulary. The formulary gets shopped from; the archive can stay scattered. Sunday planning becomes "which formulary recipe fits this week" rather than a search.

**3. Version control's "tagged release" logic**

Software projects accumulate hundreds of commits, but developers tag specific ones as releases — stable, tested, worth returning to. The commit (saving a recipe) is cheap; the tag (this one worked) is rare and meaningful. You might apply a similar two-tier system: saving remains frictionless and scattered, but you add a single lightweight "release" action when something actually succeeds at the table. A shared note, a photo with a caption, a text to yourself — anything that marks it as tagged. The constraint is that tagging only happens post-meal, not at bookmark time, which filters for real success rather than anticipated interest.

**4. The theater production's "prop bible"**

Stage managers keep a prop bible: not every prop that exists, but every prop that's needed for this production, with a photo, a source, and a note on substitutions. It's generated backwards from the script, not forward from a collection. Your Sunday shopping problem might benefit from the same inversion — instead of planning meals and then deriving a list, you could start from the ingredients you reliably have or want to rotate through, and let the meal plan surface from that. The gluten-free constraint and the spice inventory both become inputs to the prop bible rather than exceptions to manage around a recipe search. A standing "pantry snapshot" (even a quick phone photo of your spice shelf each Sunday) gives you the prop bible's source-of-truth column without requiring a dedicated app.
