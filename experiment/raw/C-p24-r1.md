Here are four angles from outside the usual YouTube-creator frame:

**1. The "storyboard backward" method from documentary film editing**

Documentary editors often have no reshoots either — they work entirely with what was captured. Their standard practice is to edit the film in reverse: identify the most striking moment in all six hours of footage first, then build the opening around that moment rather than around the chronological start of the build. Your retention problem may be a structural one — the 45-second cliff is where people hit a "weather report" opening (setup, context, backstory) before the storm. You might try pulling a single 10-second clip of something visually surprising or physically tense from deep in the footage — a split-second mistake, grain swirling under a hand plane, a glue-up under pressure — and leading with it cold, before any narration. Let the first image be the thesis.

**2. The A/B testing logic used in direct-response copywriting**

You have a multi-variable diagnosis problem (thumbnails, titles, topics), and you're trying to read it from impression data, which conflates all three. Direct-response writers, when they can't isolate variables, use the "one constant" rule: hold two variables fixed and change only one across a run of five pieces, then read the delta. You can apply this without changing your production: keep your next three thumbnails nearly identical in composition and style (same face crop, same color zone), vary only titles, and treat the impression-to-click-through ratio as your signal. This won't tell you whether topics are the problem, but it will separate thumbnail noise from title noise, which you currently can't do.

**3. The "pre-mortem" framing from decision science**

Psychologist Gary Klein's pre-mortem asks: imagine the project already failed — what killed it? Applied here: before you upload each video, run a five-minute pre-mortem on the first 90 seconds specifically. Watch it cold, on your phone, with the sound off. If you can't tell what the video is about and why it matters to you within those 90 seconds from visuals alone, the opening likely won't survive the silent-autoplay scroll. This sidesteps the reshoot problem entirely — it's a gate you apply in the edit, not the garage.

**4. The signal/noise ratio idea from radio engineering**

Radio engineers define useful signal as the part of a transmission that carries new information — and noise as anything repeated or predictable. Your editing-time problem might be a capture problem in disguise: six hours of footage for a 15-minute video implies a roughly 24:1 ratio, most of which is noise by definition (repeated takes, setup, dead time). Some woodworking channels that film in constrained windows use a "one-clip-per-step" discipline before they start filming — they write down the 12 to 15 discrete steps of the build and commit to capturing each exactly once, with no safety takes. This doesn't sacrifice quality; it reframes confidence in your craft as the editorial filter, not the timeline. The editing time compresses dramatically because there are simply fewer decisions.
