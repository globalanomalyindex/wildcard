I'm a radio interferometrist. My obsession is what happens when you separate two antennas by tens of kilometers and combine their signals: the resolving power of the array comes entirely from the *baseline* — the spatial separation — not from what either dish can do alone. A single large dish can't see what two small dishes far apart can see together. The information lives in the *difference* between them, not in either one individually.

Three things from that world seem to genuinely rhyme with what you're describing.

---

**Seed 1: Resolution is a network property, not a per-node property.**

In my world, upgrading one antenna doesn't improve resolution — only adding baselines does. The resolving power belongs to the *arrangement*, not to any individual aperture.

The mapping: right now your nine vans are treated as nine independent units, each trying to be self-sufficient. But the capability to resolve any given job on first contact is actually a property of the *fleet's arrangement* — which parts are at which van, relative to where jobs appear. A van near the depot holding a capacitor that another van two streets from the job site doesn't have is a failed baseline. The "resolution" of the fleet (its first-contact completion rate) is currently set by inventory isolation, not by fleet size.

In my world, the move is to think of coverage as a network problem: what baseline configurations maximize the chance that *some* node near any given job has the right part? You might find it useful to ask which parts could be held at one central van or a few designated "deep inventory" trucks rather than spread thinly across all nine — so that any job in the metro is within reach of the right part, even if not on the nearest truck. Entirely your call how that maps.

---

**Seed 2: The image is reconstructed from correlations — and sparse, biased sampling produces phantom beliefs.**

My array never directly observes the source. I reconstruct it from the *correlation* of signals across antenna pairs. Each observation is a sample of the source's Fourier transform at a particular spatial frequency. If my sampling is sparse or systematically biased, the reconstructed image has sidelobes — phantom structure that looks real but isn't.

The mapping: your fleet's true parts inventory — what's actually on each van — is never directly observed either. You reconstruct it from inference: what you stocked, what you think was used, what failed to close a job. If the only outcomes you log are failures (second visits), you're sampling the parts-availability function sparsely and on the worst-case end. The "image" you reconstruct — the mental model of what each van typically carries — will have sidelobes: confident beliefs about stock that haven't been validated in months.

In interferometry, the fix for sidelobes is denser, better-distributed sampling: observe more baselines, in more orientations. In your world, the analog is logging every job outcome — not just the failures, but which part was used, which was substituted, which was absent and why — so that the true distribution of parts demand and parts presence can actually be reconstructed. A parts room with no trusted count is a Fourier plane sampled at three points; you're then trying to invert it into an image. You might find it worth treating every truck return as a measurement event.

---

**Seed 3: Decorrelation under surge, and the calibration-source fix.**

The thing I fear most is decorrelation: when the signals from two antennas drift out of phase coherence — due to atmospheric turbulence, or a clock drifting between them — the interference no longer encodes the source. It encodes the drift. The measurement is corrupted precisely when baseline separation is greatest, because the longer the path, the more the atmosphere can scramble it. My fix is a *phase reference*: a bright, compact, well-understood calibration source I observe frequently throughout the night, using it to solve for the current drift and correct all other observations.

The mapping: summer surge is a decorrelation event. Your system has an implicit phase reference baked in — the assumption that four calls per day, the current parts mix, current travel distances, are the operating reality. When volume doubles, that reference drifts. The dispatch queue keeps running on the old assumption; techs end up far from their usual zones; parts consumption shifts with job mix; nothing is where the mental model says it should be.

In my world, more frequent calibration observations protect against this: rather than calibrating once at the start of the night and trusting it, I revisit the calibrator every 20 minutes. The dispatch analog might be a short weekly re-calibration during surge season: re-examine which zones each tech actually served last week, re-stock the highest-failure parts, and adjust territories before the drift accumulates rather than after it's already visible in the wait-time queue. Your call whether that cadence or a different one makes sense for how surge actually builds in your area.
