I work in tribology — specifically, quantitative wear-scar characterization using pin-on-disc testing. My day is measuring profilometry traces of wear scars, computing scar volume as a function of sliding cycles, and figuring out exactly where and why a material crossed from mild wear into severe wear. The mechanism I think about constantly: wear rate isn't a constant — it transitions sharply at a threshold contact pressure, and the system's behavior before and after that threshold is qualitatively different, not just quantitatively. My characteristic fear is running a test where the material crossed that transition mid-run without my noticing, so the scar volume I measured integrates two completely different damage mechanisms, and every engineering decision downstream of it is wrong.

Two things in your situation genuinely rhyme with what I see at the tribology bench.

---

**The scar is the wrong instrument for detecting the transition — you need the friction proxy**

In pin-on-disc work, the wear scar tells me the damage *after* it's accumulated. By the time scar volume diverges catastrophically (the severe-wear regime), the surface has already failed — I'm reading the outcome, not the event. So I don't monitor scar volume during the run; I monitor friction coefficient continuously, because the mild-to-severe transition shows up as a step-change in friction *before* the scar deepens. Friction is the leading indicator; scar volume is the lagging one.

Your p99 latency is the scar. It integrates damage that's already happened — queries that queued, blocked, and resolved. The transition event — the moment the cluster crosses from "absorbing the load" into "threads are starving and blocking each other" — has an earlier signal. Queue depth, IO wait, buffer pool pressure, merge-tree stall events in ClickHouse: these rise *before* p99 doubles. Instrumenting those and alarming at their threshold would give you lead time the latency metric doesn't. In my world, discovering this meant I could stop the machine before catastrophic material loss; in yours it might mean you can shed or defer load from the non-SLA tenants before the SLA window blows, rather than discovering the violation in the SLA report.

---

**An unattributed scar is scientifically unusable**

After a multi-phase test, the wear scar I measure is a palimpsest: early mild-wear contribution, a transition zone, and possibly a severe-wear tail, all superimposed in the same geometry. If I average them into a single specific wear rate, I've conflated incompatible mechanisms and the number means nothing — it doesn't predict field life, it doesn't tell me which material to change, and it definitely doesn't tell me who to blame (the pin or the disc). Attribution — knowing which part of the scar came from which regime, and from which material — is the prerequisite for any engineering decision worth making. The characterization isn't optional; it's what makes the measurement useful.

Your p99 latency is the same unattributed scar. It integrates contributions from SLA-tier customers, casual tenants, and the hammer-blows of scheduled batch exports — all compressed into one number. You cannot triage it, because you cannot decompose it. The tenant-ID threading problem you describe is, structurally, the failure to lay down attribution channels throughout the run. In my work, this means instrumenting multiple channels from the start — time-resolved friction during the run, SEM of debris afterward, profilometry cross-sections at multiple positions — because you cannot reconstruct attribution retroactively from the scar alone. In your world, that means threading tenant ID uniformly through the request path before you try to build rate-limiting or SLA enforcement on top of it. The decomposition isn't a nice-to-have once you want to add limits; it's what makes any limit decision based on real mechanism rather than guessing which tenant to squeeze.
