I'm a forensic evidence specialist — specifically the kind who gets called when a chain-of-custody record has a gap and a defense attorney is asking uncomfortable questions about it. My whole craft is built around one counterintuitive lesson: the goal is not to prevent all incidents; it is to ensure that when an incident occurs, you can answer every question about it cleanly, quickly, and without guessing. The mechanism I think about every day is the custody log — a prospective, timestamped record of every transfer event. When that log exists, an incident is bounded and answerable. When it doesn't, you're in the worst possible position: not because something bad definitely happened, but because you can't prove it didn't.

Your credential situation maps onto mine in a few places that I think are worth looking at.

---

**The prospective/retrospective asymmetry, and why your incident cost what it did.**

In my field, a chain of custody only works if custody events are logged at the moment they happen — not assembled from memory after something goes wrong. Retrospective reconstruction is always incomplete and always questioned, because the people doing the reconstructing have an interest in the outcome and imperfect recall.

Your stolen-laptop incident was a prospective/retrospective failure in exactly this sense: you had no running custody log for your credentials, so when you needed to answer "which credentials did this contractor hold copies of," you had to reconstruct that from memory and guesswork. That reconstruction was incomplete enough that you couldn't bound the blast radius — so you treated every credential as potentially compromised and rotated almost everything. The two days of staging outages weren't the cost of a breach; they were the cost of not knowing whether you'd had one. The incident revealed a bill you'd been running up silently: the deferred cost of having no prospective record.

The thing that transfers here: prospective custody logging is cheap per-event and expensive to start; retrospective reconstruction is free to defer and catastrophic to execute. Your team has been implicitly choosing the latter, and the laptop incident showed you what that trade costs. The question worth asking — entirely your call — is whether there's a natural moment in your existing workflow where a custody event already happens that you could instrument without adding friction.

---

**Unknowability of custody state forces worst-case assumption, and the cost scales with surface area.**

In forensics, if the chain is broken at any point, it doesn't matter that the evidence probably wasn't tampered with — you can't prove it wasn't, so you must treat it as if it was. The legal standard is binary. The cost of that forced worst-case assumption scales with how many items have broken chains.

Your situation has the same structure. When a credential's history is unknown — who generated it, who was issued a copy, whether that copy was revoked when the person left — you're forced into the worst-case assumption (rotate it) upon any incident. The more credentials with unknown custody state, the higher the cost of each incident. You rotated "almost everything" not because almost everything was compromised, but because almost everything's custody state was unknown. The remediation cost is a direct function of un-custodied surface area.

This has a practical implication that might be useful: you don't need to fix the whole system before your SOC 2 fieldwork. If you could establish custody records for even your highest-blast-radius credentials — production database passwords, signing certificates, external API keys — you'd shrink the "must rotate everything" surface area dramatically. The question I'd ask in my world: which secrets, if their custody state were unknown during an incident, would force the most expensive response? Start the log there.

---

**The item and its history are separate objects of record — and most tooling only stores the item.**

A physical evidence bag contains the item. The chain-of-custody form is a separate document that records the item's history: who collected it, under what conditions, every transfer since. You can have the item in perfect condition and have lost the form. Those are independent failures, and losing the form is often just as damaging.

Your vault (1Password, or whatever you're using) is the evidence bag — it stores the current value of the credential. But it probably doesn't store the credential's history: which developer originally generated it, which CI systems or developer machines have ever had a copy issued, when it was last rotated, what services depend on it. The vault knows what the secret is; it doesn't know where it's been. When the contractor's laptop was stolen, the vault was intact — but the "form" for many credentials didn't exist. The gap was in the history, not the item.

In my world the fix isn't a better evidence bag; it's a better form, filled out at every transfer. The transfer-point equivalent in your world might be: credential issuance (when a developer or system is granted access), credential consumption (when a service is wired to use a secret), and offboarding (when access is revoked). The interesting question — again, yours to pursue or not — is whether your existing infrastructure already emits events at those moments that you could log without touching developer workflow at all.

---

**Handoff protocols, not surveillance — and why this matters for engineer buy-in.**

Chain of custody isn't achieved by watching every person who touches evidence all the time. It's achieved through handoff protocols: moments of explicit transfer where custody is acknowledged and the record is updated. The log is built through the normal handling of objects, not through monitoring. An officer doesn't consent to surveillance; they sign a form when they receive something and sign one when they hand it off.

Your engineers push back on friction in local development, and I suspect some of the friction they're resisting feels surveillance-like — controls that monitor what they do, not controls that instrument what already happens. Handoff-protocol-style controls are structurally different: they fire at natural transfer moments (vault issuance, CI secret injection, offboarding) rather than continuously monitoring developer machines. A credential issuance event in your vault already happens; it might already emit a log you could route somewhere. The developer's dotfile isn't monitored — but the moment they were granted the secret is potentially instrumentable without touching their machine at all.

Whether that distinction lands with your engineers is entirely your read. In my world, the question I'd ask is: where do transfer events already happen in your system, and what would it cost to capture them?
