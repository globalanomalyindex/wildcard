concept=queuing under non-stationary arrival bursts with priority routing

---

I think about baggage claim and airport ground operations — not a glamorous corner of logistics, but one that has been stress-tested by exactly your structural problem: a fixed-capacity service process hit by a burst of arrivals, where the record system and the physical state are two different things, and where aggregate metrics routinely lie about individual-item health.

Here is what I see in your yard.

---

**Your Saturday morning is a burst problem, and you are staffed for average load.**

In airport operations, a flight landing empties at once. If you staff the baggage belt for the average hourly volume across the day, the burst from a single widebody will grow your queue faster than you can clear it, and the tail of that queue extends well into the next hour. The fix is never "hire for average" — it is pre-staged burst capacity: extra handlers called in only for the known landing window, a cleared holding area already prepped, a processing checklist already in hand.

Your 7–9am Saturday window is structurally identical. Two mechanics staffed for average daily throughput cannot absorb a burst of equipment returns in two hours. The queue that builds Saturday morning does not clear Saturday — it bleeds into Monday, and the "machines sitting marked available" problem is partly a burst-residue problem, not a chronic staffing problem. The move that transfers: design the Saturday intake around the burst, not around average-day capacity. That might mean a partial pre-inspection protocol, a triage-at-gate that separates "obviously clean and undamaged, needs a 5-minute sign-off" from "fuel low / damage dispute, needs full inspection" — decoupling the fast path from the slow path so the burst does not force everything into the same queue.

---

**FIFO service at a bottleneck under burst load systematically starves slow-to-process items. Your excavators are in that tail.**

Airports learned this with oversized or heavy bags: under first-in-first-out handling at a busy belt, items that take longer to move (large, awkward, heavy) accumulate dwell time and keep getting deferred while smaller items pass them. Without a rotation rule, they sit. The structural property is not that nobody wants to handle them — it is that FIFO service plus burst load plus fixed throughput mathematically pushes the slow-service items to the back of every queue, indefinitely.

Your three excavators that have not turned in weeks are almost certainly in this tail. They are not failing to rent — they may simply be the item class that keeps getting deferred during intake triage, pushed behind the faster-cycling skid steers and lifts that fill the queue during returns bursts. Aggregate utilization looks fine because the fast-cycling assets carry the numbers.

The transfer from airport priority routing is not about importance — it is about a rotation guarantee: a rule that says no asset class waits more than N days without an inspection pass and a status review, regardless of what else is in queue. This is a hard scheduling constraint, not a good intention, and it costs nothing in throughput capacity.

---

**Your system is treating the return receipt as proof of inspected availability. Those are two different facts.**

Every airport bags manifest says all 210 bags from flight 238 are present before a single one has been scanned. If the connection board updates to "carousel 4" on manifest receipt rather than physical scan, it makes promises against phantom certainty. When three bags are still in the hold, passengers go to carousel 4 and wait. Your counter staff are doing the same thing: promising units against the return receipt rather than the inspection close-out, because the system updates availability on return, not on pass.

The structural fix is a gate, not a note. The unit cannot reach available state in the system until an inspection record closes it — not just a return timestamp, but a sign-off. The manifest and the physical state have to be decoupled as two separate data events. In practice this might be as thin as a required "inspection pass" tap in whatever system you use before the status flips, rather than the current implicit flip on return. The constraint is the intervention; the cultural practice of "we should inspect before promising" will not survive burst pressure.

---

**Process quality degrades under burst load unless the process is pre-staged as a constraint, not a norm.**

In airport ground operations, the fix for inconsistent bag handling — missing damage tags, wrong carousel assignments, incorrect weight entries — is not training during the burst. The burst is when cognitive load is highest and discretionary behavior collapses. The fix is a process pre-staged before the burst arrives: damage tags already in the handler's pocket, a mandatory form that must be tapped through before a bag can be cleared, a fixed photo angle marked on the belt floor.

Your check-in photo inconsistency is the same failure mode. The photos are inconsistent because the protocol is invoked from memory under the pressure of a return pileup, and memory-invoked discretionary steps get compressed or skipped. Pre-staging the intake checklist as a mandatory form — opened on the device before the customer pulls in, with fixed prompts ("fuel gauge photo," "left side photo," "damage declaration") that must be completed before the return can be logged — means the burst pressure doesn't degrade documentation quality. The form is the constraint; the norm is what you currently have.
