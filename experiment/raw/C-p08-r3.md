Here are four angles from outside the usual on-call frame:

---

**1. Borrowed from epidemiology: specificity vs. sensitivity tradeoffs in screening**

In disease screening, a test that catches everything also flags enormous numbers of healthy people, burning out the people who have to follow up. Epidemiologists solved this by separating *screening* (sensitive, noisy, cheap) from *confirmation* (specific, expensive, only triggered by screening). Your alert system is currently conflating both steps. You might try treating the CPU/disk threshold alerts as screening-only: they page a queue or dashboard, not a human phone, unless a second confirmation condition fires within a short window (say, the same host AND a payment-flow latency spike). The regulated paths get their own separate, high-specificity alert channel that never shares a queue with the routine noise. The key question this angle opens up: which alerts are meant to *detect* vs. which are meant to *demand action*?

---

**2. Borrowed from aviation: the distinction between a caution light and a warning light**

Commercial cockpits use two very different interruption levels. A *caution* (amber) says something is out of normal range; a pilot notes it and monitors. A *warning* (red, with audio) says act now or the plane falls. Many airline incidents were traced to pilots ignoring real warnings because they were habituated to a cockpit full of amber caution lights. Your engineers' muted phones are exactly that habituation pattern. This angle suggests a structural two-tier notification system rather than a threshold change: the noisy batch-job alerts become caution-level (visible on a board, no phone wake), while only the compliance-mapped payment-flow alerts fire as warnings to phones. You don't touch the thresholds at all, just the delivery mechanism. The compliance requirement is preserved exactly because the warning tier is reserved for the things that actually correspond to regulated paths.

---

**3. Borrowed from animal behavior: the concept of a "search image"**

Predators and foragers develop a search image - a mental template for what a real target looks like - that makes them dramatically faster and more accurate at finding prey while ignoring non-prey. Your team currently has no shared search image for "a page that requires action." Nobody can say which alerts protect regulated paths because no one has built that template. A practical version of this: pull the postmortem log for the one-in-ten actionable pages, look for what distinguishes them (time of day, co-occurring signals, which specific host, which payment flow), and write those patterns up as a one-page decision tree that goes into the runbook. The search image doesn't change alerting at all initially - it just lets people recognize real signals faster and builds the institutional knowledge needed to justify threshold changes later.

---

**4. Borrowed from manufacturing: statistical process control and "tampering"**

W. Edwards Deming showed that one of the most common factory mistakes is *tampering*: adjusting a process every time output varies, when the variation is actually normal. The adjustment itself introduces instability. Your current alerting on per-host CPU during routine batch jobs sounds like a system that was calibrated for anomaly detection but is now firing on predictable, scheduled, normal-range process variation. The insight from SPC is to separate *common cause* variation (expected, structural) from *special cause* variation (unexpected, worth investigating). Batch jobs hitting CPU thresholds on schedule are common cause. You might try mapping your alert calendar against your batch job schedule and suppressing or downgrading alerts during known batch windows on known hosts. This is not raising a threshold globally - it is time-windowed and host-scoped, so it leaves the compliance-mapped paths untouched. It also generates a concrete artifact (the batch-job/alert overlap map) that starts answering the question of which alerts actually protect regulated paths.
