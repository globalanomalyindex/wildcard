Here are four angles from outside the usual pricing-and-growth frame:

**1. The "wrong instrument" insight from music pedagogy**

Music teachers have a name for a student who quits not because they lack talent but because they started on the wrong instrument for their hand size or learning style. The quit is diagnostic, not terminal. What you're seeing in exit surveys ("felt tricked") is that your trial is the wrong instrument for your actual customer's mental model. The angle to try: instead of changing the pricing unit (blocked), change what the trial measures and surfaces. During the trial, show users a live dashboard panel that translates host count into requests and projects forward: "At your current traffic, you'd need X hosts at $Y/mo." You're not changing the pricing unit, you're building a real-time interpreter that speaks the customer's language back at them. The trial becomes a tutor, not a trap.

**2. Cartography's problem of the "wrong projection"**

Every map projection is accurate and also distorts something. The per-host pricing is a projection that accurately describes your infrastructure costs but distorts the customer's cost-to-value picture. Cartographers solved this not by finding a "true" projection (there isn't one) but by annotating maps to say what they're optimized for. You can't change the projection before the board cycle, but you can annotate it. A "pricing explainer" modal or comparison row on your pricing page that says "1 host typically handles ~50K requests/day" gives customers the conversion key without changing the unit. This is different from a competitor-style calculator because it doesn't invite comparison shopping — it teaches rather than estimates, framing the unit as a stable capacity concept rather than a price-per-request.

**3. The "threshold guardian" pattern from narrative structure**

In screenwriting, a threshold guardian is a figure who blocks the hero's path not out of malice but to test readiness. The host limit you're hitting in week one is functioning as an unintended threshold guardian — it stops forward progress at the exact moment a user is just discovering value. Story structure says the guardian should appear after the hero has already glimpsed what's on the other side, not before. The practical move: restructure the trial so that the host limit doesn't appear until day 8 or 10, after users have seen a full monitoring cycle and received at least one actionable alert. The churn event and the "felt tricked" feeling are both symptoms of the guardian appearing too early. You don't need to change the limit, just time when users encounter it.

**4. Toll road pricing vs. park admission**

Toll roads charge per use; national parks charge per entry regardless of how many trails you hike. Your customers are trying to buy a park pass and you're selling them a toll booth. The churn isn't really about the unit — it's about the mismatch in commitment model. One angle to explore: offer a "flat entry" trial that doesn't count hosts, but does cap total monitored events. This keeps finance happy (host-based billing remains intact post-trial), aligns the trial with how customers think (I want to monitor my stuff, not count my hosts), and removes the "gotcha" from week one. After the trial, the conversion conversation becomes "you used X capacity, here's which tier fits you" rather than "you exceeded your host limit."
