## angles
- Spoilage happens at handoffs, not in sustained conditions; the transition from symbol-on-page to motor-command-under-pressure is where the chain breaks -> Motor memory (six pieces) is intact; bass-clef decoding and syncopation-as-real-time-instruction are the transition failures -> Drill the specific symbol-to-position translation in isolation, brief and frequent, before real-time pressure, rather than redesigning the whole chain.
- Restarting from external labels without state-checking compounds the deficit; each restart resets the visible clock while the actual gap (bass clef, syncopation) remains unaudited -> Restarting beginner books creates appearance of progress while the underlying skill deficit accumulates invisibly -> Before any practice period, audit the actual state directly (can you read and play these bass-clef notes cleanly? can you clap and play this syncopated rhythm?) rather than using book chapter structure as the entry point.
- Cold chains don't solve the whole route at once; they identify high-risk segments and give those disproportionate engineering attention -> 20-minute sessions spent re-running the full curriculum wastes the limited window on already-intact segments; the high-risk zone (bass-clef fluency, syncopation under pressure) gets whatever's left -> Identify the dangerous zone first and spend the majority of the window there (e.g., 8 minutes of bass-clef drills or a single syncopated measure played slowly until clean), followed by familiar rewarding material, rather than following beginner-book structure.
