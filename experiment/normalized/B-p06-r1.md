## angles

- A measurement's reliability depends on a traceability chain established *before* the load event, not after. In credential management, a complete registry (secret name, owning service, access, issue date) must exist before incidents occur, not be reconstructed during a breach. A credential rotation cost drops because blast radius becomes readable at a glance. -> A credential registry isn't a compliance artifact but the bond itself; it can be established as a flat file before it's complete or perfectly organized.

- A partially-bonded gauge produces plausible readings under normal conditions but drifts under stress, making the confidence interval structurally wrong. Env files and CI variables appear fine until under unusual load (like a stolen laptop or SOC 2 audit), when the system reveals unverified bonds. -> The dangerous state isn't secrets spread across five systems; it's believing you know which ones matter when that belief is unverified. Blast-radius analysis on paper before auditors arrive is cheaper than discovering during fieldwork that it was wrong.

- Calibration and bonding cannot happen during or after a test—only in the preparation window before load is applied. SOC 2 fieldwork timing creates a preparation window; credential registry work scoped to provisioning and rotation events (not everyday development) avoids adding friction to the critical path where developers request new secrets. -> The provisioning-vs-retrieval distinction weakens the friction argument because the work doesn't land where engineers feel it.
