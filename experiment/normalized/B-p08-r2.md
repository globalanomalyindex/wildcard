## angles
- Fount classification comes before character inspection: having classified which alerts sit on regulated payment flow versus batch infrastructure (a one-time pass) would make the alert population legible before any threshold decision, just as a compositor identifies risky typefaces and sorts before reading a galley proof.
- Per-host CPU and disk alerts are turned letters: they fire on a pattern (elevated CPU) that during batch jobs is noise but in other contexts signals a real incident; the consequence is that engineers habituate to discount that alert class entirely, and when a real incident fires out of context, it arrives in a form that has trained its readers to skim it; the fix is to make these alerts batch-context-aware (suppress during known batch windows or replace with a measurement that doesn't fire on batch load).
- Proof fatigue is a composition problem, not a reader problem: the alerts that are noise during batch operations are not wrong to exist but are wrong to be routed through the same channel as regulated-path pages; moving them to a lower-urgency channel (daytime Slack digest, weekly summary) is not lowering guard on regulated paths but routing different alert classes to the appropriate reader posture, keeping compliance-mandated channels exactly as they are.
