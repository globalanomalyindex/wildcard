## angles
- In knit fabrics, dropping one stitch creates a dependency cascade that ladders down through every row, destroying a whole column — the interlocking connectivity that makes the fabric strong is the same thing that gives failure an express lane -> When the contractor's laptop was stolen, the team couldn't identify which credentials were compromised or what they connected to, because there was no map of credential dependency edges; each credential is held by multiple systems, not a leaf node -> Build a credential dependency graph for each secret: what can it reach, and which machines/environments currently hold it, so that credential rotation can be surgical rather than total teardown.
- Experienced knitters thread a lifeline (contrasting yarn through a completed row) at a known-good checkpoint before problems occur, so they can rip back exactly to that verified state without losing the whole piece -> The team had no lifeline when the incident happened; a lifeline in this context is a regularly-verified, committed inventory of what credentials exist, what they scope to, where they're authorized to live, and when they were last rotated -> Treat the credentials inventory (which may already be required for SOC 2) as an operational lifeline rather than an audit artifact, maintained continuously so that at 2 AM the question "which credentials need rotating?" has a bounded, confident answer.
- A knitter works a gauge swatch (test square) before starting a project to measure actual stitch density; every individual stitch can be perfect but if gauge drifts 10% from spec, the finished garment is wrong in aggregate, and this drift is only visible when measuring the whole fabric -> The team has a stated policy ("use the vault") but hardcoded strings, .env files, and CI variables keep appearing; each decision seemed reasonable individually but in aggregate has drifted from spec, and this drift is only discovered when an incident forces you to measure the gap between policy and practice -> Run a secrets audit now, not because SOC 2 requires it, but to surface that drift so you can correct it on a manageable piece rather than discover it when an incident tests those parameters.
