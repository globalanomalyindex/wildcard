## angles
- Film archivists use scene-detection as a preprocessing step to create indices without human review -> Whisper and similar tools can batch-transcribe audio files locally, creating timestamped transcripts that inherit the source filename -> Run an overnight batch transcription job on local infrastructure using Whisper to sidestep the 250 MB upload cap and prioritize transcripts needed by the publisher deadline.
- Libraries maintained card catalogs by indexing only metadata rather than full text before digitization -> A simple one-line description per episode (guest, date, topics, memorable phrase) in a spreadsheet enables 90% of search queries without full transcription -> Build a lightweight incremental catalog in Notion or Airtable where transcribers add entries as they work, compounding over time and answering most "where did I say X" requests.
- A constraint can narrows scope usefully rather than just blocking progress -> The 250 MB cap means roughly five or six compressed 45-minute episodes per batch to transcription APIs, naturally creating a triage discipline -> Use the upload cap as a scope filter to process the publisher's requested episodes first, then proceed by guest email frequency.
- Oral history projects discovered that participants can locate their own recordings faster than external indexers -> Guests already email asking where they said things, representing free indexing labor -> Reply to incoming requests with a template asking for approximate timestamps, turning support requests into catalog contributions.
