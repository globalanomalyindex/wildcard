## angles
- Van contents encode dispatch history (what's depleted fastest, what accumulates, parts consumption patterns) -> systematic measurement of van contents across a season yields a fossil record of demand history -> treat physical parts inventory as measurable data rather than just current state
- Small, fast-moving components are systematically underrepresented in manual counts, creating taphonomic bias -> inventory accuracy varies by part type, with high-stakes parts being the most invisible to counting methods -> finding different preservation media for fast-moving parts matters more than improving the count of what's already visible
- Nine technicians hold partial records of demand; pooling "arrived without the right part" events across all vans reconstructs the full demand distribution -> absence from one van's callback data doesn't mean absence from the territory's actual needs -> audit the category of parts causing second visits across the assemblage rather than within single technician experience
- Equipment age (from permits, scheduling intake, service history, housing stock age) are causally connected to parts demand and measurable before dispatch -> using isotopic proxies yields indirect reconstruction of inaccessible direct signals -> rough age-of-equipment data shifts the prior on what to load, making dispatch informationally rich rather than empty before arrival
