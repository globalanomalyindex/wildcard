## angles
- Temperature changes fast; salinity changes slowly; both drive density. Pricing page shows only the slow variable (hosts); requests are the fast variable. This maps to missing visibility into rate-of-change during trial. Surfacing running request velocity alongside host consumption during the trial could make both timescales visible to customers.
- Forcing happens at the surface; mechanism plays out at depth; need tracers that follow the water. Pricing page is the surface; host-limit collision is depth, separated by a week of trial usage. Lack of real-time host-utilization-rate tracer that predicts days-until-limit. A tracer running on existing per-user data could trigger intervention before collision.
- Conveyor shows precursor signals before halting; watching those variables gives a window to act before tipping point. Host-consumption velocity in first 72 hours is a precursor signal of trajectory mismatch. Early-velocity signal may already be in the data; a well-timed in-product message at 60% of host limit (before 100% collision) could reach users before trust collapse.
- Cost of preventing halt is lower than cost of restarting after it stops; the two interventions are not symmetric. "Felt tricked" is a trust-state, not a service-state; removing host limit after-the-fact doesn't restart the relationship. Intervention budget may be better spent on in-trial host-limit legibility (prevention) than on win-back campaigns targeting churned users.
