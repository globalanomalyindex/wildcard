## angles
- Documentary editors work backward from the strongest moment in raw footage rather than from chronological start; retention at 45 seconds suggests a structural problem where opening context precedes visual payoff -> try leading with a 10-second visually striking clip (surprising, physically tense, a mistake, grain movement, pressure moment) before narration, letting the first image be the thesis
- Multi-variable diagnosis (thumbnails, titles, topics) conflated in impression data -> apply the "one constant" rule: hold two variables fixed, vary only one across a series of five pieces (e.g., keep thumbnail and composition identical, vary titles only), reading the delta in impression-to-click-through ratio to isolate signal from noise
- Pre-mortem framing: imagine the project already failed and identify what killed it -> before uploading, run a five-minute pre-mortem on the first 90 seconds in isolation, watch on phone with sound off; if you can't tell what the video is about or why it matters from visuals alone in 90 seconds, the opening won't survive silent autoplay
- A 24:1 footage-to-final ratio implies most of the timeline is noise by definition (repeated takes, setup, dead time) -> adopt a "one-clip-per-step" discipline before filming: write down 12-15 discrete steps of the build and commit to capturing each exactly once with no safety takes, treating craft confidence as the editorial filter rather than letting timeline decision-making drive editing time
