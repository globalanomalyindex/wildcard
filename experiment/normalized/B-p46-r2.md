## angles
- In granite, the outermost surface layer spalls first while the deep core remains intact; a collection's recently acquired digital layer (bookmarks, links, screenshots) is spalling through paywall exfoliation while older physical items remain durable -> conservation priority should reverse from surface to deep core; the grandmother's binder cards represent the highest-value, load-bearing single point and should be digitized first, not last -> sequence consolidation by identifying which layers are load-bearing versus inherently ephemeral, not by recency
- Feldspar hydrolyzes silently into kaolin clay, so apparent rock volume doesn't match actual intact material; a collection appears large (many saves and links) but contains hollow references (dead links, paywalled content, unsearchable images) -> the apparent 300 items may actually be 180 accessible plus 120 clay-soft pointers -> run an audit to find the true recoverable count before building an organizational scheme
- Granite's properties that work at depth (massive, impermeable, pressure-bearing) become liabilities at the surface where thermal cycling and infiltration act on it; the grandmother's cards are optimized for stability and permanence but the new context demands searchability, remote access, and dietary-filter awareness -> the cards aren't defective, the surface environment is different -> build a thin digitization layer that preserves what makes them valuable (notes, corrections, handwriting) while adding what the context demands (searchability, photos, tags)
- Granite under point load fractures immediately with no warning, unlike compressive load across a wide area; the fragmented collection tolerates low-stress casual browsing but fails completely under the point load of Sunday planning with a deadline and time pressure -> load fails from functional to broken, forcing fallback to memory instead of retrieval -> structure the load path incrementally before the crisis, not during it; make a two-line post-meal log right after each meal so Sunday search consults a dense log instead of probing a fragmented corpus under pressure
