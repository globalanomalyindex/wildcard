## angles
- Ambulance system demand forecasting uses predictive positioning based on call clustering patterns by time and neighborhood before the calls arrive. Summer AC failures concentrate geographically and by season in the service records; this suggests pre-staging a tech at high-probability locations each morning with the five parts accounting for 60% of no-parts returns. The first-come dispatch rule assumes all locations equally likely to fail.
- Offshore fishing vessels use "set and wait" positioning from water temperature and current charts rather than constant trolling, consuming less fuel and catching more. Batching nearby calls to the best installer anchored in a commercial corridor or apartment complex for a full day would reduce driving waste compared to cross-town routing; dispatch currently optimizes for driving distance by accident.
- Hospital pharmacy par-level stocking keeps only the 30 most-used items on nursing floors with centralized nightly restocking, rather than one central inventory. Each van could carry a personalized 10-part kit derived from that tech's last two years of work orders, not a generic list. Second-visit rates are mostly a parts-prediction problem.
- Wildland fire crew rotation assigns roles by fatigue and skill match, not seniority or queue order. The best installer should be a "complex call only" tech handling jobs only he can close in one visit, while less-experienced techs absorb volume work (tune-ups, filter swaps). His driving time drops because his call list shrinks to work matching his skill.
