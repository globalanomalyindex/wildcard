## angles
- In vacuum manifolds, the leak rate is set by the single worst joint, not the average. A single unreachable node bottlenecks the entire pipeline. -> The inaccessible binder (or other single unreachable source) sets the ceiling for the whole planning system. -> Fix the worst joint first, not the average joint. The binder may be the highest-leverage thing to address because it's the only part unreachable at the moment that matters.

- A joint with mismatched thermal expansion looks fine until the stress crack appears during the fourth cool-down cycle, after more components are already built on top of it. -> Saving a URL looks like preservation at capture time, but the test of whether it's useful happens weeks later under planning-load—by then the page may be paywalled, dead, or context lost. The failure is invisible at save time. -> Rather than deferring preservation work, pull and save the actual content at capture time (ingredients, method, pasted notes), not just the pointer.

- Dead volume in a manifold is a section that exists but cannot be reached by the pump, holds molecules the system can't do anything with, and adds noise. -> Instagram saves, screenshots, and the parents'-house binder are dead volume: they exist inside the collection's boundary and carry real value, but cannot be reached from the working channel (the Sunday planning session). -> Either connect them to the main path or deliberately isolate and remove them. The in-between state of indefinite deferral doesn't improve over time; it just costs overhead while delivering nothing.

- Every joint added increases the probability of an invisible flaw behind. The solution is to build in test-access at each sub-assembly stage—a break point or access port where you can verify the sub-assembly before it gets buried inside the complete system. -> The save-to-retrieval pipeline has no test points: capture time and retrieval time are completely decoupled, so you never find out whether a save was actually usable until weeks later when you need it. -> Build testability into the capture step: tag a screenshot with what you'd search for it under, or write two words about why you saved it. Just enough to make the save verifiable at the point when it's cheapest to fix.
