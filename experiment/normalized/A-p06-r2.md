## angles
- Custody logs work when they capture events prospectively (at transfer time), not retrospectively (from memory after incident). When you reconstruct credential custody from memory after the laptop theft, you can't bound the blast radius and are forced to rotate almost everything. The deferred cost of having no prospective record is catastrophic to execute. Ask whether there's a natural moment in existing workflow where a custody event already happens that could be instrumented without friction.
- When a credential's custody state is unknown (who generated it, who was issued a copy, whether revoked), an incident forces worst-case assumption and rotation. You rotated "almost everything" not because everything was compromised, but because custody state was unknown. The remediation cost scales directly with un-custodied surface area. Start logging custody for highest-blast-radius credentials first — production database passwords, signing certificates, external API keys — to shrink the "must rotate everything" surface area.
- A vault stores the current value of the credential (the item), but not the credential's history (the form) — which developer generated it, which systems ever held a copy, when it was last rotated, what services depend on it. When the laptop was stolen, the vault was intact but the history for many credentials didn't exist. The fix isn't a better vault; it's a better form filled out at every transfer: credential issuance, credential consumption, and offboarding. Ask whether existing infrastructure already emits events at those moments that could be logged without touching developer workflow.
- Chain of custody is achieved through handoff protocols (explicit transfer moments where custody is acknowledged and recorded), not surveillance. Engineers resist friction that feels surveillance-like. Handoff-protocol-style controls fire at natural transfer moments (vault issuance, CI secret injection, offboarding) rather than continuously monitoring developer machines. A credential issuance event in the vault already happens and might already emit a log you could route somewhere. The developer's dotfile isn't monitored — but the moment they were granted the secret is potentially instrumentable without touching their machine. Ask where transfer events already happen in the system and what it would cost to capture them.
