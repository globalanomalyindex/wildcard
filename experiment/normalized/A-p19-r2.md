## angles
- Trial users commit onboarding effort based on a mental model (requests/month) that doesn't match the actual claim structure (hosts), discovering the mismatch only at the host limit -> like a junior creditor in a distressed capital structure facing informational opacity before default -> surface the host-to-request conversion math prominently during trial signup as the primary frame, making the waterfall legible before commitment rather than at the default event.
- There's a structural gap where the cheapest tier undershoots real need and customers won't commit to a higher tier without evidence -> this mirrors the mezzanine financing gap where custom placement solves the problem through agreed-upon terms before commitment -> structure the trial as a guided discovery period that ends in a placement conversation showing actual usage, actual tier fit, and next-tier cost, reframing upgrade from "you exceeded the limit" to "here's your term sheet".
- The host-limit hit is a binary default where everything bad happens at once -> debt covenants prevent this by triggering a mandatory renegotiation at a graduated threshold (e.g., 80% of the test), converting binary adversarial default into graduated negotiated event -> implement a usage alert at 50% of host capacity framed as proactive signal of tracking toward limit, triggering a conversation while both parties still have something to protect.
