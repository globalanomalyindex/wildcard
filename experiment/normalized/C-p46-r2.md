## angles
- Field naturalists create one standardized record card per specimen (common name, date, location, conditions, what made it notable) rather than scattered field notes. Treating each meal actually cooked as the specimen, with a simple note per cooked meal (dish name, date, who ate it, verdict), creates a personal log that's smaller and more searchable than a recipe archive, like the grandmother's binder which already represents things actually cooked.
- Hospital pharmacists maintain a curated short list of what their practice actually uses (with substitutions noted) rather than stocking every drug. The spice-rebuying problem and "can't find the hit" problem are symptoms of having a library when a formulary is needed; building a small active rotation of 15-20 recipes the household has actually eaten and liked makes Sunday planning about "which formulary recipe fits this week" rather than a search.
- Software projects tag specific commits as releases (stable, tested, worth returning to) even though commits are cheap; the tag is rare and meaningful. Applying a two-tier system where saving remains frictionless but tagging (via photo, shared note, or text) happens only post-meal filters for real success rather than anticipated interest.
- Stage managers create a prop bible backwards from the script—every prop needed for this production with photo, source, and substitution notes—rather than forward from a collection. Starting from ingredients reliably available or wanting to rotate through, then letting meal plans surface from there, inverts the Sunday shopping problem; a standing pantry snapshot becomes the source-of-truth column without requiring a dedicated app.
