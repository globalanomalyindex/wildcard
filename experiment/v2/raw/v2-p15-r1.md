Drawing on cuneiform tablet impression decipherment — specifically the study of which marks across a physically heterogeneous archive descend from the same source versus which are substrate-driven noise — a few things surface here.

---

**Noticing:** A decipherer reading a population of clay impressions is always distinguishing two types of divergence: genuine scribal divergence (different source, different hand, different design) versus substrate-driven divergence (same source, different physical medium — damp clay vs. fired clay vs. coarser clay all produce different-looking marks from an identical stylus). The practitioner's first move on any fragmented archive is to find the source files, not catalogue the impressions.

**Mapping:** Nine logo versions across chapter websites, vinyl billboards, embroidery, and printed workbooks looks like a nine-way problem. But most of those nine almost certainly descend from a much smaller number of actual source files — probably two or three — with the rest being substrate-driven noise: the same 2003 EPS someone has been resizing, screen-printing, and embroidering for twenty years looks different across those media even if the source never changed.

**Concrete move:** Before spending budget on anything, request the actual files (AI, EPS, or PDF) from every chapter and map each of the nine versions to its source. Build a one-row-per-chapter spreadsheet: chapter name, source file (with date and format), primary audience (donor-facing / community-facing / partner-facing), and which mark it currently uses. If five of nine trace to the same source EPS, you don't have a nine-remediation problem — you have a one-file replacement and a redistribution. That audit is a 6-to-8-hour task at no additional cost and changes what the $12K is actually for.

---

**Noticing:** In cuneiform archives, authorized copies of tablets made for secondary locations carry an explicit authorization signal built into the copy itself — a colophon or a seal impression stating "copy of the original at temple X." The signal exists precisely because the archive is distributed and the original cannot travel. Without the signal, later readers cannot tell an authorized copy from a forgery or an error.

**Mapping:** Your chapters are printing from whatever files they have because there is no signal about which file is currently authorized. The version entropy is not primarily caused by preference for the old mark — it is caused by ambiguity about what is sanctioned. A chapter using an old file is not necessarily resisting the rebrand; they may simply not know what to use, and defaulting to what they have is rational under ambiguity.

**Concrete move:** Write a one-page "Two-Mark Policy" that explicitly authorizes both marks — each with a defined domain. For example: the crayon-style mark is the authorized mark for donor-facing print materials, year-end appeal letters, and classroom workbooks through a stated date (or "until further board notice"); the new mark is authorized immediately for all digital assets, partner-facing materials, and grant applications. Distribute it as a PDF alongside two clean master files (one per mark) to a single named contact per chapter. This formalizes what is already functionally true — both marks are in use — removes the ambiguity that drives entropy, and gives your largest donors' year-end giving a protected channel without requiring any reprinting. Estimated cost: under $500 in staff time.

---

**Noticing:** The practitioner's characteristic fear is "asymmetric firing" — when tablets from the same archive reach different states of permanence with no record of which is which, cross-comparison becomes unreliable and you cannot tell convergence from undocumented divergence.

**Mapping:** The dangerous state for your identity system is not "some chapters on the old mark, some on the new" — that is a managed phase. The dangerous state is that mix with no documentation of which chapter is in which state and why, so that when you check in a year, you cannot tell whether you are 70% converged or whether you have three new unauthorized variants you did not know about.

**Concrete move:** The chapter-audit spreadsheet from the first move above doubles as your living convergence record: add a "target mark" column and a "transition date" column alongside the current-state columns. Update it when a chapter transitions. At your next board meeting you can show a table rather than a narrative — "14 of 17 chapters authorized on the new mark for digital; 17 of 17 authorized on the crayon mark for year-end print" — and you have the evidence base to tell the donating agency's rebrand team exactly where you are and why the dual-mark period has a defined end condition.
