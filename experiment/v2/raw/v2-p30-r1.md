Drawing on court stenography — specifically the structure-and-form discipline of a realtime federal-trial transcriptionist — three things surfaced about your warehouse that the plain operational view tends to miss.

---

**1. Your fastest movers aren't just in the wrong zone — they're not clustered with their own co-travelers.**

In stenography, the physical layout of chord sequences isn't designed around individual word frequency alone — it's designed around *co-occurrence*: the pairs and triplets of words that appear together constantly in courtroom speech get assigned adjacent chord positions so the hand barely moves between them. Optimizing single words in isolation leaves that co-travel cost on the table.

Your pick-rate drop from 80 to 55 lines/hour points at something similar. "Slot fast movers closer to the ship station" is the obvious fix, but it still leaves pickers backtracking if two SKUs that routinely appear on the same order end up in different aisles. The co-occurrence gap is invisible until you measure it.

*Concrete move:* Pull your last 90 days of order lines and calculate the top 20% of SKU pairs by order co-occurrence frequency — the pairs that ship together most often. Reslot those pairs into adjacent locations within whichever zone is nearest your pack station. This is a distinct action from raw velocity slotting, and it compounds with it: velocity moves the right items to the right zone; co-occurrence clustering means a picker retrieving item A already has item B within arm's reach.

---

**2. Your Monday/Thursday receiving collisions are a brief-form conflict — and they're masked.**

A stenographer's worst failure mode is the masked stroke conflict: two different phrases produce the same chord sequence, one silently overwrites the other, and the error survives proofreading because it looks plausible in context. The collision isn't loud; it just corrupts the record.

Your dock appointments clustering on Monday and Thursday create the same structural pattern. On those days, receiving flow and pick flow contend for the same physical space — pallets spill into pick aisles — and neither supervisor is likely tracking the specific throughput hit caused by the spatial collision, only experiencing Mondays and Thursdays as generally rough. The problem survives operational review because it reads as a normal busy-day slowdown rather than a structural conflict between two simultaneous flows.

The steno fix is to resolve conflicts at *design time*, before the trial, not by improvising during it. The warehouse equivalent: before peak season, explicitly designate 2-3 specific aisles as receiving staging zones on Mondays and Thursdays, and define time windows (e.g., 7am–noon) during which those aisles are off-limits to pick assignments. Pull or defer all orders whose pick paths touch those aisles until after the window closes. Your supervisors enforce an aisle boundary with a clear rule, rather than managing chaos ad hoc. This is a scheduled spatial reservation, not a plea for more coordination.

---

**3. Your slotting is degrading because it requires a supervisor judgment call at the moment of lowest bandwidth.**

A realtime court reporter cannot stop the witness to think — her entire dictionary is pre-designed so that the right stroke emerges from structure, not from working memory at 225 words per minute. When the system requires active decision-making under time pressure, it produces stroke conflicts and dropped words. The fix is always to push the decision upstream into the structure, so the right action at the moment of pressure becomes a lookup rather than a judgment.

Your current slotting disorder accumulated because putaway decisions are made under receiving pressure, with two supervisors already stretched. Each individual "put it wherever there's space" decision was locally rational and globally corrosive. The same two supervisors will be your training bottleneck when six temps arrive in eight weeks.

*Concrete move:* Before peak, assign each warehouse zone a velocity tier (A/B/C) and add a velocity class to your receiving process — either in your WMS if you have one, or as a field on the PO receipt paperwork. Putaway crews look up the velocity class and route to the designated zone without a supervisor decision. Supervisors approve exceptions only. The zone map and velocity tiers take a few hours to design; the daily bandwidth they return to your supervisors — who are your real scarcest resource going into peak — is ongoing.
