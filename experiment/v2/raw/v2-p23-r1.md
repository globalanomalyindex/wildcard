Drawing on permafrost — specifically how it accumulates damage over time and responds (or doesn't) to surface-level interventions — a few things surfaced when I looked at your situation.

---

**1. Sort by peak-to-current drop, not by current traffic alone.**

Posts that have low traffic now fall into two structurally different buckets: ones that never had traffic (always thin) and ones that once ranked, then lost ground, and have been sitting degraded for 18+ months. The second type already has inbound links, indexed history, and a known keyword match — it lost something, which means there's something to recover. The first type might be better candidates for consolidation or removal than for revision.

The concrete move: export your Search Console data with both current and historical impressions (compare 90 days now vs. 90 days at your pre-drop peak). Sort by the delta — the absolute drop in impressions, not the current impressions. Posts with large peak-to-current drops that are also 2+ years old are your structural-damage triage list. That's a different set than "your current top posts," and it's where consolidation effort pays off disproportionately.

---

**2. Batch cannibalization decisions into a single sign-off event.**

The throughput math is brutal: 460 posts, one week per approval, one part-time writer. The bottleneck isn't writing — it's the approval queue. But keyword cannibalization (multiple posts fighting the same query) is a *structural* problem, and the fix for it is a decision, not a draft: which URL wins, which gets redirected. That decision doesn't require revised copy; it requires a call.

You can change the granularity of what goes through sign-off. Pull Search Console queries where two or more of your posts split impressions — those are your cannibalization pairs. Compile 15–20 of the clearest URL pairs into a single document, with a keep/consolidate/redirect recommendation for each pair and the Search Console impression data behind each call. Submit that as one approval request to product marketing: "These are the structural consolidation decisions for Q4 — each pair needs a single call, A or B." That's one week of sign-off queue time to unlock 15–20 fixes, rather than 15 serial weeks.

---

**3. Check SERP format before updating the tutorials.**

After a major algorithm update, the posts that lost ground often lost it not because the content is stale but because the *format* the SERP now rewards for that query changed. Step-by-step tutorials may have dominated a SERP that now returns comparison tables or feature overviews — and updating the screenshots in a tutorial won't recover a position the SERP is no longer awarding to tutorials.

The move: for your 15 highest-traffic-loss posts, run each primary keyword in an incognito window and note the dominant content format in positions 1–3 (tutorial, listicle, comparison table, product page, FAQ). Compare that to your own post's format. Any mismatch is a structural reason the post won't recover from a UI refresh alone, and it changes what the revision actually needs to do — or whether revision is the right call at all versus consolidation or a redirect.

---

**4. Filter your Q4 revision list to positions 6–15 with 500+ monthly impressions.**

The posts most likely to move in a Q4 timeframe are not your top 5 (they're already there) and not your tail posts ranking 20+ (they won't move meaningfully in 6 months from a single revision). They're the posts currently sitting in positions 6–15 for queries that still have real search volume.

Export Search Console, filter for average position between 6 and 15, and sort by impressions where the impression count is above 500/month. That list — probably 20–40 posts — is where a targeted single revision has a realistic probability of crossing onto page 1 before Q4 ends. It's also a defensible story for leadership: "here are the 30 posts we're targeting because they're closest to page 1 on queries people are actually running."

---
