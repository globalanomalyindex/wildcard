Drawing on diffusion — the concept that everything spreads through a medium according to how steep the gradient is and how resistive the medium is — three things surfaced about your situation that the standard "rebrand vs. keep it" framing tends to skip.

---

**1. Your medium is resistive, so identify the high-conductivity nodes first.**

In any diffusion process, the fastest path through a resistant medium isn't broadcasting uniformly — it's finding the few nodes with very high conductivity that then re-emit locally. Your chapter-prints-locally system is a highly resistant medium: no server, inconsistent files, autonomous behavior. Broadcasting a single email with corrected assets to all chapters equally will diffuse slowly. What actually speeds propagation is identifying the 2-3 chapters with the highest print volume (whoever orders the most workbooks or runs the biggest volunteer cohort) and getting those specific chapter contacts a corrected file kit first — with a phone call, not just an email. Their printed output becomes what neighboring chapters visually copy. Spend roughly $500 on the outreach and file kit; those nodes do the rest. You might also find that this identifies who to give a thumb drive rather than a download link.

---

**2. Two competing versions won't resolve to one clean winner — design the equilibrium zone instead.**

When two diffusion fronts compete in the same medium, they don't produce a sharp displacement — they produce a zone where both exist, with maximum confusion right at the boundary. Trying to fully replace the old crayon-style mark while it's already diffused into a decade of workbooks sitting in classrooms will produce that confusion zone, not a clean transition. The donor-survey finding is real signal: those retired teachers are not wrong that the old mark drives recognition — it's the version that's *already in the medium*. The move here isn't "new mark or old mark." It's: commission a transitional canonical file that deliberately retains 2-3 of the specific visual cues the survey flagged as recognition-drivers (the hand-lettering weight, the color temperature, whatever they are) while using the donated rebrand's underlying proportions and color system. Designate that one file as the chapter-print standard. You're not overruling the donated rebrand; you're designing a version that will actually diffuse successfully through your medium, rather than stalling at the boundary. A designer doing this scope of work — one canonical file derived from existing assets — should cost well under $2,000 of your budget.

---

**3. The workbooks already in classrooms can't be recalled — formalize the coexistence rather than fighting it.**

When something has already diffused into the bulk of a material, the engineering response isn't to try to remove it from where it already is — that's expensive and usually incomplete. You intervene at the active surface going forward, and you acknowledge what's already in the medium rather than pretending it isn't there. Your printed workbooks in classrooms are the bulk. You won't reprint them; the budget doesn't reach them; they'll age out over years. Rather than treating the old-mark workbooks as a problem to solve, give chapters a low-cost way to formally acknowledge them: a rubber-stamp template or a sheet of small stickers (total cost: maybe $300 for a print run) that reads something like "Published in our 30th year" — placed on the inside cover. This frames the visual inconsistency as intentional heritage rather than organizational chaos, which is a real distinction to a retired teacher donor who picks up that workbook. It also keeps those books in service rather than getting quietly disposed of because they feel out-of-date.

---

Three concrete moves, each independent — you could run any one of them without the others. Happy to pull any of these threads further, or to step back and leave you to it.
