My corner of the field is screen-bias diagnosis: figuring out why a well-maintained instrument in a properly-built enclosure is still giving corrupted readings, and tracing that back to something in the surrounding geometry. The obsession that defines how I see everything: the structure around the instrument is never neutral. ---

**Screen bias from nearby structures.**

In my field, a Stevenson screen placed too close to a heat-reflecting wall gives readings that look completely legitimate - the instrument passes calibration, the logbook is filled in, the data goes to archive. But every reading is a compound of true air temperature and local structural interference. The bias is invisible unless you audit the *siting geometry*, not just the instrument. You don't discover it by checking the thermometer. You discover it by looking at what's around it.

Your measurement system has this structure. The thing being measured is "work done" - sprint velocity. The instrument (the handoff process) is sited directly adjacent to a large heat-reflecting surface: the incentive to ship features. Every reading (component delivered) looks fine in sprint review. But the reading isn't measuring what you think it's measuring - it's measuring "feature count" while being silently warped by a consistency-debt that doesn't show up until a contract review makes you look at the archive.

The calibration move for screen bias isn't to replace the thermometer. It's to add a *reference station* nearby - a second measurement point that isn't subject to the same siting interference - and run both simultaneously. The divergence between them is the bias. If you run even one consistency metric alongside velocity - not instead of it, just alongside - the gap between the two instruments becomes visible as a number rather than as a customer's complaint.

**The correction factor, not the screen relocation.**

When a calibration specialist discovers that a site has been running biased for years, they can't move the screen retroactively - the historical record breaks. What they do instead is apply a *correction factor* at the transition point: document the bias magnitude at the moment it was identified, adjust the archive at that point forward, and preserve the old data as-is with a note. The correction is applied where it's most leverageable, not uniformly across everything.

You have the same constraint. You can't rebuild 312 components in two months, and you can't retroactively align every screen the React package has been reading from. But the contract flagged *focus states* specifically - that's the correction factor point. The bias has a known location and a known magnitude (the customer's review). Applying a tight correction there - the actual focus state components, the actual gaps between Figma and React for those specific states - is structurally equivalent to the calibration specialist's move: it preserves the existing record, applies leverage where the bias is largest, and gives you something that can be measured forward from this sprint rather than something that requires the whole site to be rebuilt.

**The louver geometry as a self-revealing form.**

The specific structure of a Stevenson screen's louvers - the angle, the gap, the depth - is designed so that any deviation from correct form is immediately visible on inspection. A broken or warped louver changes the shade pattern in a way you can see by walking past. The form encodes its own correctness check; you don't need a separate audit process.

Your current gap between Figma and the React package has no equivalent geometry. The drift is invisible sprint by sprint because there's no form that makes it obvious on the same timescale as velocity. You could establish a small one: a short, named list of the highest-stakes component states (focus states, since the contract already surfaced them as the load-bearing set) that gets visually checked against the standard at the point in the sprint where it costs least - not a full audit, not a new process, just the structural equivalent of walking past the screen and noticing whether the louvers are straight. The form itself, if it's the right form, does the checking.
