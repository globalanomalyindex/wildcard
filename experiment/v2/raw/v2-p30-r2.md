Drawing on momentum as a concept - specifically how it's *measured* rather than just felt - three things surfaced.

---

**1. Your 55 lines per hour is one number hiding two separate problems.**

In mechanics, momentum is mass × velocity - two independent factors, and measuring only one gives a misleading picture. Your lines-per-hour collapses two completely different costs: time-on-SKU (hands actually grabbing product) and time-in-transit (walking between picks). A picker doing 55 LPH might be spending 35% of their shift walking and only 65% picking, or the ratio might be 15/85 - the single number can't tell you. Time a random sample of 50 picks with a stopwatch split into those two buckets. If travel time is above 30% of total pick time, slot scatter is the primary culprit and relocation moves will recover most of your lost throughput. If travel time is already low, the problem is somewhere else entirely (congestion, process, product characteristics), and re-slotting won't fix it.

---

**2. Once you know travel is the problem, measure which relocations pay the most before moving anything.**

The measurement lens on momentum pushes one step further: it's not enough to know you have a problem, you need to rank the interventions by yield. Pull your velocity data for the last 60 days and build a simple table: for each of your top 100 SKUs by pick frequency, record picks-per-week and current distance from the pack station (bin location number is a reasonable proxy). Rank by the product of those two numbers - picks/week × distance. The SKUs at the top of that list are where slot scatter is burning the most collective travel time. Move the top 20 to positions within 50 feet of the pack station before peak. You don't need a full warehouse re-slot in 8 weeks; you need the highest-leverage 20 relocations, and this ranking tells you exactly which ones they are.

---

**3. Your Mon/Thu receiving surge is a force applied in two sharp spikes to a system that can only absorb a steady load - and that's a schedulable problem.**

A large impulse and a gentle sustained force can represent the same total, but the impulse causes damage the sustained force doesn't. Your weekly receiving volume isn't the problem; the shape of its delivery is. If Monday and Thursday each take in, say, 60% of the week's pallets, you're repeatedly jamming the dock and spilling product into pick aisles twice a week - which means pickers are navigating around staged pallets for most of Monday afternoon and Friday morning. Before peak, contact your top 10 carriers and restructure appointment slots so Tuesday and Wednesday each absorb 20% of weekly volume, reducing Mon/Thu to 30% each. Eight weeks is enough to get most carriers habituated to a new schedule. The aisles stay clear during the highest-velocity pick hours, and your supervisors spend less time firefighting dock backup.

---

One optional thread to check before you add the 6 temps: if your peak season brings a different order mix than the rest of the year (more multi-line orders, heavier SKUs, more gift-wrap or kitting), your historical 80 LPH benchmark may not be a valid peak-season target - it may have been achieved under a simpler order composition. Before peak, stratify your recent LPH by order complexity (single-line vs. multi-line, or by average SKU weight) and see whether your "fall from 80" is partly a mix shift rather than a pure efficiency loss. If it is, you'll deploy your 6 temps more accurately knowing which order types are the real bottleneck.
