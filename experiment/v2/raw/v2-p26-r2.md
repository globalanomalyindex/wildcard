Drawing on vanilla hand-pollination practice — specifically the technique-drift problem that arises when a crew of independent workers each develops a slightly different method over a growing season, with divergence only detectable in pod-set rates weeks later — a few things surface here that the plainest reading of the coordination problem might not reach.

---

**1. Move the authoritative reference to the point of the decision, not a step away from it.**

In hand-pollination, the fix for technique drift across a distributed crew is not more supervisors downstream — it's a physical reference card posted at the end of each row, present the moment a worker opens the first blossom. The reference travels to the action window, because turnaround pressure means no one will walk back to the shed to check.

In your setup, the loyalty-program name divergence in German is the same structure: three translators each working in their own environment, each consulting a separate glossary at their own discretion. The move is to embed the approved term at the exact moment it's needed. In most CAT tools — Phrase/Memsource, memoQ, Trados — you can configure a shared termbase so that when a translator reaches a segment containing "loyalty program," the approved German term appears inline as a flag or auto-suggestion, zero extra clicks. The translators don't need to change their workflow; the reference is just there. This is worth trialing on the loyalty-program terms first, where you already know the divergence, before rolling out across all five language pairs.

---

**2. Designate a narrow "change-detection strip" on the English source pages, and diff it automatically before assigning the weekly job.**

The long lag between a pollination action and pod confirmation is the dangerous interval in my field — the window where technique drift compounds invisibly. I designate a small reference block (about 10% of the planting) as a tightly controlled strip I can compare against crew performance in real time, rather than waiting for harvest to reveal the whole-season divergence.

Your analogous dangerous interval is between when the marketing team edits a live English page and when a translator, a customer, or a complaint surfaces the mismatch. The controlled-strip move scales: designate the loyalty-program and key feature-name pages — the sections that change most often and cause the most downstream confusion — as a monitored strip. Run a weekly automated diff of those URLs against the previous snapshot. Any edit to a controlled term triggers a targeted notification to the relevant translators before the next job is assigned, scoped only to changed segments, not a full re-review. The cost is one cron job and an email hook; the budget impact is near zero; and it converts a silent upstream change into a structured downstream signal before it reaches customers in Japan.

---

**3. Add three structured fields to the translator delivery note to convert a noisy downstream signal into a traceable per-job record.**

In a crew context, a pod that forms but is thin or malformed produces false positive feedback — the worker thinks their technique worked. The fix is a binary checkpoint at a defined intermediate stage: measure pod firmness and diameter at six weeks, below threshold the blossom is logged as a miss regardless of appearance. This moves detection earlier and makes divergence traceable to a specific date and worker.

Your current feedback signal — customers in Japan asking about a feature name that doesn't match the app — is the slow, noisy equivalent: only the most frustrated customers report it, and by then tracing which translator, which job, and which English source version produced the mismatch is nearly impossible. The intermediate-checkpoint move requires no extra review rounds and no budget: add three mandatory fields to your existing delivery brief (or job acceptance form) that each translator fills in at delivery: (1) the loyalty-program name they used in this job, (2) the Japanese app-feature name they used, and (3) the English source URL and the page date or version they translated from. This takes about 30 seconds per job, converts per-delivery output into a structured record, and makes any future mismatch traceable to a specific job — which is also the data you'd need to talk to translators about consistency constructively rather than after a customer complaint.
