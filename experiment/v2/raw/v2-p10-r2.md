Drawing on polarization — specifically the way it's measured, and what causes two waves of the same frequency to fail to interfere.

**1. Measure the degree of alignment before you fix anything**

In polarimetry the question isn't "is this beam polarized?" but "what fraction of the population is aligned, and where?" — a scalar, not a binary. Your focus-state problem probably looks systemic but may be narrow: of all the interactive components on the specific screens the customer's review flagged, how many are actually non-compliant with the React package's focus-state props? Express it as a fraction — say, 8 out of 23 interactive components on those three screens. If it's that small, the two-month fix is targeted work on a known list, not a library-wide remediation. Once you have the number, insert a lightweight drift gate: a single checklist item on your sprint review that counts new focus-state non-compliances introduced in that sprint. That count is your velocity-compatible metric — it's addable to the existing sprint rhythm, doesn't require process change, and gives you a trendline to show leadership within weeks rather than at the end of two months.

**2. The detach loop may be running on an encoding gap, not a coverage gap**

Two waves at the same frequency fail to interfere if their polarization axes are orthogonal — they pass through each other without producing alignment, even though they're "the same thing." Your Figma library and your React package share UI intent but have drifted to incompatible axes of description: different names for the same states, different granularity on variants, no agreed vocabulary for things like focus behavior. That's why designers detach — not always because the component is absent, but because they can't tell whether the prop that covers their case exists. The move that fixes this isn't syncing all 312 components; it's agreeing on a shared prop vocabulary specifically for the 15–20 components on the customer's audited screens. One two-hour session with one engineer to produce a two-page reference: "these are the exact prop names and values in the React package that map to these Figma states." Shared encoding means both representations can finally interfere constructively on the same surface.

**3. Make the package's actual surface visible at the moment of detach**

The cheapest rotation is one that doesn't move anything — it just re-aligns direction. The detach bypass is rational precisely when the React package's surface is invisible inside Figma: a designer who can't easily see that `focusVisible` already exists as a prop will detach to get what looks like a missing variant. Publishing the package's actual prop surface inside Figma — either through a Storybook-to-Figma plugin, or as a simple annotated "prop map" spec attached to the component in the Figma library itself — intercepts the bypass before it happens. This doesn't require closing the coverage gap or changing anyone's workflow; it changes what's visible at the one moment when the rational move becomes irrational. Scoped to focus-state behavior and the components on the customer's screens, this is probably a few days of work.
