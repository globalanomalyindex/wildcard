I work in wildlife forensic genetics - specifically, I genotype ivory to determine which elephant population a confiscated tusk came from. My corner of the field: I extract DNA from the ivory's physical microstructure - the dentinal canaliculi, the Schreger lines - to resolve geographic origin and link seizures to trafficking networks. The materials lens is everything in my work, because ivory isn't uniform tissue. It's a degraded, heterogeneous substrate, and how you read it depends entirely on understanding where the signal is still intact and where it's gone.

Three things from my world landed when I looked at your situation.

---

**Ivory degrades from the outside in - and so does your corpus.**

In my field: a tusk exposed to handling, UV, and humidity degrades non-uniformly. The outer cortex - the most exposed zone - is often contaminated beyond use. But the inner dentinal core, protected by the tusk's own mass, can retain readable DNA from decades ago. A sample from 1985 may still yield clean signal from its interior even when the surface is gone. We call this the preservation gradient, and ignoring it means you core from the wrong zone and waste irreplaceable material.

Your 460 posts have the same structure. The most "degraded" posts aren't uniformly distributed - they cluster by type. Posts that were tutorial-shaped and screenshot-dependent (showing a UI that no longer exists) are your outer cortex: high surface area, highly exposed to the product's version drift, expensive to restore. But many of those same posts contain an interior zone that hasn't degraded at all - the conceptual explanation of *why* a feature works, the use-case framing, the definitional content that doesn't depend on which button is where. That inner structure is still sound. In my field, the move is to core from the intact zone rather than try to rehabilitate the contaminated surface. You might find it useful to ask, for each tutorial flagged as outdated: is the procedural layer (screenshots, step numbers) the whole post, or does it sit on top of structural content that could survive a screenshot swap without a full rewrite? That changes the per-post remediation cost considerably.

---

**Chimeric signal - the cannibalization problem - is exactly what I see when I extract from a contaminated zone.**

In my field: if I sample from a region where DNA from multiple sources has mixed - cross-contamination during trafficking, mixed-population stockpiles - I get a chimeric genotype. It looks like a real sample. It runs through PCR. It produces a sequence. But when I try to assign it to a population, it doesn't resolve. The assignment algorithm returns low confidence scores across several candidate populations, and I've consumed evidence on a ghost signal that isn't definitively *from* anywhere.

Keyword cannibalization has exactly this structure. Three posts targeting the same query are each producing a real-but-mixed signal - the search engine sees authority scattered across all three, the assignment doesn't resolve to a definitive rank position, and the traffic gets split in a way that doesn't benefit any of them. The forensic intervention isn't "improve all three posts" - that's trying to rehabilitate a chimeric sample. It's: identify which of the three contains the most intact original signal (the clearest existing authority - inbound links, historical rankings, strongest topical depth), designate that one as the source of truth, and consolidate the others into it or redirect them. In forensics this is called collapsing to the highest-confidence allele. The weaker posts become supporting evidence for the canonical one, not independent samples competing with it.

The practical edge here: you don't need developer time to identify which posts are chimeric. That's a read-only analysis - Search Console query data, URL-level impressions, ranking position by keyword - none of which requires touching any post or triggering your sign-off cycle. The audit is non-destructive. And the redirect, when you eventually do it, is one CMS action per cluster, not a rewrite.

---

**In my field, we triage before we cut - because the material is irreplaceable.**

The constraint that shapes everything I do: I cannot re-run an extraction if I've consumed my sample. Every core I take from the tusk is gone. This means I never start cutting until I've done a non-destructive characterization of the whole tusk - spectroscopy, CT scan, surface mapping - to locate the intact zones before I commit material. The survey is cheap; the extraction is expensive and finite.

Your sign-off slots are your irreplaceable material. One week per piece, one part-time writer, a Q4 deadline - you have a hard cap on how many posts you can move through the gate before the window closes. If you commit those slots to posts that turn out to be outer-cortex (high restoration cost, diffuse cannibalization signal, low baseline authority to build on), you've cored from the wrong zone. The non-destructive survey in your context is the audit that doesn't require touching anything: crawl your site for canonical structure, pull Search Console data for query-level impression share across URLs, identify your cannibalization clusters, and score each cluster by the strength of the lead post. None of that needs editorial work, dev time, or sign-off. It's the characterization pass that tells you *which* cuts are worth making before you spend a single approval slot.
