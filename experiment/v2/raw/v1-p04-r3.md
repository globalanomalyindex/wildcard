I'm a meteorological instrument specialist - specifically someone who obsesses over Stevenson screens: the louvered white wooden boxes that house thermometers at surface weather stations. My corner is siting and calibration through a materials lens. The thing I think about daily is this: a thermometer measures air temperature, but what it actually reads is a convolution of air temperature *and* every material surface in its vicinity. Wood, paint, gravel, asphalt - they all absorb heat during the day and release it at night. The sensor is perfectly accurate; I just have to manage what it is exposed to. The failure I fear most is thermal mass contamination with a time lag: the screen's environment soaks up heat at peak sun and radiates it back hours later, so the measurement is accurate but temporally displaced - it is reading yesterday's afternoon in this morning's minimum. And the thing I have learned is that you cannot just add more sensors; you have to look at the material composition of what is contaminating the read.

Here is what I notice from your world:

---

**The screen vs. the siting**

In my work, parse costs come from two sources that look unified until you separate them: the instrument itself (the screen, the sensor) and the environment it sits in (what surrounds it). The message format is your environment - fixed until next quarter, like asphalt beneath a screen you cannot move. But the deserializer is the screen itself. Those two contamination sources have very different serviceability. When I see an instrument running hot, the first thing I establish is which source is doing it - because conflating them prevents you from acting on the one you can actually touch. I wonder if the per-message parse cost has been fully decomposed the same way: how much comes from the message complexity (the environment) versus from the deserializer implementation (the screen)? Alternative JSON parsers, lazy AST construction, or even byte-level field extraction that avoids building the full object graph are all changes to the instrument, not the environment - and they are available now. In my world, I can often recover half the contamination by repainting the screen before I ever touch the siting.

---

**Selective transduction**

A thermometer reads the full thermal environment it is exposed to. If I want it to respond only to air temperature, I design the screen to block radiation and promote ventilation - I reduce the surface area of contaminating signal it can see. Your consumers read every byte of every message and build a complete object graph from it. But if the scoring model uses only a subset of fields, the consumer is transducing the full thermal environment when only one property of it is relevant to the measurement you actually want. The relation is structural: you are paying full transduction cost for a signal you only partially use. In my field, a well-sited screen is one where the sensor responds primarily to what I intend to measure. Partial or lazy deserialization - parsing only the fields the scorer consumes rather than the full payload - is the same move: reducing the material surface the sensor responds to, without touching the environment at all.

---

**Using the lag's phase signature**

My most disorienting failure mode is not a wrong reading - it is a *temporally displaced* one. A high-thermal-mass siting produces a minimum temperature that accurately reflects the heat distribution of several hours ago. The reading is not an error; it is late. The thing I have learned is that if I cannot fix the thermal mass quickly, I can use its phase signature: the lag is predictable, it has a daily shape, and I can use that shape to decide how much to trust a reading rather than treating all readings as uniformly fresh. Your backlog has exactly this character: outputs produced during the peak window are accurate fraud scores for transactions that are no longer in a decision-relevant state. The lag is not uniform noise - it has a predictable daily envelope. One thing worth exploring in my world's terms: since the phase of the contamination is known, you could use it to triage output routing. During the backlog window, a message's position in the queue tells you something about whether the resulting score will arrive inside or outside the checkout flow's decision window - you could route or discard on that basis, rather than trying to drain the entire backlog at uniform priority. The stale readings do not need to be suppressed; they just need to be labeled and handled differently than the fresh ones.
