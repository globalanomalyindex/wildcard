Drawing on wave-particle duality — specifically its time-and-rhythm properties — three things surfaced from your situation.

---

**1. Formally partition the 312 components into two tiers, and audit only one of them.**

In wave-particle duality, the wave and the particle are not a degraded or incomplete version of each other — they are complementary observables of the same underlying thing, and each is fully valid within its own measurement context. Attempting to force one apparatus to do both jobs destroys the information you wanted.

Your Figma library and your React package are doing the same thing: they are two valid observables of the same UI intent, each optimized for a different apparatus (design tool vs. runtime). The ~156 components with React coverage and the ~156 without are not a failure to maintain parity — they are a working constraint that currently has no formal name, so it reads as organizational drift rather than a deliberate scope decision.

Concrete move: Annotate every component in the Figma library as either **production-committed** (has React coverage, held to strict visual parity) or **exploratory** (design-phase only; detaching is acknowledged and expected). Run your focus-state audit against the production-committed tier only — that is ~156 components, not 312. When leadership or the customer asks for a consistency commitment, you have a defined surface to point to. The "we have 312 components" library stays intact; what changes is that only half of it carries an explicit production guarantee.

---

**2. Make the "observation moment" constitutive of shipping, not downstream from it.**

The physics insight through time-and-rhythm: a quantum system doesn't crystallize into a definite state on its own — it only resolves at a measurement event. The distributed possibility (the wave form) evolves continuously, but a specific outcome only exists once an observation is forced. If you build your review protocol to observe *after* shipping, you get the particle only after it has already been deposited somewhere you can't easily move it.

Focus-state inconsistency wasn't found until a client flagged it. That means your current pipeline has no measurement event that forces resolution of interactive states (focus, hover, disabled) before production. The sprint cadence and code review weren't set up to look for it.

Concrete move: Add a requirement to the PR checklist for any component inside the production-committed tier: a Storybook story covering focus, hover, and disabled states must exist and render before merge. Not as documentation that can be filed later — as a merge prerequisite. This is a one-time policy change (one line in your PR template, one conversation with engineering), not a headcount cost. You are not building new Storybook infrastructure; you are changing when the observation happens.

---

**3. Replace the single audit with a weekly 30-minute parity pulse.**

The wave evolves continuously; measurement events are discrete. The problem with a one-time audit — or a quarterly design review — is that drift resumes the moment the audit closes, because the only crystallization event was the audit itself. What you want is not a larger audit, but a higher-frequency measurement schedule: small, regular events where one component is fully walked from Figma spec to Storybook story to production DOM, and any gap is filed as a bug.

Concrete move: Block 30 minutes every Monday morning. Pick one component from the production-committed tier. Open Figma, open the Storybook story, open the DOM inspector in staging. Name every gap you find as a specific ticket (e.g., "focus ring on Button/Primary missing `outline-offset: 2px` — filed against React package v2.4"). At 30 minutes per week for 8 weeks that is 4 hours total and covers roughly 40–50 components before the customer's next review cycle. The rhythm is the mechanism: it turns a closed audit into an open, accumulating record — which also gives you a concrete data artifact to show leadership when the "design velocity vs. consistency" tension needs resolving.

---

These are optional directions, each independent of the others — you could run all three, or start with just the tier partition since it costs only a labeling pass and immediately changes the scope of everything downstream.
