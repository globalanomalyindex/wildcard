I'm a pipe organ voicer — specifically, someone who works the nicking of flue mouths to achieve even speech across a rank. Not the builder, not the performer: the person at the bench cutting tiny kerfs into the languid, filing lips, controlling how each pipe enters speech. My whole practice lives in one small zone of the instrument, because the pipe's fundamental form — its length, its diameter — is fixed. I can't change those. I work the mouth.

Four things your project and mine actually share:

---

**1. The fixed-form leverage zone.**

In my trade, I cannot change a pipe's dimensions — they are set at casting. Every bit of my influence concentrates in the flue mouth: a small zone of adjustable geometry inside a large fixed form. The constraint seems limiting, but it means there is a precise, learnable place where all executable action lives.

Your portal has the same structure. Vendor contracts are fixed. Two stacks are what they are. State legal runs on its own clock. But the shared header code — the component nobody owns — is your flue mouth. It sits inside a fixed form, it's been left unworked because no one claimed it, and it is almost certainly the source of failures that manifest on every one of your 60-plus screens. In my world, the unclaimed mouth is the highest-leverage mouth. You might find it worth explicitly assigning ownership of that shared header as the first act, before any screen-by-screen work begins, and treating it as the zone where a small structural fix propagates everywhere.

---

**2. The rank sounds even only in passage — individual tests miss it.**

An organ voicer who tests each pipe individually can produce a rank where every single pipe passes, and the rank still sounds uneven in a scale run. The seam between pipes is only audible in passage. So I don't test individual pipes; I test runs.

Your 140 failures were likely identified screen by screen. But your users don't navigate screen by screen — they traverse a task path: arrive, authenticate, complete form A, upload document, complete form B, submit. A screen reader user on that path encounters failures cumulatively, and the effect of unevenness compounds in a way no single-screen audit can measure. It is entirely possible that a path with no individually catastrophic failures becomes catastrophic in sequence — focus management resets inconsistently, heading levels shift logic between forms, modal patterns change by vendor.

In my world, the fix is to play scales, not individual notes. The equivalent might be to run full task paths — housing application start to submit, food assistance start to submit — with a screen reader before finalizing the remediation priority order, rather than sorting the 140 failures by individual severity. Your call entirely; it's just that path-level testing is how rank-level unevenness gets found.

---

**3. Attack and steady-state are separate failure modes — the onset has its own physics.**

The nicks I cut into a pipe's languid control the attack: how the pipe enters speech, the transition from silence to stable tone. A pipe with no nicking will chiff — a burst of breathy turbulence before the pitch locks on. The steady-state behavior of the pipe may be fine; the problem lives specifically at onset.

Your users on prepaid data plans and five-year-old Android phones have an onset problem that is distinct from steady-state usability. The moment a form screen loads on a slow connection, before the layout settles, is when the screen reader fires. What it reads first — whether there's a meaningful landmark, a skip link, a sensible focus order before the rest of the page is ready — is the attack. A form that works correctly once loaded may chiff on slow connections in the seconds before it does. This is a class of failure that doesn't show up in standard audits run on fast connections, and it doesn't require wording changes to fix, which means it sidesteps your six-week legal cycle entirely. Whether throttled-connection testing is already in your plan, I don't know — but in my world, you always test the attack separately from the steady state.

---

**4. Before any voicing, make a register of the whole rank.**

Before I touch a single pipe, I map the rank: I play every note and note which pipes speak early, which speak late, which chiff, which cipher. I look for patterns — failures that cluster by position, by chest regulation, by common source. Because pipes fail in patterns, not randomly. A rank-level map tells me whether I am facing thirty individual problems or one problem with thirty expressions.

Your 140 failures almost certainly have this structure too. The question is whether anyone has deduplicated them to root cause — not just listed them, but asked: how many of these 140 trace to the same template, the same shared component, the same vendor's default behavior? A form-label failure appearing on 40 screens may be one defect in a shared pattern, not 40 defects. And a wording problem that appears 20 times may need only one legal submission, not twenty sequential batches.

In my world, the register always changes the sequence. If you haven't yet produced a root-cause map of the 140 — organized by source, not by screen — that map might materially change the work. Especially given that the legal batch size and timing is one of your hardest constraints: if you can show legal a single cross-screen wording fix rather than twenty individual ones, that is one six-week cycle instead of many.
