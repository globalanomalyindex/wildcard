A professor of fluid dynamics — someone whose daily work is measuring and modeling convective transport — looks at your portal situation and notices something familiar in the structure.

---

**Seed 1. The threshold that separates blocked from degraded**

In convection, the driving gradient doesn't produce uniform transport — it produces cells. Below a critical threshold (the Rayleigh number, in thermal convection), viscous resistance wins and nothing moves. Just above it, transport becomes dramatically faster. The threshold is the most important number in the system, and it's not obvious from a static snapshot: you have to ask "which zones are above it, and which are below?"

Your 140 failures are not a uniform distribution. Some are at-threshold: a missing form label means a screen-reader user sees nothing — the task is completely blocked, not merely degraded. Others are sub-threshold: annoying, slowing, but navigable. A violation count treats these the same. In my field, that would be like measuring total heat without asking whether convection has started. If you've mapped which failures co-occur on a single task path (housing application, food assistance intake), and which of those are blocking vs. degrading for your actual user populations, you've located the threshold — and those clusters deserve a different treatment than the long tail. You might find it useful to sort by "does this failure stop the task completely for AT users" before sorting by WCAG criterion or vendor ownership.

---

**Seed 2. Probe placement is load-bearing — you can't see the gradient directly**

In convection, the measurement problem is that temperature probes placed in the wrong spots show you a static picture of a dynamic system. The gradient that drives transport is often invisible in a uniformly-sampled reading; you only see it if your instruments are in the right places. A badly placed probe reports "nothing interesting here" right next to an active cell boundary.

A WCAG audit produces a count, but the count doesn't show you where user experience is most blocked. The gradient you actually care about — "where do screen-reader users on five-year-old Android phones with 15-minute session limits fail to complete a task?" — isn't directly visible in a violation list. It has to be inferred from proxy signals: which failures appear together on high-traffic screens, which criterion failures are disproportionately represented on the housing application path vs. general navigation, which failure types are known to be session-ending (a modal that traps keyboard focus on a library computer terminates everything). If you're not already scoring by task-path density and user-constraint severity, your probes may be showing you a flat picture of a dynamic system. Repositioning them doesn't create new data — it shows you the gradient that was always there.

---

**Seed 3. Stratification — when convection stops, layers form and diverge**

In thermal systems, stratification is the failure mode that happens when convective mixing stops. Each layer develops its own equilibrium, and the longer it persists, the harder eventual mixing becomes — the layers have diverged.

Your three-vendor situation across two stacks, with shared header code nobody owns, is structurally stratified. Each vendor is developing their own local equilibrium for WCAG handling — their own interpretations, their own fix schedules, their own component patterns. The unowned shared header is the blocked zone between layers. In convection, a blocked boundary between cells doesn't mean nothing happens — it means each side diverges further over time, and when you eventually need to align them (say, for a shared accessible focus-management pattern), the work is much harder than it would have been earlier. The structural question this raises, from inside my field: is there one shared zone you can deliberately keep "mixing" — maintained together across vendors — before the layers diverge further? Not necessarily the whole header, but one high-impact component (skip-nav, focus trap, error announcement region) that all three vendors implement consistently. The value of that isn't just compliance — it's stopping the divergence clock.

---

**Seed 4. The seam problem, and what maintains the gradient of non-compliance**

In convective cells, the boundaries between cells are where the most interesting (and often most chaotic) dynamics live. A blocked cell boundary doesn't stop transport — it redirects it, often in ways that are invisible to anyone looking only at the interior of a single cell. And separately: convection persists as long as something maintains the gradient from outside. Remove the gradient source and motion decays; identify what's maintaining the gradient and you understand the system.

Two connected observations for your situation: first, vendor boundary failures — the WCAG defects that live at the seam between two vendors' code, or in the shared-but-unowned header — are structurally invisible to any single vendor doing their own cell's accounting. Each side sees their interior; nobody owns the boundary. That's not a process failure, it's a predictable consequence of the cell structure. In my field you solve blocked-cell coupling with an interface layer that exists specifically to own the seam. Second, and separately: what is actively maintaining the gradient of non-compliance? The legal batch cycle (six weeks per wording change) is one viscosity — it keeps content failures in place even after they're identified. But the structural question is which gradient-maintaining forces are inside your control and which aren't. Technical failures (missing ARIA attributes, keyboard trap, contrast) cycle differently from content failures (label wording). You may find it useful to track them on separate flow rates, because they respond to completely different interventions, and mixing them into one remediation stream may be what's making the deadline feel uniformly hard when parts of it aren't.
