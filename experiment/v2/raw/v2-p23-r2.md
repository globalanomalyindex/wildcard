Drawing on wildlife forensic genetics — specifically the genotyping of degraded ivory samples, where the central problem is extracting a reliable signal from material that has been damaged, contaminated, and mixed — a few moves surfaced that seem worth handing over.

---

**1. Run a triage protocol on a CMS export before touching a single post.**

In forensic work on degraded material, the first step is never the full assay — it is a cheap classification pass that sorts material into three bins: clean signal, degraded-but-informative, and noise-dominant. Noise-dominant samples are archived, not processed; running expensive analysis on them wastes the resource and returns nothing usable.

Your 460 posts are similarly heterogeneous. The non-obvious move is not to identify your best posts and fix them — it is to explicitly identify the posts where remediation would produce no measurable signal improvement, and exclude them from the queue before you build it.

Export your CMS data (most platforms have a CSV export even without bulk-review tooling) and score each post on four observable signals: 12-month traffic trend from GSC (flat/declining vs. stable/growing), number of posts in the archive targeting the same primary keyword, last-updated date, and whether the post contains product UI screenshots. Assign each post to one of three tiers: remediate, consolidate, or freeze. Only tier-1 posts enter the approval queue. If the freeze tier holds even 50% of posts, your effective work universe drops from 460 to ~230, and your 20-24 approvals over Q4 represent a meaningfully larger fraction of the actionable set.

---

**2. Map keyword cannibalization from GSC before any writing work begins — cluster, then assign one dominant URL per cluster.**

Export your Google Search Console query data, group queries by semantic cluster (keyword + close variants), and for each cluster identify which URL holds the majority of impressions. That URL is the cluster's dominant. Every other URL ranking for queries in that cluster is a candidate for consolidation into the dominant or re-targeting to an adjacent, non-overlapping intent — not for individual remediation.

This matters for the approval-queue math: consolidation (redirecting a thin post into a stronger one, or updating the dominant to absorb the thin post's content) counts as one approval, not two. A cluster with four overlapping posts becomes one approval event instead of four. Across a 460-post archive where three writers never checked for overlap, you likely have 30-50 clusters with 2+ competing URLs; resolving those as cluster operations rather than individual post fixes changes the throughput ceiling substantially.

---

**3. For clusters where both posts carry link equity worth preserving, separate their impression footprints rather than consolidating.**

Sometimes you cannot merge two competing posts because each has independent backlinks that would be lost in a redirect. The forensic parallel: when two samples are contaminated by each other and you can't discard either, you redesign the assay to target non-overlapping regions of the sequence — so each assay reads only the signal it can cleanly resolve.

In blog terms: for any keyword cluster where two posts each have more than roughly 200 referring domains (check Ahrefs or Moz), rather than consolidating, use GSC to examine the specific query strings driving impressions to each. Update post A to target the broader navigational query (the "what is X" or "X overview" intent) and update post B to explicitly address a specific comparison or how-to sub-query ("X vs. Y" or "how to do Z in X"). The goal is that their GSC impression curves no longer substantially overlap — they stop competing in the same query space. This requires two separate approvals but produces two posts that reinforce rather than suppress each other.

---

These are optional moves — you may find the cluster-mapping one most immediately actionable since it changes how you scope the approval queue before any writing happens. Happy to pull any of these further, or to step back out.
