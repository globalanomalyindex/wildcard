Drawing on the practice of cylinder-seal engravers — specialists who cut continuous narrative friezes into hard chalcedony, where every decision compounds and no material can be added back — a few things surface about your situation.

---

**The ground-lines come before the figures.**

In a narrative frieze, the engraver cuts horizontal register bands first: ground line, figure zone, sky line. These bands tell the eye which figures are at the same level of the scene, which are dominant, which are detail. Without them, figures float in an undifferentiated field and the whole scene becomes unreadable — not because any individual figure is wrong, but because the architecture is missing.

Your 460 posts are an undifferentiated field. Three writers added figures without cutting the ground-lines first. The keyword cannibalization isn't fundamentally about duplicate content — it's about having no structural layer that tells Google (or a reader, or a new writer) which post is the authoritative answer for a cluster and which posts are subordinate. The remedy isn't to touch individual posts yet. It's to cut the architecture first.

Concrete move: Before you edit anything, build a 3-band post-type taxonomy in a plain spreadsheet — **Pillar** (one definitive answer per keyword cluster, the post you want to rank), **Supporting** (a specific sub-question that links up to its Pillar), **Dated** (a tutorial that shows old UI, quarantined for update). Assign every post to a band using only its title and URL — a 10-second gut-check per row, no reading required. At 460 posts, five hours of triage produces the structural map you've been missing. The sign-off bottleneck doesn't apply here because you're not publishing anything. This scaffold is what you navigate from for all subsequent decisions.

---

**When two figures compete for the same space, you don't delete one — you differentiate their action-lines.**

The failure mode at a frieze's repeat-seam is two figures crowding the same visual zone, each trying to be the dominant actor. The scene becomes ambiguous — whose arm is whose, who acts on whom. The engraver's solution is not to carve one out (that's irreversible, expensive, and destroys material) but to make their action-lines unambiguous: one figure acts, one receives. Both stay; the directionality is clear.

For your keyword-cannibalization clusters — say, three posts all targeting "how to [do X in your product]" — the expensive path is consolidating them through full rewrites and redirects, each requiring its own sign-off cycle. The lower-cost path is differentiating their intent signals without touching body content. The Pillar post keeps the head keyword. The Supporting posts get their title tags and H1s edited to target the long-tail variants within the cluster ("how to [do X] when [specific condition]", "how to [do X] for [specific use case]"). You can batch five clusters into a single sign-off request — all title tag and H1 changes, no body rewrites — and process them in one approval cycle instead of five. That's the lever: one sign-off covers five clusters if the changes are scoped narrowly enough that product marketing can evaluate the whole batch together.

---

**Economy of figures: the minimum set, and the moratorium that enforces it.**

A great frieze uses five or six figure-types, clearly differentiated, each carrying a unique structural job. The failure of an inexperienced engraver is to multiply figures with slight variations that don't differentiate structurally — they look different but do the same job. Under the constraint of chalcedony (no adding back), the discipline is: before you cut anything new, define the minimum figure-type set. Any figure that duplicates an existing type's structural job doesn't get cut.

Your hardest constraint is throughput: one part-time writer, one week per sign-off, roughly 4-6 touches per month. Every new post commissioned before the register is set is another figure cut into a scene whose architecture hasn't been settled — it will collide too, and the sign-off budget pays for the collision instead of the repair. The minimum-set discipline translates directly into a commissioning freeze: pause new posts for a defined window — say, six weeks — while the inventory audit and top-Pillar repairs run. In that window, redirect the writer's capacity to the highest-traffic posts in your Pillar band, ranked by current organic sessions. Those are the ground-line posts; any improvement to them multiplies across the authority of everything that links up to them. A Pillar post repaired at position 8 on a query you're now losing carries far more Q4 recovery leverage than a new post written into an unresolved cluster.
