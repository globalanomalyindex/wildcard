I work the provincial problem: tablets from Umma look different from tablets recording the same administrative formula at Girsu, and my obsession is the question of which differences are *meaningful* and which are just the noise of a reed stylus wearing down, a scribe trained at a different school, a clay body that sets faster in summer. **Seed 1. Sign identity is functional, not shape-identical - and the load-bearing features can be mapped**

In my field: across the thousands of provincial administrative tablets, the same sign varies enormously in physical impression - depth of wedge, angle, number of strokes - but it holds its identity because its *relational function* in the formula is stable. Assyriologists working this corpus had to develop a discipline of separating the functional core of a sign (what makes it legible and meaningful) from its surface variation (what changed because of medium, tool, or scribe). The method is to ask: which features, if you altered them, would cause a misreading? Those are the load-bearing ones. The rest can vary freely.

The mapping: your donor recognition survey tells you that the old crayon-style mark drives year-end giving from retired teachers - but what the survey almost certainly did not isolate is *which features of that mark* are doing the work. Is it the color palette? A particular letterform? The crayon texture itself? The proportions of a specific shape? The "old mark" as a whole is nine versions; retired teachers have encountered it in embroidered thread, cheap workbook print, and billboard vinyl. What they recognize is not one pixel-perfect file - it is a functional core that has survived substantial physical variation across those media.

Worth exploring: before spending any of the $12k on deployment, run a quick feature-isolation pass on the recognition data you already have. The sleek rebrand may have discarded a feature that is genuinely load-bearing; or it may have preserved all the load-bearing features and only changed the surface variation donors already tolerate. The method is in the survey, not in new design work: show retired teachers the rebrand alongside stripped-down variants of the old mark (just the color, just the shape, just the texture) and watch where recognition drops. That tells you which features the rebrand must protect.

**Seed 2. The distributed scriptorium problem - and how to set a tolerance threshold**

In my field: provincial Ur III scribes worked under three constraints that sound familiar - time pressure (clay dries in minutes), tool degradation (reed styli wear), and distributed training (no scribe in Umma learned at the central scriptorium in Ur). The result was variant corpora that administrators had to be able to read across all sites. The solution was not to achieve a single standardized form; that was physically impossible given the constraints. The solution was to establish a *threshold*: here is the minimum that makes a sign still readable as this sign, even if it looks different from the canonical form.

The mapping: your chapters print locally from whatever files they have, with no shared asset server, and $12k cannot build infrastructure that solves that. You have the same distributed-scriptorium constraint. The question is not "how do we achieve a single canonical mark everywhere" - that is not achievable under your constraints - but "what is the minimum set of features that must be preserved for the mark to still function as your mark?" A tolerance document costs almost nothing to produce and can be emailed to every chapter printer tomorrow.

In my world, this looks like a field guide: here are the three features that are non-negotiable (the mark fails if you alter these), and here is everything else that can vary without harm. Printers and embroiderers know how to use that. They are already adapting; you would be giving them the right adaptation rules rather than leaving them to guess.

---

**Seed 3. The two-tier structure: canonical text and working variant corpus**

In my field: there is a long-standing and productive distinction in the study of administrative cuneiform between the *canonical reading* established for publication and the *variant working corpus* that scholars actually use for research. The canonical reading is a convenience - it lets people cite a stable reference. The working corpus is where the meaning lives, because that is where all the provincial variants, medium-forced adaptations, and scribe-specific features are preserved. Making the canonical reading too canonical - insisting it is the only legitimate version - has caused real damage: tablets were misdated, formulae misread, because a provincial variant was dismissed as scribal error when it was actually meaningful.

The mapping: you have a donated rebrand that functions as a canonical text, and a working variant corpus (the nine versions, the old mark, the chapter adaptations). The question being implicitly posed - which one is the real logo? - may be the wrong question. In my field, the answer is: both tiers are legitimate and serve different functions. The canonical version handles external-facing contexts where a polished, consistent presentation matters (grant applications, new donor outreach, national communications). The variant corpus handles the contexts where recognition and medium-adaptation matter most (local chapter chapters, retired teacher donors, the workbooks already in classrooms).

This is not a compromise or a transitional phase toward eventual full replacement - it is a stable institutional arrangement that many organizations with long histories and distributed chapters use intentionally. The $12k question becomes not "how do we replace one with the other" but "where does each version belong, and who decides?" That is a governance question, which is cheaper to resolve than a production question.
