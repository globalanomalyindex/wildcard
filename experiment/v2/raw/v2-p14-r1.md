Drawing on convection — the physics of heat and mass moving through a medium along gradients — a few things surface when I look at your situation.

---

**1. Your 140 failures are not one population — and the split determines which pipeline each fix enters.**

In convection, the rate of transport is proportional to the *local gradient*, not the total heat in the system. The steepest gradient is where the most material moves with the least energy. Looking at your portal: some of those 140 WCAG failures almost certainly live in the shared header, the global stylesheet, or a common form component — code-layer issues (missing ARIA landmark roles, focus-order in the shared nav, color contrast in a global `.btn` class) that touch every screen simultaneously and require no wording change at all. Others are single-screen labeling or instructional text issues that do require legal review.

The move: before any remediation sprint begins, run all 140 failures through a two-axis sort — (a) *remediation type*: code/ARIA/semantic-only vs. requires wording change; (b) *scope*: global/shared-component vs. single-screen. The first axis tells you which pipeline the fix needs. The second tells you leverage: a fix to the shared header propagates to 60+ screens in one deployment. With a 9-month window and a 6-week legal cycle, you have roughly six legal batches available. Sending code-only fixes through legal review, or discovering mid-sprint that a fix needs legal sign-off, wastes those windows. The classification exercise takes a few days and sets the sequencing for everything else.

---

**2. The shared header with no owner is a dead zone — and dead zones accumulate debt rather than merely delay it.**

In a container with uneven heat, regions that no circulation reaches don't just stay static — they build up the scalar (heat, in the physics; WCAG failures, in yours) that the rest of the system can't drain. The header isn't just unowned in the sense of "unclear who fixes it." It's unowned in the sense that every audit, every regression test, and every downstream screen failure that traces back to it will re-fail until someone is accountable. Under court supervision, re-failures in testing don't just slow you — they reset trust with the court.

The move: before the first sprint, designate one party as the accountable owner of shared header remediation with a defined scope contract (which vendor, which changes, which test suite signs it off). It doesn't have to be permanent; it just has to be explicit for the duration of the nine months. The designation should be in writing, specific enough that a court-appointed monitor could verify it. Once circulation reaches the dead zone, it stops accumulating.

---

**3. The audit itself may have probed the wrong locations — and counting failures without checking where they were measured gives you a misleading gradient map.**

In heat-transfer measurement, a probe placed in a well-mixed zone, or in a stagnant corner, gives you a reading — it's just not a reading of the gradient you care about. Automated WCAG scanners are extremely good at enumerating certain failure types (color contrast, missing alt text, ARIA attribute syntax) but structurally limited at detecting others: session-timeout handling, focus traps inside multi-step form flows, screen reader announcement order under dynamic state changes, and keyboard-only nav through a form sequence that changes fields based on prior answers. For users on library computers with 15-minute sessions and screen readers, a session-expiry warning that fires with insufficient notice, or a focus trap on step 3 of a 7-step form, is a higher barrier than a dozen color-contrast failures on a page they never reach.

The move: take the 140 failures and cross-reference them against the three or four highest-stakes user journeys — at minimum, the housing application submission path and the food assistance application submission path, end to end — paying particular attention to session-boundary screens (the timeout warning, the saved-progress confirmation) and multi-step form transitions. Flag any screen in those journeys that does *not* appear in the 140 failures list: that gap is where the scanner's probe placement may have missed. A targeted manual audit of those screens, specifically for focus management under state changes and session-timeout behavior on a simulated 15-minute clock, will tell you whether the audit map matches the user-impact gradient.
