Drawing on mica — the layered mineral whose usefulness comes not from its bulk but from the precise differentiated properties of its individual sheets — three things surface for your Kafka lag problem.

---

**The measurement isn't in the right units.**

In mica, a thin sheet is used as a measurement substrate precisely because it contributes no signal of its own — what you measure is what's on it. Your consumer-group lag is currently tracked in message count (20M messages), but message count isn't the quantity your fraud SLA cares about. What matters is *seconds of unprocessed time* in the backlog: partition-lag × 95th-percentile deserialization latency per message.

Concretely: instrument your consumer group to expose a second metric — estimated lag-seconds per partition, derived from the existing lag-count metric multiplied by a rolling p95 of your deserialization time. At 9 PM you'd know immediately whether you're 45 seconds deep (recoverable) or 8 minutes deep (triggering a circuit-breaker). Set your load-shedding threshold and alerting at 45 seconds of estimated backlog time rather than at any message-count number. This costs no infra and takes maybe an afternoon to wire; it doesn't change throughput, but it tells you earlier and more precisely when you've crossed into the unprotected window.

---

**Your 48 partitions are treated as identical layers — they don't have to be.**

A mica book is a stack of mechanically independent sheets behaving coherently; the key structural property is that differentiated treatment of individual layers doesn't break the stack. Right now your deserializer applies the same full JSON-to-object mapping to every message regardless of which partition it arrived on or what the current lag is.

Add a lag-threshold-triggered selective parsing path: when estimated lag-seconds on a partition exceeds a configurable threshold (e.g., 30 seconds), switch that partition's consumers to a streaming JSON parser (Jackson's `JsonParser` streaming API, or `simdjson` if you're on a JVM shim) that extracts only the 3–5 fields the fraud model actually reads — transaction amount, merchant ID, card hash, velocity bucket, country code, or whatever your model's feature set actually is — and skips deserialization of the rest. Below the threshold, full parsing runs as today. This sidesteps the format-locked constraint entirely: you're still consuming the same JSON, you're just not materializing the full object graph during peak load. The CPU saving on a typical fraud-scoring payload (where the scored fields are a small fraction of the wire format) is often 40–70%, and you can tune the threshold partition-by-partition.

---

**Not all messages have equal signal value — your intake gate can select by that.**

Mica differentiates in geological settings by intercalating selectively: some layers are impermeable to ions, others trap specific molecules by size and charge. All 48 partitions currently push every message through the full scoring pipeline regardless of its prior-probability risk profile.

Build a pre-filter stage that operates on only the first ~50–80 bytes of each JSON message — enough to extract transaction amount and merchant-category code — and routes messages to a "full-score" queue vs. a "default-score" bucket during lag-spike windows. Transactions under a tunable floor (say, $25 at MCCs in your historically clean tier) get a pre-computed default score and pass through to the checkout without touching your scoring model. Full deserialization and scoring is reserved for the high-signal messages. This is a runtime circuit-breaker parameter, not a permanent policy — you'd enable it only when estimated lag-seconds crosses the threshold from strand 1 above, and disable it automatically when the backlog drains. No format change required; no new partitions; no broker cost.

---

These three are additive and independent — the measurement fix is worth doing regardless, the selective parsing path is the highest-leverage one if your scored fields are genuinely a small fraction of your payload, and the pre-filter is the more aggressive lever if you need to protect throughput under the hardest peaks.
