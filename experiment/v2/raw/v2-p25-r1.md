Drawing on the world of operatic claque organizing — the practice of coordinating paid applause cues across a live performance under strict positional constraints — a few things surface.

---

**The consequence inventory**

A chef de claque cannot station someone at every moment in a five-act opera. The constraint forces a specific question: which moments, if the cue fires late or not at all, cause irreversible damage to the evening — versus which ones the genuine house will carry anyway? The triage is by consequence, not by effort.

Your documentation has the same structure: not all pages are equally dangerous to let decay. The auth guide that locks a customer out for two days is not the same as a stale FAQ about notification preferences. What isn't in the plainest reading of your problem is that you could formalize this: build a consequence inventory by scoring each help center page and each README section on two axes — (1) how frequently the underlying system changes, and (2) how damaging a stale version is when a customer follows it. Multiply the two. Your top 5–10 pages are your high-consequence seats. Those are the only pages that need a hard review trigger on every related PR; the rest can stay on a softer cadence. You stop trying to maintain everything at the same intensity and instead concentrate protection where a miss is catastrophic.

---

**The pre-wired cue**

Claques are placed before the performance starts. Once the curtain rises, there is no reseating — improvising mid-act is how a claque gets caught. The placement has to be architectural, not responsive.

You currently learn about features at launch. The structural move isn't asking engineers to tell you earlier (that's asking them to improvise mid-act) — it's inserting a pre-wired gate before the moment of consequence. Concretely: add a required three-option field to your PR template — "does this change affect user-facing docs? [help center / README / none]" — and wire a "help center" selection to an automatic Slack notification to you before merge, not after. This costs engineers one click per PR. It puts you in the change-propagation path architecturally rather than relying on social goodwill or post-launch reports. The gate fires at the right moment because it was placed there before the work began, not because someone remembered to tell you.

---

**Withdrawing the dead signal**

The failure I fear most isn't a missed cue — it's a paid applauder still clapping after the moment has passed, or clapping for a performer who has already left the stage. The artificial signal is louder than the live one, and the house notices.

Your Confluence space with three years of unreviewed pages is exactly this: archived or superseded content still in circulation, ranking above current content in search because nothing has actively pulled it off the stage. Adding more current pages doesn't fix it — the dead signal keeps drowning the live one. The intervention is withdrawal, not addition. Export your Confluence space's full page list with last-modified dates; any page not touched since January 2024 with no assigned owner gets either archived immediately or flagged with a visible "needs review before trusting" banner. Then go one step further on your help center: configure search to explicitly exclude pages with an archived status rather than relying on the ranking algorithm to de-prioritize them — because it isn't.
