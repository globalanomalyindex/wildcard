A tidal forager's whole practice is organized around hard-constraint windows — not "go when convenient," but "commit to the window that's actually open, or lose it until next tide." That framing surfaced three things in your situation that I think are worth considering.

**1. Do all the zero-bandwidth work first, before touching any remote storage.**

In the marsh, you classify zones by which tidal window they require before you leave the truck. Work that needs the low-tide window goes first; work that needs a boat goes last. Your slow rural DSL and nearly-full 2TB account are the operative constraints — any strategy that starts with "consolidate everything to one place" requires re-uploading, which is the window you don't have. A practical first move: run a local-only deduplication pass on just the two external hard drives, using something like `jdupes` for byte-exact matches and then `digiKam`'s similarity scanner for perceptual matches (different resolutions, edited vs. original). This produces a concrete candidate-for-deletion list with zero upload cost and zero risk of touching originals. Only after you've confirmed that list do you decide what needs to move anywhere.

**2. Protect the genuinely irreplaceable files first — and that set is probably much smaller than you fear.**

A forager never clears a stand to zero; the worst error is harvesting what you can't regrow. But the key move is identifying which stands are load-bearing (the only population of a species in the marsh) versus abundant. In your archive, that's the "only good copy" question — and right now it's blocking everything because it's unresolved. You can surface the answer quickly without deduplicating anything: run `exiftool -r -csv` on both external drives to export a flat list of every file's date, size, and camera model into a spreadsheet, then compare those date ranges against what's visible in Google Photos (no download needed — just scroll the timeline). Files that appear in only one location, from pre-2020, are your protected set. In a corpus of 80,000, this is probably a few hundred files at most. Flag those as never-delete. Once that list exists, the emotional weight lifts off the other 79,000+, because you know which ones you can't afford to lose.

**3. The wedding photos are a targeted retrieval problem, not an archive problem — and you can solve that one right now.**

When I'm looking for a specific harvestable in season, I don't search the whole marsh. I go to the habitat it grows in. The wedding photos have a known date. On both external drives, running `find . \( -name "*.jpg" -o -name "*.JPG" -o -name "*.HEIC" -o -name "*.heic" \) -print0 | xargs -0 exiftool -DateTimeOriginal 2>/dev/null | grep "YYYY:MM:DD"` (substituting the actual wedding date) will surface every photo taken that day, regardless of what folder it's buried in under the old 2019 scheme. Do this on both drives. Then do a Google Photos date-range search for that same day. Pull all four results (both drives, Google, iCloud if accessible) into a single staging folder. That's your complete wedding-set candidate pool, assembled in one step, no upload required — and you can get it to your mom without solving the broader archive problem first.
