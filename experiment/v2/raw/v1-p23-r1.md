I'm a permafrost scientist - specifically, I study what happens to ground ice and organic material across different timescales when the thermal regime shifts. My daily obsession is the difference between material that's been cold-locked for centuries and material that's been cycling seasonally. They look the same from the surface and behave completely differently when disturbed.

Three things I notice from your problem:

**1. Age is a triage instrument you already have.**

In permafrost terrain, material doesn't decay uniformly - depth is a proxy for how long something has been frozen, and frozen things don't degrade at the same rate as things in seasonal cycling. The oldest material is the most locked, the most changed from the original conditions it was formed in.

Your 460 posts span eight years. That age gradient is already a thermal profile. The 2018-2020 posts were formed when your product looked different and three writers had no coordination system - they've been sitting frozen since publication, accumulating decay. Posts from the last 12-18 months are in your "active layer": they've been touched, checked, are more likely current.

What transfers is this: you don't need to audit all 460 to find where the most degraded material is concentrated. A spreadsheet of posts by publication year, cross-referenced against your search console's traffic drop per URL, gives you a depth profile without touching a single post. The oldest cohorts, sorted by traffic loss, are where the outdated UI tutorials and the worst keyword pile-ups almost certainly live. You can rank your repair queue before you open a single document.

The technique in my field is thermal profiling before drilling - you map the temperature gradient to find where you should invest drilling time. In your context, publication date plus search-console delta-traffic is that thermal probe.

**2. Keyword cannibalization needs group treatment, not individual repair.**

In permafrost systems, stored load doesn't release one piece at a time. When thaw begins, the material that was co-located - locked together in the same frozen zone - releases together and the instability is structural, not local. You can't address one slumping slope without accounting for what's adjacent to it.

Three writers independently covering the same keywords didn't create three separate problems. They created a cluster problem: if you fix one of three competing posts without touching the others, the search signal stays fractured because the competition is between your own pages. A single "fixed" post competes against two unfixed ones - you've moved one piece of ice but the thermal load hasn't changed.

What transfers is the principle of treating the cluster as the unit of repair, not the individual post. For any keyword group where cannibalization exists, the repair options are: consolidate into one post (redirect the others), differentiate them so they target clearly distinct intents, or retire the weakest performers. The point is that any one of those three actions is the *minimum* viable unit - touching fewer than that leaves the system unstable.

This also matters for your sign-off bottleneck. If you bring a cannibalization cluster to product marketing as a single decision (these three posts, here's the proposal for which survives and how) you may use one sign-off week rather than three.

**3. The surface looks stable; the decay is subsurface and you need instruments, not inspection.**

A classic diagnostic problem in permafrost research: the ground above a degrading permafrost layer can look entirely solid. The surface vegetation is green, the soil looks normal. The only way to know what's happening below is to drill, or to use indirect instruments - subsidence mapping, ground-penetrating radar, temperature sensors. Surface inspection is actively misleading because the cold-lock below the active layer is invisible to it.

Your CMS without bulk review is a surface-inspection problem. You can only see posts one at a time, which means your mental model of the corpus health is built from what happens to surface-visible posts (the ones that get traffic, get linked, get touched). The actually degraded material - the frozen stuff - is invisible because nobody goes there.

What transfers: the instruments you need may not require developer time. Search Console exports all indexed URLs with their traffic and click data - sorted by traffic loss over the period since the algorithm update, that's a subsidence map. A simple URL list exported from your CMS, joined against those numbers in a spreadsheet, gives you a ranked view of the full corpus health without touching the CMS interface post-by-post. You're not building a bulk review tool; you're doing a satellite pass from data you already have access to.

The reason this matters for your constraints: without this map, you're spending your one-piece-per-week repair budget on posts that surface in conversation or feel urgent, not necessarily the ones with the highest recovery potential. An hour building the profile redirects that same budget toward the posts where repair actually moves your Q4 number.
