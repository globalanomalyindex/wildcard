A bryologist walked in — someone who studies moss, specifically how water and nutrients move through a mat that has no internal plumbing. Through that lens, a few things in your situation look familiar.

---

**Isolated colonies, not a mat**

In a moss mat, water and dissolved ions move laterally without any central routing — purely by adjacency and concentration gradient. That only works if the clumps physically touch. When a mat fragments into isolated colonies, each colony drifts independently; you can't slow it down without restoring contact between the clumps.

Your five-language freelance system is structurally the same thing: each translator's personal glossary is a colony that touches no other colony and no shared surface. The divergence you're seeing — three German names for the same loyalty program — is exactly what isolated colonies produce. Every translator locally optimizes without reference to anything but their own previous work.

In my field, the repair isn't to build a vascular system (an expensive central routing authority). It's to give the colonies a surface to touch: a single, authoritative terminology file that every translator reads from. Even a thin shared surface — one canonical glossary treated as the source of record — lets terminology diffuse passively across the mat without requiring a new review round. You might find it useful to ask: what's the minimum shared surface all five languages could touch?

---

**The mat holds water so it doesn't have to respond to every rainstorm**

A moss mat can retain six to twenty times its dry weight in water. That retention is what decouples the mat's internal moisture from the noise of external weather — it doesn't need to respond to every rain event; it holds what it accumulated between events.

Your English source copy is the weather: it changes weekly, live, without notice. Your translators have no buffer between them and the weather. Every live marketing edit is a signal they can't detect but are implicitly expected to track.

In my world, the structure that builds a buffer is one that retains state: the mat holds what it absorbed. The corresponding structure for you might be an authoritative, versioned English "glossary of record" that updates on a deliberate cadence — weekly, or per release — distinct from the live marketing pages. Translators sync to that buffer, not directly to the live pages. The change-blindness problem (marketing editing without telling anyone) becomes a much smaller question: who owns the buffer, and when does it get refreshed? That's a narrower coordination surface than "notify translators of every live edit."

---

**Broadcast dispersal doesn't require a routing table**

Moss spores travel without addresses. They broadcast wide-radius and let the environment filter for where germination conditions are right. No routing table is maintained; propagation is passive and scales effortlessly.

Your change-notification problem currently requires someone to know which translator last touched which page in which language — an expensive routing table to maintain under weekly edits, and one you presumably don't have.

In my field, broadcast plus environmental filter is the move: emit the signal, let the receivers filter for what's relevant to them. The translated version: what's the cheapest signal you could emit whenever an English page changes — even just a "last modified" timestamp in a shared spreadsheet row — that any translator could passively scan for pages in their language? No coordinator needs to maintain a translator-page assignment map. The translators (or their tools) do the filtering.
