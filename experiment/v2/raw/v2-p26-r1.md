Drawing on moss and how it moves resources without a vascular system...

Moss has no internal plumbing - no pump, no pipeline from a center to a periphery. It moves moisture by capillary differential across its surface: wherever a drier cell is adjacent to a wetter one, flow happens automatically. The colony self-levels by gradient, not by coordination. Three things this surfaces for the German-loyalty-program / Japan-feature-name problem:

---

**One.** Your three German loyalty-program names exist because each translator maintains a private glossary - there is no shared wetter cell for them to equalize against. The lowest-cost fix is not a meeting or a review round: it is a small, locked master glossary (a shared document, not an editable one) containing roughly 30 terms - the loyalty program name in all five languages, the top feature names, any brand nouns that have caused confusion - that every translator is contractually required to consult before accepting a job. Not a process, not a workflow: a reference surface they touch once. The gradient then works: if the locked document says *Treueprogramm* and their private file says something else, the delta flows one way. You might try piloting this on just the German team with the loyalty-program name as a single-row test case, confirm the naming converges across two consecutive job cycles, then expand to all five languages.

**Two.** Moss doesn't monitor its environment continuously - it goes dormant and reactivates on the signal of rehydration. The Japan feature-name problem almost certainly traces to the fact that your English source pages change weekly and no signal reaches the translators. Because you can't add review rounds, the lever is earlier and cheaper: have someone on the marketing side - whoever has publish rights - run a one-time setup of a 5-term diff check against the 30 terms in the master glossary before hitting publish, and send a single-line alert to a translation channel when any of those terms changes. The translators stay dormant until the signal fires. The cost is front-loaded (building the 30-term list and the diff check once) rather than recurring (a review round every week).

**Three.** Because moss spreads by a single spore-dispersal event that reaches the whole colony at once, it handles the "broadcast without a coordinator" problem natively. When the master glossary changes - when you rename the loyalty program in English, or when a feature gets a new name - the right move is to version the glossary (a simple version number or a date stamp in the document header) and include that version in every job brief you send to freelancers. The translator then knows immediately, without anyone telling them, whether they are working from the current glossary or a stale one. The version number does the coordination work that currently requires a human to do or doesn't happen at all.

---

All three of these are optional and each can be done without the others. The locked 30-term glossary is probably the highest-leverage first move given that it addresses both the German divergence and the Japan mismatch at once and costs only the time to compile it.
