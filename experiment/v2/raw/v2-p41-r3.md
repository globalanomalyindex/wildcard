Drawing on marquetry of dyed veneers cut with the donkey — the foot-pedaled fretsaw where you cut a whole stack of veneers at once and sort the pieces afterward — a few things surface here.

---

**The shape is ground truth; color is an artifact you have to read.** A dyed veneer looks uniform until you angle a light across it — then the early wood and late wood reveal that the dye penetrated unevenly, and what looked like a coherent surface is actually an artifact of the finish, not of the underlying structure. The failure I fear most is committing a cut to that artifact instead of to the true grain beneath.

Your folder names, filenames, and file modification dates are the dye artifacts: they tell you how files were ingested, not what images they contain. The folder scheme you abandoned in 2019 is especially misleading — it is an artifact of that era's ingestion workflow, not a map of the photographs. For the wedding photo hunt specifically, the EXIF `DateTimeOriginal` field is the true grain — it is written by the camera at shutter-click and cannot be accidentally falsified. If you know the wedding date, you can run `exiftool -r -if '$DateTimeOriginal =~ /YYYY:MM:DD/' -filename -directory /path/to/drive` across both external drives and recover the full set in one pass, entirely offline, with no upload and no bandwidth cost. Folder structure is irrelevant; filenames are irrelevant. The date in the EXIF is the grain.

---

**Same-stack cutting gives you shape-identical pieces across all color variants.** When I cut a packet of veneers with the donkey, every layer in the stack gets the same cut simultaneously — the shape is invariant regardless of what color or grain each veneer happens to have. The pieces are the same silhouette from different materials.

For a photo collection with duplicates at different resolutions, edited and original pairs, and copies scattered across four sources, the equivalent of the cut shape is the **perceptual hash** — a compact fingerprint of the image's visual content that is stable across resolution changes, mild compression, and color-grading. Two files with the same perceptual hash are the same photograph in different dress. Tools like `czkawka` (cross-platform, fast, free) or `digiKam`'s built-in duplicate finder will cluster your collection by perceptual similarity without requiring any upload. Within each cluster, the highest-resolution, least-compressed member is provably the one to keep — and the others can be deleted without the catastrophic-loss risk, because you have confirmed they are the same shape. Files that have no cluster-mate are never touched.

---

**Cut the whole stack before sorting individual pieces.** The specific failure mode the donkey technique prevents is trying to resolve each veneer individually before cutting — examining each one for grain direction, deciding whether it belongs, handling it separately. That is how you introduce misalignment. You stack them, cut once, and sort afterward. The structural unlock for your situation is the same: stop trying to make individual photo decisions and run two collection-level passes first. Pass one: EXIF-date filter across the drives to extract the wedding cohort as a named folder — that is the urgent job, done offline, no deletion needed. Pass two: perceptual-hash clustering across all four sources to produce a map of confirmed duplicates. Both passes require zero uploads and create no irreversible changes. Only after those two passes do you have the structure needed to make targeted moves — delete confirmed cluster redundants to recover storage, and consider the 2TB Google Photos budget as a separate question from the local archival question. The paralysis comes from treating this as a photo-by-photo decision problem; the two passes turn it into a cluster-level decision problem, which is a much smaller set of choices with far lower individual risk per decision.
