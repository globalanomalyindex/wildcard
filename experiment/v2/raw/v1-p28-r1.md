I'm a reef restoration biologist specializing in coral micro-fragmentation — the technique of cutting living coral colonies into fragments of two or three polyps each to trigger stress-accelerated regrowth in nursery tanks. My entire practice runs inside brutal resource limits: tank space, temperature windows, the short window between cut and mortality. Every decision I make is a triage decision. Looking at your archive, a few things genuinely rhyme.

---

**1. Tank-space triage: you can't fragment everything in one cycle, and that's not a failure — it's the method.**

In a reef nursery, the fatal mistake is trying to fragment every donor coral at once. Tank space is finite. The corals that go in this cycle are chosen by value: genetic rarity, request frequency, at-risk status. Everything else stays in the sea until its turn. The work moves in deliberate waves, and each wave is complete.

Your 70,000 images and your 3 hours a week are structurally the same problem. Trying to retroactively tag the whole archive is trying to fragment the whole reef in a weekend — it fails on resource limits and produces nothing usable in the meantime. What works is declaring a first-cycle batch explicitly: the images that appear most in marketing requests, the shows most likely to generate licensing interest, the last three or four productions. Tag that batch completely. Leave the rest untouched until cycle two. The archive doesn't get smaller, but the high-value material starts returning results immediately, and the scope of each session is bounded.

You might find it useful to define your batch criteria the same way I define mine — not "easiest" but "most likely to be requested in the next six months."

---

**2. The provenance ticket is what makes a fragment usable — not decoration, not paperwork.**

In my field, a coral fragment without a tag is worthless for reef seeding. I can't prove donor provenance, I can't meet the genetic-diversity targets in a restoration contract, I can't honor a sale. The tag isn't documentation attached to the fragment; it *is* the thing that makes it a fragment rather than just a piece of coral.

Your rights/clearance status is the same structure. An image without a clearance note isn't an untagged image — it's an image that cannot be sold. The licensing sale you lost wasn't lost because the image didn't exist or because the file couldn't be found; it was lost because the clearance ticket wasn't attached to it. The downstream buyer needed provenance, and provenance wasn't there.

In my world, that means clearance status gets tagged first — before costume description, before performer name, before anything else. It's the one metadata field whose absence has a known, specific dollar cost already demonstrated. Everything else is useful; this one is load-bearing.

The practical shape: a simple three-value rights flag (cleared / needs review / uncleared) applied to every image in your first-cycle batch before you add any other tag. Even a partial archive with reliable rights flags is more useful for licensing than a fully described archive where clearance is unknown across the board.

---

**3. The phase-shift failure: amplifying biomass without amplifying retrieval axes.**

The nightmare scenario in micro-fragmentation isn't low survival rate — it's the phase-shift. You grow a thriving nursery of a thousand fragments, then a single warm-water pathogen moves through and wipes it, because all thousand fragments came from three donor colonies. You amplified biomass without amplifying diversity. The nursery looked like success right up until it didn't.

Your archive has a version of this. Fifteen years of photographs — real biomass — organized by a single retrieval axis (date) that was fine for one use case (production recall: "what did we shoot in November 2019?") and invisible to a different class of user entirely (licensing: "which performers in this image have cleared rights?"). The system was built for one type of query and amplified without ever adding a second axis. When a second query type arrived — clearance confirmation, costume search — the single-axis index couldn't respond, and the whole archive's value dropped to near zero for that use case.

In my world, the lesson is to diversify donor-colony provenance from the start, not after the pathogen arrives. For you it's too late for the oldest material, but not for any shoot going forward: building even one additional index — rights status — in parallel with the date index as you photograph new productions means the archive grows two axes at once from here on, and the failure mode never compounds further.
