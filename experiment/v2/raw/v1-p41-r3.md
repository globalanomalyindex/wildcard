I'm a marquetry practitioner who works specifically with dyed veneers cut on the donkey - the foot-operated fret saw that lets you stack two sheets and cut them simultaneously so the positive piece from one fills the negative void in the other exactly. My particular obsession, shaped by the lens of signals-and-noise, is **reading grain through dye**: dye is visually dominant but structurally uninformative, and in my field the most expensive mistakes happen when you let the salient feature (color) do work that only the diagnostic feature (grain direction) can actually do.

Three things I notice from this corner:

---

**1. Your search is running on the noisy signal, not the diagnostic one.**

In dyed veneers, if I sort pieces by apparent color, I get a pile that looks coherent but will fail under finish - because color is what the dye put there, not what the wood is. The grain direction, invisible at a glance, is what determines whether two pieces will register. I never sort by color. I sort by grain.

Your folder structure, filenames, and thumbnail previews are color. They are visually salient and cognitively dominant - but they are not what makes two photos "the same" or "different." You're currently searching for the wedding set by navigating folders and recognizing thumbnails, which is exactly sorting by dye. The wedding photos exist in your collection; they're just not readable through that signal.

The diagnostic signal for photo identity is the pixel-level perceptual hash - a fingerprint computed from the image content itself, independent of filename, folder, resolution tier, or export format. Tools like `fdupes -r`, `rdfind`, or dedicated photo tools like `digiKam` or `dupeGuru` compute these and group files by content identity rather than naming. If you haven't yet tried content-hashing across all four sources as a single flat scan, that is probably the single highest-leverage move available: it would surface the wedding set as a cluster without requiring you to navigate any folder scheme at all. *Whether that's the right tool for your setup is your call - I'm only noting that in my field, the rule is never let the colored surface do the sorting.*

---

**2. Edited and original versions are not duplicates - they are veneer and packet.**

When I cut a packet, I produce two pieces simultaneously: the inlay piece and the waste piece that the void came out of. These are geometrically identical but functionally opposite - one is the figure, one is the ground. I never treat them as "duplicates to be deduplicated." I keep both because each is irreplaceable in its role.

The edited/original pairs in your collection have this structure. An edited JPEG and its original RAW are not two copies of the same thing - they are figure and ground: one is the visual artifact you made a creative decision about, one is the lossless record you can never regenerate from the JPEG. Tools that deduplicate aggressively will collapse this distinction and you will lose the ground. The failure mode in my field is laminating with only one piece - you discover the void too late.

If you do run a deduplication pass, it may be worth treating edited/original detection as a *prior step* rather than a post-processing cleanup - identifying RAW-plus-derived-JPEG pairs first, then setting those aside before running any deletion logic. The structural question is: does the deduplication tool you use understand this relationship, or will it sort by apparent content similarity and collapse the pair? *Entirely your choice how to handle this - I only notice it because I live in a world where two nearly identical things can have opposite functions.*

---

**3. The seam-under-finish test: differential examination to break ties.**

My failure mode is a seam invisible in raw form that appears under finish because I misread grain under ambient light. My response is not to choose randomly between the two pieces - it is to examine both under a second, diagnostic angle: raking light, which reveals grain direction that ambient light conceals. Two views of the same object; one diagnostic, one not.

When you find two files that appear identical in thumbnail - same shot, different resolution - you face exactly this situation: you cannot tell from the thumbnail which is the better copy. The rule I use is: never choose by the ambient feature (visual appearance, file size alone); expose the diagnostic feature instead. For photos that means: check the EXIF fields that record whether a file is a camera original vs. a re-export (OriginalFilename, Software, or the presence/absence of lens metadata); check pixel dimensions to confirm which is the higher-resolution source; check if one has GPS or flash data the other lacks. These are the raking-light views. Any file that has more intact EXIF is almost certainly closer to the camera original, which is the one you keep in the event of a tie.

*This won't tell you which file is in which folder - but it gives you a tie-breaking rule you can apply locally when you find candidates, without having to guess.*
