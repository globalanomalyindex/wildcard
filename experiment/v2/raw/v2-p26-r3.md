Drawing on a stone quarryman who splits dimensional rock using the feather-and-wedge method — someone whose entire trade runs on working with the material's latent grain rather than against it...

---

**1. Key to the rift, not the surface.**

In feather-and-wedge splitting, the rift is the stone's latent cleavage plane — invisible on the surface, but the only plane where a clean split is possible. A quarryman who keys their drill line to the surface texture rather than the underlying rift will fracture the block unpredictably.

Your translators are keying their glossaries to the English *string* — the surface. When marketing edits the loyalty program's English name, each translator re-solves "what do I call this?" independently, producing three German answers.

Concrete move: assign each recurring term (loyalty program, every named feature, every product tier) a short internal ID — a stable token that never changes even when the English label does. Store your canonical glossary against that ID, not against the English phrase. Practically, this is a small spreadsheet or a column in your existing translation memory: `TERM_042 | Rewards Program | [German] | [Spanish] | [French] | [Japanese]`. When marketing renames the program in English, you update the English column once; every translator's reference still points to TERM_042 and the agreed translations hold. The three-German-name problem is a symptom of a missing stable key; adding the key makes the glossary resilient to upstream edits without adding review rounds.

---

**2. Drive wedges in passes, not one wedge all the way.**

The method's discipline is that you never advance one wedge to final depth before the others are roughly even. You make a first pass, advancing every wedge a little; a second pass, a little more — distributing load evenly across the whole split line. A quarryman who drives a single wedge to full depth first induces a stress concentration and the crack runs off-grain.

When the marketing team edits a live page, the "wedge" of that change is driven all the way to publication before any translator sees it. Each translator then resolves the new phrasing independently — the stress concentrates wherever that translator's individual choice happens to diverge from the others.

Concrete move: build a lightweight delta-notification step triggered only on source changes, not on every delivery. Practically: whoever owns the English CMS sets up a simple rule — when any page containing a glossary term is published, a short excerpt of the changed sentences is automatically posted to a shared translator channel (Slack, email thread, wherever they already communicate). Translators don't do anything unless a term conflict appears; if two of them flag the same new phrase within 24 hours, one quick round of alignment happens before individual delivery, not after. This is not an extra review round on the full translation — it is one synchronization pass on the delta only, which fits inside the existing turnaround. The Japan feature-name drift also becomes catchable here: a changed feature name in the English copy would surface as a flagged delta before it reaches a published translated page.

---

**3. Know which grain cannot be cut across.**

The quarryman's worst outcome — a block shattered rather than split — happens when the drill holes cross the rift instead of running along it. There are planes in the stone where you simply cannot split clean; drilling there wastes the block.

Your Japan feature-name mismatch is exactly this fracture. The app's own UI renders a string; the translated marketing copy invented a different label; the customer sees both and they don't match. The app's string is the rift — it is set by the engineers, not negotiable, and anything running across it breaks.

Concrete move: export the app's own string table (the actual labels the UI renders) for each of your five locales, and load them into your translation memory as a locked no-translate list — strings that translators must use verbatim, because they must match what the customer sees in the app. This is distinct from your preference glossary: the glossary says "prefer this term"; the no-translate list says "this string is fixed by the product and cannot be locally adapted." In practice it's a second tab in the same spreadsheet, populated once from a dev export, updated whenever engineering ships a localization change. Any translator who opens a job where a locked string appears sees it flagged; there is no judgment call to make.
