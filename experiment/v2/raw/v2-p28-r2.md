Drawing on dynamical systems theory, a few things surfaced that the usual "tag everything" framing tends to miss.

---

**A dynamical system that isn't being measured is unobservable - and unobservable systems can't be steered.**

In control theory, the reason you put sensors on a system is not to capture every detail but to reconstruct enough of the state to act. Your archive is currently measured on exactly one coordinate (date). Rights status, performer name, costume - those dimensions exist in the real state of every image, but they're invisible to the system. The operative move here isn't retroactive tagging of 70,000 files; it's adding a minimum viable set of required fields at ingest for every shoot going forward - say, four to six fields: production title, performer(s) in frame, costume or scene descriptor, and rights clearance status (cleared / pending / unclear). New shoots take maybe ten minutes to log at download time. Within two or three years, the forward-looking archive is fully searchable and clearable, even if the back catalog stays dark. The lost licensing sale points specifically at rights status: making that a hard required field at ingest means no future shoot enters the archive without a clearance note attached.

---

**Effort concentrated at the high-query part of the state space recovers far more than effort spread uniformly.**

High-dimensional dynamical systems have a useful property: most of the interesting behavior lives on a low-dimensional submanifold - a small fraction of the state space accounts for almost all the dynamics. Your query load is almost certainly the same shape. "Carmen, any production" is a request you'll get again; a specific date in 2009 probably isn't. Before touching the back catalog chronologically, rank your production titles by two proxies: (a) which shows have come up in marketing requests in the last two years, and (b) which titles have the most licensing or press value. Take the top 8-10 shows - probably 3,000-5,000 images - and tag those as a focused block using an AI-assisted auto-tagger (Google Vision, Amazon Rekognition, or the open-source CLIP-based tools will pre-fill costume color, scene type, and approximate crowd/solo/duo framing for free; you review and correct). That block will cover the large majority of future marketing requests and rights queries. The remaining 65,000 images can be addressed over years, or left for a dedicated intern sprint, without blocking any high-value use.

---

**The backlog is an integrating system, and integrators that accumulate faster than they discharge enter a losing regime.**

A pure accumulator - no outflow, constant inflow - eventually saturates regardless of initial conditions. The question worth making explicit is: at 3 hours/week, can you process more images than each production season adds? If you're shooting 1,500 images per production and running 4-5 productions per year, new intake alone is 6,000-7,500 images annually. Three hours/week is about 150 hours/year. That means you need to process the intake *plus* retroactive backlog at fewer than 1 image per 1.5 minutes to stay even - which is impossible manually but achievable with AI pre-tagging doing the first pass (rough benchmarks: 500-1,000 images/hour for auto-tag + spot-review). Run that arithmetic once, concretely, against your actual shoot volumes and your current tool options. If the numbers show you can discharge faster than intake with AI assistance, you're in a recovering regime and the backlog shrinks over time. If not, the structure of the problem tells you that the 3-hours/week budget alone can't solve it - and you'll know to make the case for either an intern sprint on the back catalog or an institutional AI tagging subscription, rather than grinding at a losing rate.
