What you're describing - a Figma library and a React package that are both valid accounts of the same design intent, that cannot be made to fully overlap without cost, and that produce paradoxes whenever someone tries to treat them as one thing - has a structure I recognize.

**Seed 1: Complementarity is a duality to navigate, not a contradiction to resolve.**

In quantum mechanics, wave and particle are not two wrong accounts of one thing - they are two *valid but mutually exclusive* descriptions, each fully correct in its own observational context. The system is not broken. Physicists stopped spending energy trying to unify them into a single ontology and started building the protocol for switching between them cleanly.

Your Figma library and your React package are genuinely different kinds of descriptions of design intent. The Figma library is the wave account: it holds the full space of intended states simultaneously - every variant, every density, every edge case, the design in superposition. The React package is the particle account: discrete, shippable, actualized implementations that can be observed in production. One holds the full possibility space; the other collapses it into deployed reality. The drift isn't evidence that one is wrong. It's evidence that you've been asked to use them as if they're the same description.

In my field, the move isn't to build one formalism that contains both - that requires adding hidden degrees of freedom that make the system unwieldy. The move is to build a **conjugate pair protocol**: a defined translation ritual that specifies exactly what is preserved, what is lost, and what is acknowledged when you move from one description to the other. You might find it worth asking whether the artifact that's missing from your workflow is not a bigger Figma library or a bigger React package, but the explicit translation protocol between them - the document that says, "these are the Figma states that have no React equivalent, and this is the team's current ruling on how each is handled."

---

**Seed 2: The rhythm of observation bounds or unbounds the drift.**

In quantum optics experiments, whether you see wave behavior or particle behavior depends entirely on when you measure - the timing of observation is not incidental, it's causal. Interference patterns only appear when no which-path information is recorded. Particle impacts only appear when that information is captured. The measurement cadence governs what kind of truth you have access to.

In your problem, the customer review was a measurement event. It actualized the particle description (production UI) against the wave description (design intent) and the gap was visible. The trouble is that measurement event happened post-ship - at a cadence that let the gap compound over many sprints. 

In a well-designed experiment, you don't discover your apparatus was measuring the wrong thing after you've run it a hundred times. You specify the measurement cadence as part of the experimental design. You might find it interesting to think of "when does Figma get compared to production" as a measurement cadence question rather than a process question - because the answer determines whether the divergence is bounded (caught each sprint, each sprint a small delta) or unbounded (caught by a customer, a large accumulated delta). The cadence is causal, not just diagnostic.

---

**Seed 3: Every collapse should be a logged event.**

In quantum mechanics, the act of measurement forces a superposition to collapse into a single eigenstate. The collapsing is not the problem - you need to collapse to ship. What matters is that every collapse is a deliberate, recorded event in the experiment log. You always know what you measured, what state it collapsed to, and what alternative states are now inaccessible from that branch.

Each Figma detach is a collapse event: a component that held multiple possible states (the full variant space) is forced into one specific local configuration with no link back to the source. In my world, that event would go in the log automatically. In your world, detaches currently happen silently and accumulate into invisible debt.

The quantum version of your detach log is simple in structure: every detach is tagged with *why* the collapse was forced - what variant, state, or prop the React package couldn't express. That log is not a compliance artifact; it's a **measurement dataset**. Sorted by frequency, it becomes an engineering prioritization backlog with actual signal: these are the states designers need often enough to detach for. If you're working with two months and no headcount, a prioritized list of the ten most-detached missing React props is a tractable target - and it comes directly from the physics of where collapses are happening, not from anyone's opinion.
