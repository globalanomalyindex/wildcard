Looking at your situation, three things genuinely map.

---

**The gradient is near zero, and that's why the transition stalls.**

In my field, diffusion rate is proportional to the concentration difference driving it. When two regions are nearly equal, movement nearly stops - not because the medium is hostile, but because there's no driving force. Your rebrand isn't failing to spread because chapters reject it; it's failing because chapters have no reason to reach for it. The old files are right there, they work, they've always worked. The gradient from "central source" to "local chapter" is essentially flat. The standard move when you need faster diffusion isn't to push harder - it's to raise the concentration at the source boundary so high that the gradient reinstates itself. In your context: if the canonical new-mark files were dramatically easier to find and use than the old ones (pre-sized for common print formats, organized by use case, one click away), the thermodynamics changes. You don't need enforcement; you need a steep enough gradient that the path of least resistance runs through the new file. A $12,000 budget applied mostly to making that source boundary frictionless - a well-organized shared asset folder with ready-to-print files for every known use case your chapters actually have - does the structural work that mandate-and-police cannot.

---

**Two fronts superposing is the physics, not a failure state.**

When two diffusing substances meet in a heterogeneous medium, they don't fight to a winner - they coexist. Each gradient runs on its own logic. What you're calling a "rebrand problem" may actually be a controlled-superposition problem: the crayon mark and the new mark are both valid in their respective concentration zones, and the question is whether you've *designed* that coexistence or just drifted into it. In my field, designed superposition means you know which zones carry which substance and you set those as boundary conditions intentionally. In your situation: a formal policy that says donor-facing and legacy major-gift communications stay with the crayon mark for a defined period, while all new chapter onboarding materials, new workbooks ordered after a date certain, and digital-first contexts carry the rebrand - that is not compromise, it is designed superposition. The two gradients coexist on purpose, the system knows its own geometry, and neither mark is provisional or embarrassed. You might find it useful to ask which of your communication surfaces are in the "donor-chemistry zone" and which are in the "new-chapter zone," and draw that map before asking which mark is right.

---

**Legacy deposits in a heterogeneous medium age out; you don't clear them.**

In transport problems, when you change the boundary conditions in a heterogeneous medium, some pockets - low-permeability zones, dead-end channels - stay saturated with the old substance for a very long time. You don't drain them; that's expensive and often impossible. You let them deplete on their natural schedule while you run the new front through the high-permeability channels. The embroidered shirts, the decade of workbooks in classrooms, the donated billboard faces - these are low-permeability pockets. They will age out. Shirts wear out, workbook stockpiles deplete, billboard contracts end. Concentrating your $12,000 on clearing those pockets (re-embroidering shirts, reprinting stockpiled workbooks) is spending against entropy doing your work for free. The high-permeability channels - new chapter onboarding kits, digital assets, any print run being ordered fresh - are where the gradient change is both cheap and durable. You might find it clarifying to sort your nine-plus version contexts by their natural refresh rate, and put the budget where things turn over fastest.
