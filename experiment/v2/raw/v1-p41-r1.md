I'm an evolutionary biologist who studies adaptation failure — specifically the ways populations end up exquisitely tuned to a world that no longer exists, or carrying structural legacies that used to serve a purpose and now just cost energy. **1. Evolutionary lag: the folder structure as a relict organ**

In my field: when an environment shifts, an organism's expressed form often lags behind. The trait was adaptive once; it got encoded deeply; now the world has moved on but the expression hasn't, because nothing has actively selected against it yet. The organism is still running on calibration from a previous era — not wrong exactly, just tuned to a signal that's no longer the signal.

The mapping: the 2019 folder scheme is evolutionary lag made visible. It was calibrated to a workflow that no longer exists, and it's still structurally present because you haven't had the opportunity to select against it — you've been too busy just adding photos. What makes this more than a metaphor: the failure mode in my field is that organisms with deep evolutionary lag are *surprisingly hard to find* when you're looking for them. The landmark cues you learned to navigate by are all from the old map. You're searching for the wedding photos using a map drawn for 2019's terrain.

The provocation, from my side: in palaeontology, the first move when you suspect evolutionary lag is to stop trying to read the current organism's traits as adaptive and start mapping them as *relict structure* — artifacts of history, not guides to current function. You might find it useful to treat the existing folder structure the same way: not as something to reconcile or extend, but as geology to read. The question shifts from "where should these go?" to "what were the rules then, and where would they have put the wedding?" — which is a navigable historical question even when the current structure isn't.

**2. Genetic load: the capacity crisis as a selection threshold moment**

In my field: populations carry "genetic load" — the accumulated cost of variants that are neutral or mildly deleterious but haven't been purged because selection pressure was insufficient. Load is always present and always has a cost, but it doesn't trigger action until that cost exceeds a threshold. When it does — a drought, a bottleneck, a new selective environment — the population that had been coasting on slack suddenly has to pay.

The mapping: 80,000 photos spread across 4 locations, many at multiple resolutions, edited and original pairs both present — this is load. The per-item cost is low; nothing individually demands attention. The cost is invisible until the storage hits 2TB and suddenly the accumulated load requires a response. That threshold moment is structurally identical to what I study: the environment changed (capacity ran out), and now every variant that wasn't worth purging before is costing something real.

What transfers: in population genetics, load management doesn't require eliminating every deleterious variant — it requires reducing load below the threshold. And the most efficient way to do that is to target the *highest cost, lowest fitness* variants first: in your case, that's not "edited versus original" (that's a judgment call) — it's the resolution duplicates. A 12MP version of a photo that also exists at 48MP has near-zero fitness value and high storage cost. The genetic load framing suggests a practical ordering: you don't need to solve deduplication completely before you can act; you just need to identify the obvious low-fitness, high-cost variants first, because they're the ones that can be safely purged without needing to know the full copy graph.

**3. Local optima escape: build the new habitat first, don't repair the old one**

In my field: a population trapped on a local fitness peak can't climb down to climb higher — every step away from the current peak is a step toward lower fitness, even if a much better peak exists nearby. The only reliable escape is a change in the fitness landscape itself: the environment shifts, the current peak collapses in value, and now the intermediate steps are no longer costly.

The mapping: any strategy that tries to fix the existing structure in-place (reorganize the drives, merge Google Photos and iCloud, reconcile the 2019 folders) is climbing around a fitness peak that already proved itself inadequate. The reorganization has to compete with the current state at every step, which is why it never gets finished — each intermediate state is messier than either extreme.

What transfers from my field: the escape from local optima is not willpower applied to the climb — it is building the new habitat first. In evolutionary terms: introduce a new selective environment (an empty canonical archive) that makes the current state costly by comparison, then let selection act. In practical terms this means the move is not "sort the existing photos" but "establish a new canonical home, define what goes there, and move only what you can confirm." The old drives don't get reorganized; they get read as source material for the new canonical archive. The new space has no legacy structure to fight against. This reframes the wedding photo search too: instead of searching the old structure, you temporarily treat "find the wedding set" as the first migration job into the new canonical space — a bounded, completable task that also proves the workflow.
