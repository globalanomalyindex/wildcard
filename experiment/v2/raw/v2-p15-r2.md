Drawing on the concept of **umbrella** — specifically its failure modes — three things surfaced about your situation.

---

**1. The dangerous zone isn't the obvious holdout — it's the border adopter.**

An umbrella's worst failure isn't the person standing clearly outside it; it's the person just inside the canopy edge who believes they're covered while wind-driven rain still reaches them. The structurally similar position in your situation: chapters that are already *attempting* to use the new mark but are printing from unofficial files — a low-res JPEG someone grabbed from the website, a Word doc with an embedded logo from the newsletter. They think they're in compliance. Nobody flags them because they're nominally "using the new brand." The result is undetected, uncontrolled variant production that looks worse than either system.

*The move:* before spending any of the $12,000 on rollout support, audit specifically the chapters you believe have already adopted the new mark. Ask each one to send you the file they're currently printing from. That audit is a few emails and an afternoon — and it will almost certainly reveal the worst variants are coming from the people you already counted as solved.

---

**2. Two deliberate zones managed cleanly beats nine accidental variants.**

An umbrella canopy is held out by spokes that all have to reach the same radius for the geometry to work. A single immovable spoke at the wrong length warps the whole canopy — and the failure propagates to everything adjacent. In your situation, the classroom workbooks are fixed spokes: a decade of printed material that cannot be recalled or replaced at any realistic budget. Any unification plan that tries to stretch the new mark over those fixed points produces a warped composite — worse than either a cleanly old or cleanly new system.

*The move:* formally define two zones and stop trying to unify them. Zone A — new print runs, digital properties, any donated billboards scheduled for replacement, new volunteer shirts — gets the new mark exclusively, with a shared asset folder (even a Google Drive or Dropbox with access-controlled folders per chapter, at zero marginal cost). Zone B — classroom workbooks, existing embroidered shirts, any printed materials already in distribution — stays with the old mark and is listed explicitly as permanent, not "aging out." Making this two-zone system intentional transforms nine accidental variants into two managed ones. The board member's agency delivered a real rebrand; this just gives it a territory it can actually hold.

---

**3. Test whether the recognition link transfers before you spend rollout budget on the assumption that it will.**

An umbrella inverts — collapses inside-out — when force is applied from the wrong direction. It holds fine against rain from above; it fails under wind from below. The agency-donated rebrand was designed against one force vector (strategic coherence, professional presentation) and the donor-recognition finding from your spring survey describes a different one entirely: an emotional signal built up over years of association with a specific visual form, driving year-end giving. Those are not the same vector. More rollout effort — more chapter compliance, more consistency pressure — applies force from the strategic direction. If the recognition link is specific to the crayon-style mark's visual elements rather than portable to the new mark, more rollout is force from exactly the wrong direction.

*The move:* before allocating any of the $12,000 to new-mark rollout materials, run a short recognition test with 30-40 retired-teacher donors — 15 questions, mixing the new mark, the old mark, and three or four variant versions — to determine whether the year-end giving link is specific to the crayon mark's visual elements or whether it transfers. This is not a decision about which mark "wins"; it is data on whether the revenue signal is mark-dependent. If the link is portable, roll out with confidence. If it is not, that finding changes how you frame the new mark to the board: not "we're phasing out the old mark" but "we're running both marks in their respective zones and protecting the signal that drives giving." Your donor survey already gave you the question; this test gives you the answer before you spend money on an assumption.
