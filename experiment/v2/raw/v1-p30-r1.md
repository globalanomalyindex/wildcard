I'm a federal court stenographer. Fifteen years in criminal court, the last ten building my own theory dictionary - the layer of abbreviation that sits on top of standard steno. My obsession is the architecture of that dictionary: identifying which phrases actually dominate the record in this court, then pre-assigning them to the shortest possible strokes before the trial begins. The mechanism I think about every day is frequency-weighted layout of effort. The failure I fear is rank inversion: a brief assigned to a phrase that turned out to be rare, while the truly frequent phrase got no abbreviation, so I'm spending maximum strokes on maximum volume. My constraint is that all structural decisions must be committed before the session starts - I cannot reorganize my theory mid-deposition.

Here's what I notice looking at your building.

**The frequency-rank inversion your layout has built.**

In my world: a steno dictionary assigns every word its "natural" spelling by default - which means common and rare words cost exactly the same effort. The fix is pre-commitment: before high-volume sessions begin, identify the actual highest-frequency phrases and assign them to the minimal-motion zone. The failure mode I live in fear of - frequency-rank inversion - is when the most-traveled paths carry the most expensive strokes. That's what your current slotting has built. Your fastest movers are assigned to wherever space happened to be free, which means their pick paths are random with respect to their frequency, and probably inversely correlated with it (because heavy movers cycle through more often, they also churn their slots more often, which tends to push them outward over time). Your 55-line-per-hour rate is the cumulative cost of that inversion.

The structural connection: pick path length is stroke cost, and the frequency distribution of your SKUs is the corpus. The question my field would force is not "how do I pick faster" but "does the travel-cost rank of every slot mirror the actual pick-frequency rank of the SKU assigned to it?" If not, the architecture of your dictionary is wrong, and no amount of faster walking repairs it.

What you might find useful: before you move a single pallet, pull 90 days of pick data per SKU and rank by actual picks-per-day - not received units, not inventory value, just picks. Then rank your locations by travel-time-to-pack-station. The assignment should make those two rank lists mirror each other. In my theory work, the input that surprises most practitioners is how *steep* the frequency cliff is: the top 5% of words account for over 60% of all court text, so the briefing payoff concentrates sharply. Your velocity distribution is probably similarly steep - meaning a relatively small number of SKUs, re-slotted to golden-zone locations, will recover most of the lost throughput.

**The pre-session batch-commit rule, and why partial reorganization is its own failure mode.**

In steno, there is a discipline about when structural theory changes are made: always off-line, always in a complete batch, never rolling during live sessions. The reason is that a partial reorganization leaves two overlapping maps active simultaneously - the old muscle-memory and the new brief - and mid-session conflict produces errors worse than either state alone. A hybrid-state theory is more dangerous than a bad theory you know completely.

The structural match to your situation: you have eight weeks. The receiving-cluster problem and the slotting problem are two independent pathology loops that both want intervention before peak - but if you're still mid-re-slot when the peak volume hits, your pickers will face the warehouse equivalent of a hybrid-state theory: some SKUs in new locations, some in old, and no mental map that covers either cleanly. The deadline pressure here is not "do it faster" but "commit the architecture completely before the session begins." A 70% re-slot completed by week seven is worse than a 100% re-slot completed by week six, even if they involve the same number of SKUs moved, because the incomplete version forces pickers to hold two maps simultaneously under peak cognitive load.

What you might find useful: set a hard freeze date well before peak (week five or six at the latest), after which no slotting changes happen until after peak. The design decision and the physical move happen before the freeze; nothing crosses the line after it. That's the pre-session commit.

---

**The dual-input collision and the pre-assigned channel.**

When two speakers overlap in court - the judge and a witness simultaneously - I have two competing streams arriving in the same channel. The structural fix is not faster fingers; it is pre-assigned priority routing established before the session: certain input types flow through a dedicated path, and the separation is a structural commitment, not an improvised one. Without it, the two streams corrupt each other regardless of individual typing speed.

Your dock surges on Monday and Thursday, and inbound pallets compete with picker travel for the same physical channels - the aisles. The structural shape is identical: two flows, one channel, no pre-assigned separation. The congestion you're describing, pallets pushed into pick aisles, is the warehouse equivalent of both speakers recorded on the same track. The fix in my world is not to process one stream faster but to route them separately, and to pre-commit that routing before the surge begins.

What you might find useful: designate specific aisles as receiving corridors on receiving days - where inbound pallets stage, travel, and land - and route pick operations around them in advance, not reactively when the pallets have already arrived. The structural move is the separation itself, decided before Monday morning, not a workaround improvised at dock-open. The two-day receiving cluster you described is actually useful for this: because the surge is predictable and recurs on the same days, you can commit the channel separation schedule once and run it every week rather than improvising a new workaround each time.
