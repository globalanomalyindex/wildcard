I'm a mineralogist who studies phyllosilicates - the sheet-structure silicates, mica chief among them. My obsession is cleavage: why some materials split trivially along one plane and resist cutting across any other, and what that geometry reveals about where energy actually lives in a structure. Let me look at your problem from there.

---

**Seed 1: the cleavage plane you haven't cut along yet**

In mica, the crystal has perfect basal cleavage - it splits effortlessly along the interlayer plane because the bonds there are weak, while cutting across the laminae is costly because you're breaking strong covalent bonds. The geometry of the material determines which cuts are cheap. You have a fixed partition topology (48 partitions, consumers matched to them) - that cleavage plane is exhausted. But JSON has its own internal geometry: a document has fields, and fraud scoring almost certainly reads a fraction of them. Full deserialization - tokenizing, inferring types, allocating a complete object - crosses every bond in the document whether you use those fields or not. A streaming partial parser that exits early after extracting only the scorer's required fields is cutting along the cheap seam: the document's own structure offers a cleavage plane you haven't hit yet. If your scorer uses 8 fields out of 40, you might be spending 80% of your CPU budget on bonds you then throw away. Worth profiling which fields the scorer actually reads before concluding that deserialization is an irreducible cost.

---

**Seed 2: thin sections are transparent where bulk is opaque**

The interesting thing about mica as a measurement material is that a block of it stops light entirely, but grind it to a thin lamina and it transmits with high fidelity. The same substance, the same information content - but only accessible when the thickness is reduced. Your backlog has the same property: 20 million messages is opaque to the checkout flow (nothing scores in time), but the fraud signal you actually need to protect the evening peak is not uniformly distributed across those 20 million. High-value transactions, high-velocity account behavior, flagged device fingerprints - a thin section of the backlog. In my field we'd call this a sampling strategy with a structural prior: instead of draining the queue in arrival order, a priority lane that identifies the thin section of the backlog where fraud risk is actually concentrated lets you make that section transparent (scored within your 1-minute SLA) while the bulk drains overnight. The information needed to triage already exists in the message metadata before full parse. You might find it useful to ask what the scorer would do if it could only process 5% of the evening backlog - and whether that 5% could be the right 5%.

---

**Seed 3: interference fringes - layered structure makes the signal richer in aggregate**

Thin mica films produce optical interference patterns: light reflecting off adjacent layers constructively and destructively superposes, and the resulting fringes encode the layer spacing with high precision. The signal emerges from the aggregate of layers, not from any single one. Your consumer loop is almost certainly processing messages one at a time through the same parse-and-score cycle. If messages in a partition arrive from correlated sessions - same device, same account, same network block - a micro-batch that parses a small buffer of messages together rather than one-at-a-time does two things. First, it amortizes the setup cost of parsing (schema detection, allocator warm-up) across the batch, which is a direct throughput gain even if each message is independent. Second, it opens the door to aggregate signals: three modest-risk transactions from the same device fingerprint arriving in 200ms is a pattern that single-message scoring misses but batch scoring sees. The same CPU budget may produce both more throughput and richer signal if the unit of work is a micro-batch rather than a single message. In my field, you lose the interference pattern the moment you examine one layer in isolation.

---

**Seed 4: the measurement port must survive the environment it's observing**

Mica was historically used as the window in furnace observation ports and early radiation-exposure measurement devices - the one material that could survive the extreme environment while still letting you see through it. The measuring artifact had to be inside the thing it was measuring. Your lag counter - consumer group offset lag - is produced by the same consumer group infrastructure that is saturated during the peak. If your consumers are CPU-bound to the point of stalling, their offset commit cadence degrades, and the lag metric you're watching may understate the real backlog depth precisely during the events where accurate measurement matters most. The observation port is inside the furnace. You might find it worth checking whether your lag alert is reading committed offsets (which lag behind true consumption when commit interval stretches) versus high-watermark tracking, especially during the peak window. A saturated consumer that commits offsets every 5 seconds instead of every 500ms is reporting yesterday's lag.
