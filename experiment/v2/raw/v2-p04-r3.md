Drawing on weather-station thermometry — specifically the challenge of siting and calibrating sensors whose materials must respond fast enough to capture events that pass quickly — a few things surface when I look at your Kafka problem.

---

**1. The staleness gauge you are probably not tracking**

In precision thermometry, a sensor can report "a temperature" accurately but still be wrong — if its thermal mass is too high, it integrates over a longer window than the event lasts and reads what the air *was* rather than what it *is*. The failure is invisible unless you measure *response lag* separately from *reading accuracy*.

Your consumer lag metric (message count) is the equivalent of "the thermometer eventually reaches the right value" — it tells you how far behind you are but not whether what you are currently processing is still live. What you probably are not emitting: a per-partition gauge of `(wall-clock minus Kafka record produce-timestamp)` for the message currently being consumed. This number is available on the consumer record before deserialization — essentially zero CPU cost. When that gauge crosses 60 seconds on any partition, your consumer group has crossed into backlog territory and every scored message is already useless to the checkout flow.

Concrete move: instrument each consumer instance to emit `consumer_message_age_seconds` (wall-clock minus record timestamp, partitioned by consumer group and partition ID), alert at p50 > 60s, and use that gauge as the decision input for any routing policy you build next. Without it, you are operating blind to the live/stale boundary; with it, you have a precise instrument that costs nothing at your CPU bottleneck.

---

**2. A fast-lane consumer group that seeks past the backlog**

A thermometer with high thermal mass in a fast-cooling event: the fix is a separate low-mass probe that can track the rapid change, while the primary instrument finishes its slower integration. You can run both simultaneously.

Kafka consumer groups are independent — they hold their own offsets. A second consumer group, call it `fraud-scorer-live`, can be configured to seek each partition's offset forward to the position that corresponds to `now minus 90 seconds` at the start of the evening peak window (you can compute this offset by scanning recent records or using a timestamp-to-offset lookup). This group processes only the fresh arrivals; your existing group continues draining the backlog overnight. No producer changes needed, no new partitions, no broker cost increase — just a second consumer group and the seek logic.

Concrete move: stand up `fraud-scorer-live` as a separate consumer group on the same 48-partition topic; at peak-window start (say, 17:00), have it seek each partition to the offset whose timestamp is within 90 seconds of wall-clock; let it run with the same instance count you already have (partition count is your ceiling anyway); decommission or throttle it after the peak window. Your `consumer_message_age_seconds` gauge from strand 1 is what tells it when to stop seeking and let normal consumption resume.

---

**3. A routing proxy that bifurcates live from stale before deserialization**

The real CPU cost is deserialization. A Stevenson screen's job is to let *only the air you care about* reach the sensor — the louvered walls exclude ground radiation and direct sunlight so the sensor is not spending its response budget on sources that do not represent the ambient air.

Your consumers are spending their entire deserialization budget on messages regardless of whether they are inside the fraud-scoring freshness window. If the Kafka record headers carry a produce-timestamp (or if your message envelope has a fixed-offset timestamp field you can read with a raw byte slice before full JSON parsing), a thin proxy consumer can classify each incoming message as live or stale *before* deserializing it, and route it to one of two downstream topics: `fraud-topic-live` (small, 8 partitions, fast drain) and `fraud-topic-backlog` (full backlog queue). The fraud scorer pulls exclusively from `fraud-topic-live` during the peak window; `fraud-topic-backlog` drains on a separate, lower-priority consumer pool overnight.

Concrete move: if your message format has a timestamp in the first N bytes that can be extracted without a full JSON parse (even a simple `indexOf` scan for the timestamp field key), implement a proxy consumer that routes on that timestamp with no other deserialization, writing to two topics; size `fraud-topic-live` at 8 partitions (well below current CPU ceiling, low broker cost), and shift the fraud-scorer instances to that topic during peak hours. If the format has no parseable timestamp before full deserialization, this strand requires waiting until next quarter's format change — but it is worth pre-designing now.

---

The three moves stack: the gauge (1) costs nothing and makes the other two precise; the seek-forward consumer group (2) is the fastest to deploy and needs no proxy layer; the bifurcating proxy (3) is more involved but eliminates deserialization waste on stale messages entirely, which is where your actual CPU budget is going.
