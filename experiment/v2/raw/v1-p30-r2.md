**concept: momentum — lens: measurement**

I'm a physicist whose work lives at the measurement end of classical mechanics — not the elegant theory, but the grubby question of what you can actually observe and what the number you're reading actually encodes. Let me look at your operation through that lens.

---

**1. Your rate metric is a composed quantity, and you may be treating it as simple.**

In mechanics, momentum is mass × velocity — and a key mistake is measuring only the composite and intervening on the wrong factor. Your lines-per-hour dropped from 80 to 55. That number compounds two distinct things: the structural factor (how far a picker travels per pick, set by slot assignment) and the execution factor (how fast a picker actually moves, affected by congestion, confidence, fatigue). A decline in the composite can come from either, or both — but the interventions are completely different. If you instrument travel distance per pick separately from picks per travel-hour, you might find that the structural factor (scattered fast movers) accounts for most of the loss — and that adding temps without reslotting just adds bodies to an unchanged travel problem. The measurement question is whether your current numbers let you decompose the rate into its factors, or only tell you the product.

---

**2. An inelastic collision leaves residue that a conserving system would not.**

In mechanics, an elastic collision is one where kinetic energy is fully recovered afterward — the system bounces back to its prior state. An inelastic collision converts some energy to heat or deformation, and the residue persists. Your Monday/Thursday receiving surges are collisions between two workflows sharing a floor. The question is whether your system is elastic or inelastic: when the receiving wave passes, does pick-floor efficiency snap fully back, or does it stay degraded for some period? If it's inelastic — pallets linger in aisles, pickers reroute around them for hours after receiving ends, supervisors stay pulled into put-away coordination — then your effective operating capacity is lower than your physical capacity every week, and it gets worse when peak adds more volume to the same collision. Worth measuring: what is the recovery time after a receiving event? If it's two hours, that's a bounded problem. If it's until Tuesday, that's a structural one.

---

**3. Impulse is force × time — the same total work, with a different peak.**

A key result in mechanics: the same change in momentum (the same total work delivered to a system) can be achieved by a large force applied briefly or a small force applied for longer. The peak stress is entirely a function of concentration, not of total load. Your Monday/Thursday appointment clustering is a large force applied briefly — a lot of receiving throughput required in a short window. The total weekly receiving volume is what it is, probably set by supplier contracts. But its temporal shape is a variable. Spreading appointments more evenly across the week doesn't reduce the total work; it reduces the peak force on the dock-plus-floor system at any one moment. The measurement version of this question is: what is your actual capacity at the dock per day, and how much of the Monday/Thursday surge exceeds it? Knowing the excess tells you how much flattening is actually needed, and whether partial redistribution (adding Wednesday appointments, not full five-day spreading) would be sufficient to stay below the threshold.

---

**4. Where momentum goes in a collision: tracing the transfer, not just the loss.**

When two bodies collide, momentum doesn't disappear — it transfers. What looks like "lost" picker efficiency during a receiving surge is actually efficiency transferred elsewhere: into waiting at blocked aisles, into longer travel paths around misplaced pallets, into supervisor attention pulled from picking to receiving coordination. The measurement move is to follow the transfer rather than just noting the output drop. Each transfer point is a specific intervention site. If most of the loss goes into aisle blockage (the pallets-in-pick-aisles problem), the leverage is physical separation — a staging zone or a receiving window that keeps pallets off the pick floor. If most goes into supervisor capture (your two supervisors pulled from one workflow into another), the leverage is pre-assigned receiving lead roles so supervision isn't the bottleneck. You likely can't instrument this finely before peak, but a rough tally — where do supervisors' hours actually go on receiving days versus non-receiving days — would point at the highest-transfer collision first.
