Drawing on tide-table harmonic constituent computation — specifically the practice of decomposing a single observed signal into its underlying periodic sources — three things surfaced from your problem that felt genuinely useful.

---

**1. Build a constituent table before you start remediating.**

In harmonic tide work, the observed water-level signal looks like 140 irregularities, but underneath it are maybe 12 constituent forces — each with a distinct amplitude and phase — that generate the whole surface. The craft move is to decompose first, because fixing a surface anomaly without knowing its source means you might fix the same root cause five times and leave the dominant one untouched.

The 140 WCAG failures in your portal are a surface signal. The underlying constituents are probably something like: missing or misassociated `<label>` elements, insufficient color-contrast tokens propagated from shared components, broken focus order across form sequences, missing ARIA roles or landmark regions, and session/timeout handling. Each root-cause category generates multiple failure IDs across multiple screens.

Concrete move: before any remediation sprint, build a spreadsheet that maps all 140 failures to their root-cause category, and adds one column — whether that category requires state legal re-approval of wording or is a code-only fix (ARIA attributes, contrast tokens, focus management, landmark structure). Most WCAG failures are not wording changes and don't need the six-week legal cycle. That single column immediately separates the work that can move in the next thirty days from the work that must be batched through legal, so you can sequence the nine months against actual bottleneck structure rather than against failure count.

---

**2. Don't let your triage group failures that share a root cause under different criteria.**

A specific failure mode in harmonic analysis: if two constituent frequencies are close enough and your data window is too short, they alias — their energy bleeds together into one apparent signal, and you publish predictions that look precise but are wrong at a periodicity neither constituent correctly represents. The practical version in your problem: failures filed under different WCAG success criteria may be the same root cause, and grouping them separately means you fix them separately at three times the cost.

Here is the specific case I'd watch for: your users on prepaid data and library computers with 15-minute session limits, hitting slow-loading forms. That combination is likely generating failures recorded under SC 2.2.1 (timing adjustable), SC 3.3.1 (error identification — because the session expires and the error message appears), and SC 1.3.1 (info and relationships — because auto-recovered state on resubmit may not preserve field structure correctly). Three failure IDs in your 140, but one root cause.

Concrete move: audit the failure list for any cluster where SC 2.2.1, SC 3.3.1, and SC 1.3.1 failures appear on the same screens, especially screens that are form-heavy and slow to load. A session-extension warning triggered at the 12-minute mark, plus server-side preservation of form-field state on session expiry, addresses all three — and none of that change touches form wording, so it bypasses the legal approval queue. The 12-minute threshold gives users enough time to act before the hard cutoff, which is the specific behavior SC 2.2.1 requires.

---

**3. Use failure counts from the shared header to resolve the vendor ownership problem.**

In tidal prediction for mid-latitude harbors, the M2 constituent — the principal lunar semidiurnal — often accounts for 70-80% of the total signal amplitude. Solving M2 first is not optional; it is the only rational sequencing because every other effort is noise until it is addressed. The analog in your portal: shared header code that "nobody owns" is almost certainly your highest-amplitude constituent. A missing skip link, an absent `<nav>` landmark, or a broken focus trap in the header creates a failure on all 60-plus screens. Those 60-plus instances are filed as individual failures in the 140, but they are one fix.

The useful thing about amplitude is that it makes the political argument concrete. The vendor ownership vacuum exists partly because no vendor is being charged for failures on screens they don't control. The constituent table you build in move 1 lets you count exactly how many of the 140 failures trace to the header. If that number is 30 or more, bring it to the court-supervised vendor coordination meeting framed this way: "These 30 failures appear on every screen and trace to 3 issues in shared header code. No vendor currently owns the header. We are asking the court to assign header stewardship to a named vendor lead with a 30-day remediation window." The count is your lever — it shows that resolving the ownership question changes the scoreboard faster than any screen-by-screen triage, and it gives the court something specific to order rather than a general instruction to "coordinate."
