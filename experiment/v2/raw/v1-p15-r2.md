A concept came up from outside your problem: **umbrella**, viewed through its failure modes.

---

I'm working with umbrella structures not as objects but as a relational model - specifically how protection works when it's delivered from above through a canopy while everything beneath it remains distinct, and where that structure breaks down. Three things from that model genuinely map onto what you're describing.

**1. The architecture is "shared overhead, differentiated below" - and that's the move you may not have named yet.**

In umbrella design, the protective function lives in the canopy, not in what's underneath. The people below can be carrying different bags, wearing different shoes, moving at slightly different paces - none of that matters as long as they're under the same span. The failure mode I care about is when someone tries to solve the "differentiation problem" by making everything underneath identical. That's not how the structure works and it collapses the wrong thing.

Your organization may have two marks when what you actually need is one mark at the canopy level and acknowledged variation below it. The crayon-style mark and the sleek rebrand aren't necessarily competitors if they occupy different registers - one as the "chapter-level program identity" that appears on workbooks and embroidered shirts and resonates with retired teachers, the other as the "organizational identity" that appears on grant applications and the national website. Many nonprofits run exactly this architecture: a parent mark that signifies the institution, and a program or campaign mark that carries the emotional warmth. The structural move is naming them as different things rather than treating one as broken and the other as correct. Your $12,000 doesn't have to fix a problem that may be better reframed as a solved architecture.

**2. Umbrella failure concentrates at the perimeter, not at the center - and your problem probably does too.**

An umbrella looks intact from underneath when it's actually failing at the edges. The rim leaks, the tips pull through, the fabric separates from the ribs at the outer ring. The center stays dry long after the perimeter has given out. The diagnostic move is to stop checking the center and walk around the edge.

Your mark fragmentation - the nine-plus versions - almost certainly follows this pattern. The national office's own collateral may be in decent shape. The chapters printing locally from whatever files they have are the perimeter. The classroom workbooks are at the very edge. A governance effort that starts by establishing what the mark "officially" looks like at the center doesn't touch where the degradation is actually happening. The more useful first move is a perimeter audit: catalog exactly which nodes are producing their own versions and what's driving each one (missing files, bad files, outdated files, no-files-at-all). That's a different problem than "which mark is canonical," and it's one you can actually address with modest resources.

**3. The handle is far from the canopy - but it's the only control point that works.**

You can't grab an umbrella's canopy to steer it. The control point is the handle, which is nowhere near the working surface. This is actually the structural key to how umbrella governance works: you don't reach every rib individually. You tilt from one grip.

For your distributed printing problem, trying to control every chapter's output is reaching for the canopy. The handle is the source file. A chapter that has the right file in a format they can actually use will print something close to correct. A chapter that has nothing, or has a blurry JPEG from a 2009 email, will print whatever they have. The investment that tilts the whole system is a canonical asset source that is genuinely easy to use: a single Google Drive link, pinned in chapter communications, with clearly labeled files for every use case (print-ready EPS, screen RGB, embroidery vector, black-and-white). Not an asset management platform costing half your budget. Not a style guide no one will read. The single handle that chapters naturally reach for when they need to print something. You can build that this week for almost nothing, and it addresses the actual mechanism by which fragmentation reproduces.
