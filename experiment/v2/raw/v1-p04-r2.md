I monitor varroa mite loads in beehives using sticky boards — sheets of petroleum-jelly-coated paper slid under the hive's screened bottom. Mites that naturally dislodge fall onto the board. I count the drops over 24-72 hours and convert that to a natural-fall rate. But the number I obsess over isn't the aggregate — it's the *distribution* across the board's spatial zones. The zone directly below the brood cluster drops far more mites than the outer zones, and that structural imbalance tells me something the total count cannot: whether the infestation is concentrated in capped brood cells (where it's reproducing, invisible to most treatments) or dispersed across adult bees. Same aggregate. Completely different intervention.

A few things in your setup caught my eye.

---

**The aggregate lag number may be hiding a zoned signal.**

In my work, summing all zones into a single count collapses the diagnostic pattern that actually drives the treatment decision. Your 48 partitions are the zones of your topic. If you're monitoring total consumer-group lag as the primary signal, you may be making the same collapse. Partition-level lag is almost never uniform — hot key-hash buckets (high-volume merchants, repeat card patterns) concentrate on specific partitions while others coast. If a handful of partitions are running 10-15x hotter than the others, the problem isn't that you've hit the partition ceiling uniformly; it's that you've hit it in a locus. In my hive, a locus calls for a targeted intervention — not more of the same treatment spread thin. You might find it useful to pull per-partition lag and offset-commit rates and look at the distribution shape before concluding that no lever remains on the horizontal-scaling side.

---

**The drop rate is a proxy; the rate-of-change is the diagnostic.**

My board count isn't the thing I care about — it's an indirect sample of an underlying infestation dynamic I can't directly observe. What I actually act on is whether the rate is accelerating. A count of 50 drops/day that was 20 last week is a more urgent signal than 80 drops/day that was 100 last week, even though the latter is the larger number. Your current lag figure has the same structure: it's a proxy for a dynamic you care about — whether you'll breach the 60-second window. The rate of change of lag during the evening ramp-up, measured against the 60-second latency budget at current processing throughput, gives you the time-to-threshold directly rather than waiting for the raw lag to cross an arbitrary count. That's not just a monitoring improvement; it's a trigger that lets you act on the trajectory before the window is already compromised, rather than reacting to a number that already represents 20 minutes of exposure.

---

**The temporal form of the accumulation is a resource-timing lever.**

My board's daily drop pattern has a form: it accelerates when the queen's brood cycle peaks, plateaus, then falls. I use that form — not the level — to time treatments. Your lag curve has a form too: it has a characteristic ramp-up inflection point each evening, a peak, and an overnight drain. If that inflection is consistent to within, say, 15 minutes across evenings, the *form* of the time series is something you can pre-register against. Capacity staged before the inflection point (even modestly — partial pre-warm of a consumer pool, pre-parsed schema cache hot-loaded, priority queueing kicked in) responds to a pattern you already know rather than to lag you've already accumulated. This is also friendlier to your broker-spend constraint than sustaining elevated capacity around the clock: you're provisioning for a known 90-minute window, not a 24-hour posture. The finance conversation looks different when you can show that the window is bounded, predictable, and targeted.
