I'm a sea-vegetable forager who works salt-marsh tidal windows on the Atlantic coast - specifically the narrow salinity zones where glasswort and sea purslane grow. My lens is constraints-and-limits: the tide does not wait, the harvest window closes on its own schedule, and everything about how I work is shaped by that asymmetry.

Looking at your situation from inside that world, three things genuinely rhyme.

---

**The tidal window imposes a sequencing rule: hardest-to-recover beds first, convenient beds last.**

In the marsh, the forcing constraint isn't "find everything eventually" - it's that the window closes whether or not I'm done. Combined with an asymmetric loss structure (an over-harvested bed takes years to recover; a skipped bed costs me one tide cycle), this forces a hard sequencing rule before I put on boots: I hit the lowest-lying beds first, the ones exposed only at the bottom of the window and inaccessible any other time. The easily-reached beds near the shore I leave for last, or skip entirely on a short window - they'll be there next time.

Your storage pressure is the forcing constraint, but your loss structure is the same asymmetry: deleting the only good copy is irreversible; keeping a duplicate wastes a few megabytes. The tidal sequencing rule maps cleanly: the material on your external drives is your lowest-lying bed. Those drives have no redundancy guarantee - a bad head crash and they're gone with no recovery path - while anything already in Google Photos or iCloud has at least one other copy. Before you touch a single duplicate, before you worry about the storage meter, the move in my world would be: confirm that everything on those drives exists somewhere else, or get it there. That's your closed-window material. The duplicates are your shore-margin beds - they'll still be there after the urgent work is done.

---

**Species misidentification under time pressure is the characteristic failure mode, and the fix is a three-pile triage.**

The failure I fear most isn't moving slowly - it's moving fast through a dense bed and pulling the wrong species because I was confident and didn't stop to check. The misidentification is cheap to make and expensive afterward.

Your deduplication problem has the same structure. Moving through 80,000 files under storage pressure, the error to fear isn't keeping one extra duplicate; it's deleting the edited version thinking it's just a lower-quality copy of the original, or deleting the original because you saw the web-export first. The "species" - the file's identity - is ambiguous when two versions share the same subject but differ in resolution, edit state, or filename convention.

The field fix is a three-pile triage before any harvesting: confident-ID items (act quickly), uncertain-ID items (slow down or skip this pass), and clearly-unique items (protect first). Applied to your collection: a duplicate where you can see both copies, confirm they're identical, and confirm the one you're removing is lower quality - that's a confident-ID case, safe to move fast. A file where something seems off - the resolution is unexpectedly low, the edit state is unclear, the filename doesn't match the pattern - that goes in the uncertain pile for a separate, unhurried pass. The confident and uncertain piles should be physically separated before deletion starts, not mentally tracked during it.

---

**The salinity gradient partitions the marsh; you don't survey the whole marsh to find what you want.**

A salt marsh isn't homogeneous. It has natural zones - elevation, salinity, substrate - and an experienced forager uses those gradients to narrow the search before walking. I don't survey every square meter to find glasswort; I go to the zone where glasswort grows.

Your brother's wedding photos are a retrieval problem sitting inside a storage problem, and the two are worth separating. The collection has natural gradients already: storage location, date range, folder naming conventions, file density. Weddings generate burst shots - long runs of nearly-identical frames fired in rapid sequence - which are a distinctive density signature in a file listing sorted by date and time. The wedding has a known date (or a narrow range). That date narrows the zone. Within that zone, the burst-shot density narrows it further. In my world, the move would be to solve the retrieval problem entirely independently of the deduplication work, using the gradients rather than a full survey: pull every file from every store that falls in the date window, sort by timestamp, look for the burst-shot clusters. The full-archive reorganization doesn't need to be finished - or even started - before your mom gets the wedding set.
