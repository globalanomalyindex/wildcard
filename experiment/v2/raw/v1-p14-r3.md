I work on the harmonic analysis of tidal records — specifically the materials side: which gauge types and housing materials introduce systematic bias into constituent estimates, how a slowly settling pressure transducer contaminates your zero-reference datum without triggering any visible alarm, and how gaps in the observation record cause spectral leakage that distorts everything you compute from them. My obsession is that the constituent table you publish is only as good as the physical substrate that produced the raw record. With that in mind, three things in your situation genuinely caught my attention.

---

**1. Decompose before you attack**

In my work, tidal height at any location is the sum of roughly 37 independently predictable harmonic constituents — each with its own frequency, amplitude, and phase. The cardinal rule is: you never try to improve the total water-level prediction by working on the summed record directly. You decompose first. Each constituent responds to different forcing, fails for different reasons, and requires a different remedy. Working on the whole signal is working on none of the parts.

The 140 WCAG failures are a summed record. They were filed together, but they are not governed by the same constraints — some require legal review before a wording change can ship, others are pure CSS and can deploy today, others live in the shared header and cross-cut everything. The structural rhyme is that the velocity classes are as distinct as tidal constituents: one class is batched and serialized (legal gate, ~6 weeks/round-trip), the other is parallelizable and fast. If the 140 are treated as one body of work, the slow-path failures set the tempo for everything, and the court deadline quietly gets eaten. The move in harmonic analysis is explicit decomposition before any remediation strategy; you might find it worth running the same step here — partition the failures by governing constraint first, so the fast-path work can be running concurrently while the first batch is in legal review. Your call on whether that changes anything.

---

**2. The unowned datum**

The failure mode I fear most is datum drift: the instrument's zero reference shifts slowly — a corroded float, a pressure transducer settling into soft sediment — and all your constituent amplitudes are off. You don't discover it until you've published a tide table and a vessel runs aground. The insidious part is that the individual constituent computations look internally consistent; the contamination is upstream, in the substrate.

The shared header code that nobody owns has the same structural position in your portal: it is the reference substrate for every screen. If it carries unfixed focus-management or landmark failures, those propagate across all 60-plus screens regardless of what individual vendors do on their own pages. Closing individual screen failures while the datum drifts means your fix count rises but the user experience for screen reader users on the header remains contaminated — and the header ships with every deploy by every vendor. The tidal question I'd ask first is: who owns the datum calibration? Not as a procedural point, but because in my field, fixing constituent errors in individual records while leaving the datum unfixed produces beautifully computed answers that are still wrong. If a shared-header governance decision (even a lightweight one — one named owner, one review step per sprint) is achievable before the fast-path fixes start shipping, it might change what "done" looks like for a large fraction of the 140. Worth considering, or not — you know the organizational constraints better than I do.

---

**3. Gaps in the record produce leakage into dependent measurements**

When a tidal observation record has gaps — instrument downtime, storms, maintenance windows — energy from real constituents spreads into neighboring frequency bins. The result is that your estimates for constituents adjacent to the gap contaminate each other. The standard mitigation is to map dependent constituent pairs *before* you start computing, so you can flag which estimates will be degraded when the gap arrives.

The legal review cycle is a planned gap structure: a batch enters review, nothing emerges for six weeks. During that window, the wording fixes are suspended while the rest of the remediation continues. The leakage risk is any fix that has a cross-dependency on a wording change: if a form field's error state is inaccessible both because of its label text (legal gate, in the batch) and because of a broken `aria-describedby` reference (no legal gate, shippable now), fixing only the `aria-describedby` ships a partial remediation that might behave unpredictably with screen readers until the label lands. When the wording fix finally clears legal review and is applied to a codebase that has moved on, the integration may not be seamless. In harmonic analysis we solve this by pre-mapping dependent pairs before the gap, not after. Doing the same here — identifying which non-legal-gated fixes are tightly coupled to in-flight legal-gated changes before those batches are submitted — might save some rework when the batches clear. A small pre-flight check, not a process overhaul; entirely your call.
