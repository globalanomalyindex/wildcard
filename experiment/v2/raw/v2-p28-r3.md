Drawing on the dynamics of glacial landforms — specifically how a glacier deposits material not uniformly but according to where and when its energy changes — a few moves surface for your archive.

---

**1. Work the high-yield deposit zones first, not from the beginning.**

Glaciers don't scatter material evenly. High-energy zones erode and carry; low-energy transition zones are where sorted, coherent deposits form — the moraine that records where the ice slowed down. The geometry of what gets deposited is a map of energy gradient, not total volume.

Your 70,000 images are not uniform in licensing value. Some shows get called by marketing constantly; others have never been touched in five years. Right now you're either working from the oldest folder forward (uniform effort against non-uniform yield) or responding reactively. Instead: pull your licensing request history for the past three years — even a rough mental list — and rank shows by how often they generate requests. Take your three hours a week and do all metadata and rights work on the top five shows before touching anything else. A ranked triage list by show title, with a "phase 1 done / pending / never" status, takes an afternoon to build and makes every subsequent hour roughly three times more useful than working chronologically.

---

**2. Flag the rights ambiguity by contract-regime boundary, not image by image.**

Old glacial moraines get reworked by subsequent glaciers — the stratigraphy becomes ambiguous specifically at the older layers, not because of age per se, but because later events disturbed the record. Reading age from a moraine requires first finding the reworking events.

Your rights ambiguity almost certainly doesn't distribute evenly across fifteen years either. It clusters around regime changes: a union contract renegotiation, a transition between artistic directors who used different performer agreements, a year the company moved to a new standard contract. Rather than auditing each old shoot individually when a licensing request arrives — which is why you lost the sale — identify the 2-3 dates in your company's contract history that represent those regime shifts. Flag every shoot folder whose date falls inside an ambiguous window with a single RIGHTS-UNCONFIRMED label. Now the rights problem has a defined perimeter: a set of flagged folders you can work through with your legal contact or AD one show at a time, and a set of clean folders you can confirm immediately. That perimeter also stops the next licensing loss — you'll know within seconds whether a requested image sits in a clean zone or a flagged one.

---

**3. Run "esker passes" — one request category at a time, across the whole archive.**

An esker is a narrow ridge of sorted gravel that records a persistent channel of meltwater cutting through the glacier's bulk. The esker is coherent precisely because one kind of flow shaped it, sorting a single material type cleanly along one path through the chaos.

Marketing's requests are not actually random. There are maybe five recurring categories: costume/character shots, solo performer portraits, set-establishing wides, ensemble crowd scenes, production detail (props, lighting, set pieces). Spend thirty minutes with your marketing contact to inventory which five categories drive 90% of their requests. Then run your archive remediation as five sequential passes — one pass per category — rather than comprehensive show-by-show tagging. On the first pass, you open each show folder and tag only costume/character shots across all 70,000 images. At the end of that pass, every "red dress from Carmen" style request is answerable, even if nothing else is tagged. This is faster than comprehensive tagging because you're making one search decision per image instead of fifteen, and each pass delivers a complete capability rather than partial coverage everywhere.

---

These are three independent moves — you could run any one of them without the others. Happy to pull on whichever feels most tractable given your current setup, or to step back and leave you to it.
