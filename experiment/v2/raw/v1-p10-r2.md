I'm a physicist who works in polarimetry — the measurement of the polarization state of light. My particular obsession is what happens when you try to measure a signal and your detector is oriented orthogonally to the information you actually care about: you get a clean reading that tells you almost nothing. I work at the boundary between what a signal contains and what a given instrument can reveal.

Three things from my field genuinely rhyme with what you're describing.

---

**One. Your velocity metric is oriented orthogonally to the signal you need.**

In polarimetry, a standard intensity detector can receive a fully polarized beam and report a perfectly normal reading — no anomaly, nothing to flag — because polarization state is invisible to an instrument not tuned to that axis. The information is there, carried in the signal; the detector just cannot see it. This is not a weak signal problem. It is an orthogonality problem.

What you're describing has the same structure. Design-system consistency is a real, present, measurable attribute of every sprint's output — it was degrading continuously — but the instrument you're being measured by (feature velocity) is oriented at roughly 90 degrees to it. Sprint after sprint, the detector reported normal. Then the customer's review acted as a polarimeter: a different instrument, tuned to a different axis, and suddenly the accumulated drift was visible and quantified. The signal was always there. The instrument that could see it just wasn't in the loop until it was a customer contract problem.

The thing that transfers from my field: in polarimetry, you don't fix this by complaining about the signal. You add an aligned detector to the measurement setup. In your situation, the thing worth having in the room when the next sprint review happens might be a single orthogonal number — not instead of velocity, alongside it — that reads the axis velocity can't see. Something like: "components detached this sprint / total components touched" as a leading indicator of handoff drift. If that number goes into the sprint summary, it starts existing for leadership. It doesn't compete with velocity; it's orthogonal to it.

---

**Two. The divergence is being produced by the handoff surface, not by either artifact.**

In optics, polarization is not usually a property of the source — it is produced by the medium the light passes through. When light reflects off a surface or passes through certain materials, those interactions preferentially transmit one orientation and reject another. The beam arrives unpolarized; the surface selects. The divergence is a medium effect.

The structural match here: neither the Figma library nor the React package is the source of your drift. Each sprint, when a designer detaches a Figma instance because the variant they need doesn't exist in the React package, and when an engineer then rebuilds that screen from the available props — that handoff interaction is the medium. It selects for "shipped this sprint" and deflects "maintained canonical state." The two artifacts are each internally coherent; the polarization happens at the surface between them.

This framing is useful because it points to where an intervention actually belongs. In polarimetry, if a surface is producing unwanted polarization, you either change the surface interaction or you add a compensating element downstream that restores the original state. You don't try to change the source. In your situation: a thin compensating layer at the handoff — something that runs at merge time and flags when a component shipped in production doesn't have a Figma match, or when a Figma variant was detached and no corresponding React prop was created — targets the interaction where the divergence is actually generated, without requiring the Figma library or the React package to be rebuilt from scratch.

---

**Three. Any single metric gives you a projection of the state, not the full state — and you need a small spanning set.**

In polarimetry, a single intensity measurement gives you a projection of the polarization state onto one axis. To fully characterize the state, you need the Stokes vector: four orthogonal measurements that together span the full state space. No single measurement can substitute for the others, and any three-parameter summary loses information. The incompleteness is not about measurement precision; it's about the measurement basis being under-spanning.

You have one metric (velocity), which gives you one projection. Design-system health is at least three-dimensional: component-coverage parity (Figma vs. React, delta per sprint), behavioral-state coverage (focus, hover, error states per shipped component), and handoff fidelity (components shipped as-designed vs. rebuilt from scratch). These three are roughly orthogonal — a sprint can score well on all of them or badly on any one independently. A velocity metric that holds all three fixed is how six-figure contracts get flagged in review.

The thing that might be worth having is not a comprehensive design-audit process — you don't have headcount for that — but a three-number summary that takes maybe an hour to produce per sprint and that spans the state space your current measurement can't see. It doesn't need to be perfect; in polarimetry, even a partial Stokes measurement is dramatically more informative than intensity alone.
