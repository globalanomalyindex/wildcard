I engrave cylinder seals - those small stone rollers, usually chalcedony or carnelian, that stamp a continuous narrative frieze into wet clay when rolled. My medium is hard and resistant; I work in negative so my impressions read in positive; and the circumference I have to fill is fixed. What I've spent decades on is *frieze collapse*: the failure mode where two structurally similar scenes, placed adjacent on the register, bleed into each other in the impression. The viewer can no longer extract a discrete narrative unit. The frieze becomes noise.

Three things from my workshop genuinely rhyme with what you're describing.

---

**The frieze collapse diagnostic.** When I suspect two scenes are too similar, I don't re-examine every carved line on the stone - I roll it on clay and read the impression. The collapses become instantly visible: same protagonist, same action, same object, repeated. The eye cannot resolve them into separate units.

Your 460 posts almost certainly have zones of frieze collapse - clusters of posts with the same keyword-intent structure, placed so close in the index that crawlers cannot resolve them into discrete, authoritative units. The diagnostic instrument is not your CMS (that's the stone, invisible in use). It's Search Console's query-to-page mapping: pull every query that routes to more than one URL, and you will see your collapses directly in the impression. No developer time, no bulk CMS review - you're reading the rolled clay, not re-examining the negative. You might find it useful to start there before touching a single post.

---

**The fixed circumference.** My cylinder has a circumference I cannot extend. When a frieze is overcrowded, I cannot wish for a longer roller - I have to decide which scenes earn their register space and which are redundant. The discipline is ruthless consolidation: two scenes that share protagonist-action-object structure become one, and I reclaim the register for something genuinely different.

Your sign-off pipeline is a fixed circumference. One week per piece, one part-time writer - you cannot extend the roller. The question this reframes is not "how do I process more posts per month" but "which posts already occupy register space that another post makes redundant, and can I consolidate them into a single authoritative unit?" A consolidation that merges three cannibalizing posts costs one sign-off and removes three collisions. An engraver would solve the bandwidth problem by cutting the number of scenes that need to exist, not by trying to carve faster.

---

**The separator that earns its place.** In Ur III seals, the best engravers used zone separators - a stacked column of hatching, a standing figure - to tell the viewer's eye where one scene ends and the next begins. The discipline is that a separator only earns its position if the scenes on either side are genuinely differentiated. A separator between two nearly identical scenes is decoration; it does not resolve the collapse.

Your canonical tags and internal linking hierarchy are separators. Most blog archives place them decoratively - "this canonical exists" rather than "this canonical resolves a structural collision." An engraver would ask: does the post I'm pointing *to* have genuinely differentiated zone structure, or is it also just a slight variant of the posts pointing at it? If the consolidation target isn't itself well-differentiated, the canonical won't hold. That might be worth checking before you invest a sign-off cycle in pointing a cluster of posts at a canonical that is itself structurally weak.

---
