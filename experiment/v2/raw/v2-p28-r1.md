Drawing on coral micro-fragmentation research — specifically the practice of running reef restoration nurseries under strict throughput constraints — three moves surface for your archive.

---

**1. Triage by demand signal, not chronological order**

In a coral nursery with limited tank space and labor, you never start by propagating the oldest colony or working through inventory in sequence. You start with the genotypes that restoration sites are currently asking for — demand-first, not shelf-order. Your incoming marketing requests are already a live priority signal. Rather than starting your retroactive tagging effort at 2009 and working forward, keep a running list of every show or season that has generated a marketing request (including this Carmen inquiry). Those shows get indexed first, in the order asked. Within three hours a week, this means your backlog shrinks fastest exactly where it hurts most. Practically: add a shared request log (a simple spreadsheet row per request — show title, season year, what was asked, date received) and use that log as your processing queue.

---

**2. Stub records first, full descriptions later**

The key operational discovery in fragmentation work is that you do not need to complete a fragment's characterization at the moment you take it. You need to *initiate* its record — a minimal tag that gives it a hook — and the full characterization accumulates over time. For your archive, this means that "make a photo searchable" and "fully describe a photo" are two separate operations that do not need to happen in the same session. A two-field EXIF comment written in under a minute — show title and one visual keyword ("Carmen / red-dress") — transforms an invisible image into a findable one. That stub also unlocks a second-pass path: once stubs exist, AI vision tools (e.g. Google Vision, AWS Rekognition, or Adobe Lightroom's AI tagging) can run batch keyword enrichment on stubbed images without your hands-on involvement, filling in costume color, performer count, scene type, and staging descriptors. Without the stub, AI tools have nothing to attach results to. With it, your three hours a week funds the stubs, and automated enrichment does the heavier lifting on batches you've already touched.

---

**3. Build the rights ledger as a separate track, before the visual tagging**

In restoration work, a fragment without a clean chain of custody back to its source colony cannot be legally deployed at a restoration site — even if it is biologically healthy. The provenance record is not decoration; it is the deployment gate. Your archive has the same structure: a visually found image that can't be cleared isn't deliverable, and that's the failure that cost you the licensing sale. The move is to treat rights as a separate, parallel effort that runs independent of visual tagging. Before your next photo session, spend one hour building a one-row-per-production rights ledger: columns are show title, year, contract file location on disk, and clearance status (confirmed / unclear / no record on file). This can be done entirely from contract files and production records — no images need to be opened. Once the ledger exists, any future search result can return a clearance status in seconds rather than requiring a separate afternoon of contract archaeology. Start with your ten most-licensed show titles and work outward.
