I'm a quarry hand who splits natural stone using the feather-and-wedge method — you drill a line of holes along a rock face, seat a pair of steel feathers in each hole, then drive a wedge between them. The stone releases cleanly along its rift plane, the layer of least resistance baked into it by geology. The whole craft turns on one discipline: read the pre-existing grain before you place a single hole. Drive against it and the block runs wild, shattering cross-grain. Drive with it and a modest force produces a clean face.

I looked at your problem through that lens — and I found three things that genuinely rhyme.

---

**1. The rift is already in the material — you're not imposing a line, you're reading one**

In the quarry, stable structure and fragile structure coexist in every block. The rift planes are set; I only choose where to align my intervention. Your content works the same way. The terms that cause customer confusion — loyalty program names, feature labels, navigational elements — are a small, high-stakes, low-churn stratum. Marketing prose, seasonal offers, and promotional copy are a different stratum entirely: volatile, low-stakes per word, and fine to vary. Right now everything gets routed the same way and lands with whichever freelancer picks it up. That's treating homogenous stone when there's a rift already in the material.

The quarry move: differentiate the stratum before anything else. A small locked layer — product names, UI labels, the loyalty program name — treated as non-negotiable across all translators for a language, regardless of who picks up the job. The volatile layer floats freely beneath it. This costs nothing, requires no extra review round, and targets exactly the failure mode your customers in Japan are hitting.

---

**2. No single wedge splits the stone — the pre-stress has to be distributed across all nodes before anyone drives hard**

The feather-and-wedge system works because each node is loaded together, in sequence, before the stone moves. If I drive one node too hard while the others are still loose, the fracture propagates cross-grain. In your setup, each freelancer per language is an independent node — and right now there is no shared pre-load. One translator commits to one German name for the loyalty program before the others have been given any constraint to work within. The fracture runs wild because the nodes were never loaded together.

The structural fix is the pre-load, not a heavier blow: a per-language locked term sheet — not a full style guide, just the eight or twelve terms that absolutely cannot drift — that every translator for that language receives and commits to before they pick up any job. Budget-neutral, turnaround-neutral, and it changes the force geometry: instead of each freelancer striking independently, they're all seated in the same feathers before the first blow lands.

---

**3. "Running wild" is a diagnostic you can run before a fracture propagates**

My worst failure is when a cross-grain fracture has already moved before I notice it. After that, the block is lost. The tell is detectable earlier: I check whether any node was loaded out of sequence — whether one wedge ran ahead of the others before the system was pre-stressed.

Your version of "running wild" is a term appearing in a customer-facing surface — an app UI, a help article, a booking confirmation — before it's been locked across all translators for that language. Once a customer has seen the wrong name, the correction is harder than the prevention. The diagnostic question is: has this term reached a customer-facing surface before a single canonical form was committed to? If yes, the fracture is already moving. You can wire that check into your routing, independent of turnaround or budget — it's a question asked at job assignment, not a new review round added after.
