Drawing on coral reef ecology, specifically the temporal dynamics of how reefs get colonized and what determines which zones stay productive...

---

**Seed 1: The slot assignment is a recruitment decision made at the worst possible moment**

In a reef, coral larvae settle in brief pulses - and *when* the substrate is ready relative to *when* larvae arrive determines everything. If the substrate is fouled at the moment the pulse hits, larvae settle badly or not at all, and the spatial damage persists for a season because established organisms are hard to displace.

Your Monday/Thursday receiving clusters are exactly that pulse. Inventory arrives faster than slotting decisions can be made well, so pallets "settle" wherever substrate is free - random slotting under pressure - and that assignment locks in. The problem is not that you're slotting wrong in principle; it's that the decision is being made at the moment of worst information and highest congestion.

If your suppliers send ASNs (advance ship notices) 24-48 hours before the truck, you can run velocity lookups and assign target slots the day before the appointment - so put-away crew is executing pre-decided moves, not making slotting calls on a jammed dock floor. Even partial coverage (the top 30% of volume) takes most of the damage out. Concrete setup: build a one-page pre-slotting sheet per PO that goes out to the put-away team the morning of the appointment; any SKU not on the sheet gets a temporary holding row near the dock rather than a permanent slot, and gets properly slotted in the next quiet window (Tuesday, Friday morning).

---

**Seed 2: Re-slot by frequency times distance, not by frequency alone - and cap the scope at what you can finish**

In a damaged reef, not all degraded zones are worth intervention. Ecologists managing recovery before a known storm season focus effort on *high-value substrate in degraded state* - the patches where the productivity potential is highest and the current state is worst. Trying to restore the whole reef before storm season is a way to restore none of it.

The warehouse version: rank every SKU by daily pick frequency multiplied by current slot distance from pack stations. The top of that ranked list is your damaged high-value substrate - items picked constantly from the far end of the building. Re-slotting "fast movers" by frequency alone often produces a shorter list but misses the worst travel offenders; the distance multiplier finds the real pick-rate killers. With 8 weeks and two supervisors, you probably have capacity to physically re-slot 15-20% of SKUs - which is enough if you're working the right 15-20%. Run the calculation (pick frequency × average slot distance to pack, last 30 days), sort descending, draw the line where your re-slot capacity runs out, and stop there. The bottom 80% stays put; disrupting it costs more than it saves.

---

**Seed 3: The dock-to-floor leak is a zone-boundary problem that needs a formal buffer, not an ad-hoc clearing rule**

In a reef, animals that repeatedly cross zone boundaries (foraging at the productive crest, sheltering in the lagoon) pay a metabolic transit cost. When habitat fragmentation forces all foragers into high-transit patterns, system-level yield drops even if each individual zone is healthy. The fix is maintaining zone integrity - distinct, contiguous habitat types with clear boundaries.

Your Mon/Thu pallets bleeding into pick aisles are zone fragmentation: receiving and picking are meant to be spatially distinct, but the clustering punches through the boundary and the interdigitation forces pickers into detour paths. "Clear the aisles" as a rule fails because under dock pressure there's nowhere to put the overflow. What holds is a *formal buffer zone* - 2-3 designated staging rows near the dock that are always reserved for incoming pallets on receiving days, sized to absorb a full Monday truck without reaching the pick floor. The zone has one rule: pallets enter the buffer, not the pick aisles; put-away crew works the buffer to zero before each pick shift, not reactively. With six temps arriving before peak, two of them can own buffer-to-slot exclusively on receiving days, which keeps the zone boundary intact without adding supervisor load.
