A professor of glacial geomorphology — someone whose career is reading landscapes as energy records, reconstructing what ice did from what it left behind — looked at your archive problem and found three places where the structure genuinely rhymes.

**Seed 1: Deposition-time sorting**

In my field: when a glacier deposits material, it sorts by energy. Coarse boulders drop where transport energy first fails; fine silt drifts further and settles last. A terminal moraine — the clearest, most dateable feature in the whole landscape — forms right at the threshold moment when the ice could no longer carry its load. Retroactive sorting of a glacial deposit is enormously expensive; the sorting that happened at deposition cost almost nothing, because the energy context was live.

The mapping: in your archive, you are at maximum information-energy at the moment of the shoot — you know which frame is the climax scene, which performer is the lead, which costume will matter to marketing. That context decays fast. Three days post-shoot it takes memory work; three years post-shoot it may be gone entirely. The cost of tagging a landmark shot at acquisition is close to zero. The cost of recovering that same tag retroactively across 70,000 images is what you are currently paying.

You might find it useful to ask: what is the minimum viable deposition-time tag — the one note written at the shoot or within 48 hours — that would convert a future afternoon-search into a five-second query? In my world the terminal moraine is worth more than all the undifferentiated till behind it, and it cost no extra energy to form — it just formed *at the right moment*.

**Seed 2: Erratic boulders as sparse high-signal indicators**

In my field: you do not need to core every cubic meter of sediment to reconstruct a glaciation. You find the erratic — the anomalous granite boulder that could only have traveled from one source outcrop, 200 kilometers north — and from that single object you read the entire flow path. Sparse indicator objects carry disproportionate information about vast surrounding deposits.

The mapping: your clearance problem has the same structure. You do not need to examine 70,000 images to know the rights status of the archive. The indicator objects are the production contracts — one contract set per show, covering a date range that maps to hundreds or thousands of images. Read the contract, and you have characterized the clearance state for all the images deposited during that production. The 70,000 images are not 70,000 independent rights decisions; they are a relatively small number of contract events plus a large undifferentiated deposit.

The lost licensing sale happened because the contract-layer search was coupled to the image-layer search. A glaciologist would separate those: build the erratic index first (one entry per production: show, date range, contract status, known ambiguities), and any clearance query resolves at that level without touching the image corpus at all. You might find it useful to ask whether your three hours a week could be pointed at building that index rather than the image metadata — it may be the higher-leverage deposit to read.

---

**Seed 3: One deposit, two independent signal types**

In my field: the same glacial landscape encodes multiple distinct signals that require completely different reading instruments. Striations on bedrock tell you direction of flow. Erratic size and sorting tell you transport energy. Moraine height tells you where the front stalled. These are not redundant — they answer different questions, and confusing the instruments wastes field time.

The mapping: your archive encodes two genuinely distinct signal types — visual/semantic content (what is in the frame: a red dress, a particular performer, a specific scene) and compliance/clearance state (can this image be licensed). These require different instruments and a different query path. At the moment, both queries collapse into the same manual image search, which is why a clearance question takes as long as a visual search. Separating the layers — a contract index for clearance, image-level tagging for content — would let each query hit the right instrument directly.

You might find it useful to ask which of the two instruments is more urgent to build first. In my experience, the answer depends on which question is being asked more often and with more consequence — which for you, given the lost licensing sale, sounds like it may be the clearance layer rather than the visual search layer.
