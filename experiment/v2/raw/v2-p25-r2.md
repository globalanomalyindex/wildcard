Drawing on the concept of **mirror** — specifically its failure modes...

A mirror is exact at the moment it's made, but has no mechanism to track movement in the source. The divergence between copy and original accumulates invisibly, with no internal error state, no flag, no warning — until someone walks into the room and finds the reflection no longer matches.

Three things surfaced from looking at your situation through that lens:

---

**1. The PR template checkbox**

In optics, a mirror's most important failure mode isn't the decay — it's that the mirror never knows it's failed. It keeps faithfully showing *a* room even when the actual room has changed. The only fix is an event signal at the *source*, not at the mirror.

Right now your three documentation stores (help center, README, Confluence) receive no signal when the source changes. Engineers ship; docs drift; the signal arrives at support weeks later, after a customer breaks against it. The cheapest source-side hook available to you, without needing process authority over engineers, is a single required checkbox in the PR template: "Does this change any user-facing behavior that is documented in the help center, README, or Confluence?" A yes triggers a Slack message or a Jira ticket assigned to you — not for approval, just visibility. You'd catch the auth-guide class of problem at merge time rather than post-lockout.

*The concrete move: add one checkbox to the PR template, routed to a doc-review channel, this sprint.*

---

**2. Ghost images: zone your three stores and kill duplicates**

When a mirror has a degraded or partially-silvered backing, two competing images appear simultaneously — one from the front surface, one from the back — and neither is flagged as authoritative. Your help center, README, and Confluence already have this problem: overlapping content with no declared canonical source, and archived help center pages ranking above live ones in search results. Customers are navigating a ghost-image system.

The fix isn't a cleanup pass — it's a zoning decision. Assign each store an exclusive scope that the others don't touch: for example, the help center owns all customer-facing procedural content (including auth); the README owns installation and local-dev setup only; Confluence owns internal process, never customer-facing content. Once zones exist, anything outside them is not "outdated" — it's structurally misplaced and can be archived or redirected without editorial judgment. The search ranking problem becomes mechanical: archived pages get a 301 redirect to the live canonical, and the search surface collapses to one image per topic.

*The concrete move: write a one-page zoning policy (three stores, each with a declared scope and an "out of scope → archive or redirect" rule), circulate it for engineering sign-off, and apply it to the auth section first as the highest-risk zone.*

---

**3. Decay starts at the edges — triage by age weighted by traffic**

Silvering decay in mirrors begins at the perimeter and moves inward invisibly. The surface still looks fine. The degradation reaches the critical load-bearing areas of the reflection last, but by then it's been progressing undetected for a long time. The auth guide that locked out a customer was almost certainly not a recent article — it was old content, at the edge of your attention, still receiving search traffic.

The triage heuristic that follows: articles that are both old (last edited more than 6 months ago) and still actively visited are your highest-decay-risk zone. They've drifted the most and are still being relied on. Most help center platforms expose last-edited date and page views; a spreadsheet sort on those two columns takes about an hour and gives you a prioritized queue. Each item gets one verdict: validate as current, or archive. Not rewrite — just validate or archive. That keeps the pass fast enough to be quarterly.

*The concrete move: export last-edited date and monthly page views for all help center articles, sort by (age descending, traffic descending), and issue a validate-or-archive verdict on the top 20 results in the next two weeks.*

---
