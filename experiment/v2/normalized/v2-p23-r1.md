## angles
- Posts with large peak-to-current traffic drops (not just current low traffic) have inbound links and indexed history, indicating something to recover rather than starting from scratch -> pages that ranked and then degraded are structurally different from pages that never ranked, suggesting different triage strategies -> sort Search Console data by absolute drop in impressions (peak vs. 90-day window) and prioritize pages 2+ years old with large drops; these are consolidation candidates where effort pays off disproportionately
- Keyword cannibalization is a structural decision problem (which URL wins) that doesn't require multiple weeks of serial approval per post -> decisions unlock fixes faster than revised copy does, and batching reduces queue time from serial weeks to a single sign-off event -> compile 15–20 clear cannibalization pairs with keep/consolidate/redirect recommendations and Search Console data into one approval request to product marketing
- Posts that lost traffic after algorithm updates may have lost rankings because the SERP format changed, not because the content is stale -> a tutorial that worked on a SERP that valued step-by-step may not recover even with UI refreshes on a SERP that now favors comparison tables or feature overviews -> for high-traffic-loss posts, check the dominant content format in top SERP positions and compare to your own post's format; format mismatches signal whether revision is the right approach or consolidation/redirect is needed instead
- Posts in positions 6–15 with 500+ monthly impressions are most likely to reach page 1 in a Q4 revision cycle, versus top-5 posts (already there) or tail posts (won't move meaningfully) -> targeting a defensible, achievable set of posts makes the case to leadership and focuses effort where impact is realistic -> filter Search Console for average position 6–15 with 500+ monthly impressions to get a 20–40 post list of realistic Q4 targets
