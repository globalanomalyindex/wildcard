## angles
- Tidal windows impose a sequencing rule based on asymmetric loss: the window closes whether or not you're done, and some beds recover slowly while others don't. -> Storage pressure creates the same asymmetry: deleting the only copy is irreversible while keeping duplicates wastes space. -> Confirm everything on external drives exists elsewhere first (they lack redundancy guarantees), then handle the duplicates—the external drives are your closed-window material that must be secured before deduplication work.
- Species misidentification under time pressure is the characteristic failure in foraging: you move fast through dense areas and pull the wrong thing because you were confident and didn't verify. -> Deduplication under storage pressure has the same structure: you might delete the edited version thinking it's just a lower-quality copy, or delete the original because you saw the web-export first. -> Use a three-pile triage before deletion: confident-ID cases (both copies visible and confirmed identical, safe to remove the lower-quality one), uncertain cases (unclear resolution, edit state, or filename—separate pass later), and clearly-unique items (protect first).
- Salt marshes have natural gradients that let experienced foragers narrow their search without surveying every square meter. -> Your collection has natural gradients too: storage location, date range, folder naming, and file density (weddings generate burst-shot sequences). -> Solve the wedding retrieval problem independently of deduplication: use the known wedding date and burst-shot density patterns to pull every file from every store in that window, sorted by timestamp, without waiting for the full-archive reorganization to finish.
