## angles
- Deposition-time sorting: When a glacier deposits material, sorting by energy happens at the moment of deposit; retroactive sorting is expensive. In the archive problem, tagging at acquisition captures maximum information-energy; three days post-shoot requires memory work, three years is costly to recover. Asking what minimum viable deposition-time tag — a single note written at shoot or within 48 hours — would convert a future afternoon-search into a five-second query.
- Erratic boulders as sparse high-signal indicators: A glacial erratic — an anomalous boulder from a distant source — reveals the entire flow path without coring every cubic meter. For the archive: clearance is not 70,000 independent rights decisions but a small number of production contracts plus a large undifferentiated deposit. Building a contract-layer index (one entry per show with date range and status) rather than image-level search is higher-leverage.
- Two independent signal types in one deposit: The same glacial landscape encodes multiple distinct signals requiring different instruments — striations show flow direction, erratics show transport energy, moraine height shows stalling point. The archive encodes two distinct signals — visual/semantic content and compliance/clearance state — that currently collapse into the same manual image search. Separating layers with a contract index for clearance and image-level tagging for content would let each query hit the right instrument directly.
